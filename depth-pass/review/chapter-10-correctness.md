## Flags for Human Review

| Location | Claim | Uncertainty |
|----------|-------|-------------|
| Para 1 | "the women's prison at Breslau" | Sources consistently describe Luxemburg's Breslau confinement as "the town prison in Breslau," not a specifically designated women's prison. She was moved between several facilities (Barnimstrasse, Wronki fortress, then Breslau). Verify whether the Breslau facility had a formal women's section or was a general town prison. |
| Para 6 | "Lenin read it and suppressed it" | The manuscript was published posthumously in 1922 by Paul Levi. Whether Lenin actively suppressed it is contested: one account says Clara Zetkin was instructed to burn it (attributed to Lenin or to Leo Jogiches depending on the source); another holds that Jogiches withheld publication to avoid alienating the Bolsheviks. The clean "Lenin suppressed it" framing is disputed in scholarship. |
| Para 9 (debate section) | "Luxemburg's response, published the same year" | *What Is To Be Done?* was published in 1902. Luxemburg's essay "Organizational Questions of the Russian Social Democracy" was published in 1904 — the same year as Lenin's *One Step Forward, Two Steps Back*, which was the immediate target of her response, not WITBD. The text frames this as a single 1904 exchange but attributes Lenin's position to WITBD (1902), conflating two different works two years apart. "Published the same year" is accurate for the 1904 exchange but the framing is ambiguous. |
| Para before final section | "The paramilitary that killed her included officers who would later form the core of the Nazi SA" | The unit responsible (GKSD Freikorps) fed into Nazism broadly — dozens of members became senior SS officers and the Freikorps generally were a recruiting ground for the SA. However, "form the core of the Nazi SA" overstates the specific connection to this particular unit. Waldemar Pabst, who ordered the killing, collaborated with the Nazis later but was not an SA founder. The claim is directionally accurate but imprecise. |
| Para before final section | "They found her four months later" | Body recovered May 31, 1919; murdered January 15, 1919 — approximately four and a half months, not four months. Minor but worth noting. |

## Checked and Clear

- **Rosa Luxemburg's age**: Born March 5, 1871; 47 years old in summer 1918. Correct.
- **The essay title**: *The Russian Revolution* — correct.
- **Year the essay was written**: 1918, in Breslau prison. Correct.
- **Publication date of posthumous essay**: Published 1922 by Paul Levi. Consistent with text's implication.
- **Lenin's exile in London**: Lenin was in London in 1902 when WITBD was published (arrived April 1902). Correct.
- **Lenin's *What Is To Be Done?* — core argument**: Trade union consciousness argument and vanguard party concept are accurately attributed to this text. Correct.
- **Kronstadt, March 1921**: Rebellion began March 1, 1921. Correct.
- **Kronstadt demands**: Free soviets, freedom of speech, end to forced grain requisition — matches historical record. Correct.
- **Trotsky commanded the assault on Kronstadt**: Trotsky organized the military response and led the assault with Tukhachevsky. Correct.
- **Franco's coup, July 1936**: Coup launched July 17–18, 1936. Correct.
- **CNT membership "over a million"**: CNT claimed 1.6 million members in 1936. Correct.
- **Barcelona trams collectivized, workers' control**: Confirmed — CNT workers took over tramways; 6,500 of 7,000 tram workers were CNT members. Correct.
- **Spanish Communist Party as Comintern affiliate directed by Moscow**: Correct.
- **George Orwell's *Homage to Catalonia***: Title correct; account of witnessing the revolutionary collectives being dismantled is confirmed. Correct.
- **Alexandra Kollontai silenced, sent abroad**: Kollontai led the Workers' Opposition; was removed from party positions and sent to diplomatic posts abroad (Norway, Mexico, Sweden) from 1922. Correct.
- **Luxemburg shot January 1919**: January 15, 1919. Correct.
- **Body thrown into Landwehr Canal**: Confirmed. Correct.
- **Luxemburg quote ("Freedom only for supporters...")**: Verified exact text from *The Russian Revolution*. Correct.
- **Sylvia Wynter "overrepresented Man"**: Concept confirmed as Wynter's. Correct.
- **Gramsci "hegemony"**: Correctly attributed. Correct.
- **Cooperation Jackson, Jackson Mississippi**: Organization confirmed; founded 2014, builds worker cooperatives, focuses on marginalized communities. Correct.
- **Zapatistas refusing state power**: Confirmed — EZLN explicitly structured governance to prevent military commanders from holding political office. Correct.

## Notes

The chapter's framework claims (vanguardism vs. distributed self-governance, the soviets being captured by the party, the means-are-the-ends argument) are analytical positions, not fact-checkable claims, and were left untouched per mandate.

The most substantively uncertain claim is the Lenin suppression framing. If the argument is that the manuscript was withheld from publication — which is undisputed — the text works. If the claim is that Lenin personally ordered its destruction, that is contested and should be softened or verified before publication.

The "women's prison" detail is the second most uncertain — if this characterization is wrong, it creates a minor but unnecessary inaccuracy in an otherwise precisely rendered opening scene.
