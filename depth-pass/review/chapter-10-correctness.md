## Flags for Human Review

| Location | Claim | Uncertainty |
|----------|-------|-------------|
| Para 1 | "the women's prison at Breslau" | Sources consistently describe this as "the town prison in Breslau." Luxemburg moved between several facilities (Barnimstrasse, Wronki fortress, Breslau). Whether the Breslau facility had a formal women's section or was a general town prison is unverified. |
| Para 6 | "Lenin read it and suppressed it" | The manuscript was published posthumously in 1922 by Paul Levi. Whether Lenin personally ordered suppression is contested: one account attributes the instruction to burn it to Lenin (via Zetkin), another to Leo Jogiches, who may have withheld publication to avoid alienating the Bolsheviks. The clean causal framing "Lenin suppressed it" is disputed in scholarship. |
| Debate section, para 2 | "Luxemburg's response, published the same year" | *What Is To Be Done?* was published in 1902. Luxemburg's "Organizational Questions of the Russian Social Democracy" was published in 1904 — the same year as Lenin's *One Step Forward, Two Steps Back*, which was her essay's actual immediate target, not WITBD. "Published the same year" is accurate for the 1904 exchange but the text attributes Lenin's position to WITBD, conflating two distinct works two years apart. |
| Para before closing section | "form the core of the Nazi SA" | GKSD Freikorps members went on to senior SS and Nazi roles broadly, and the Freikorps generally fed into the SA, but "form the core of the Nazi SA" overstates the documented connection to this specific unit. Waldemar Pabst (who ordered the killing) later collaborated with the Nazis but was not an SA founder. |
| Para before closing section | "They found her four months later" | Body recovered May 31, 1919; murdered January 15, 1919 — approximately four and a half months. "Four months" is slightly short. |

## Notes

The chapter's structural claims — vanguardism vs. distributed self-governance, the soviets captured by the party, the means-are-the-ends argument — are analytical positions, not fact-checkable claims, and were left untouched.

The most substantively uncertain claim is the Lenin suppression framing. The underlying fact (the manuscript was withheld from publication during Lenin's lifetime) is undisputed. Whether Lenin personally ordered this is what is contested. If the text intends only the former, no change is needed; if it intends the latter, the claim should be softened.

The "women's prison" characterization is a secondary concern — if incorrect, it creates a minor but unnecessary inaccuracy in an otherwise precisely rendered opening scene.

The "form the core of the Nazi SA" is the claim most likely to require revision; the SS connection is better documented than the SA connection for the specific GKSD unit.
