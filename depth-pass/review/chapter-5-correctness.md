## Checked and Clear

- **Rosa Luxemburg's birthplace:** Zamość, Russian-occupied Poland — confirmed.
- **Family description:** youngest of five children of a Jewish timber merchant — confirmed. Father Elias Luxemburg was a timber merchant; Rosa was the fifth and youngest child.
- **The Accumulation of Capital (1913):** Publication year confirmed correct.
- **German Social Democratic Party:** Described as "the largest socialist party in the world" — accurate for the period.
- **Death by government-backed paramilitaries, January 1919:** Confirmed. She was killed by members of the Guards Cavalry Rifle Division (Freikorps-aligned) on January 15, 1919.
- **Body thrown into the Landwehr Canal:** Confirmed.
- **Alexandra Kollontai grew up in Petersburg:** Confirmed. Born in St. Petersburg, 1872.
- **Kollontai's father described as a Tsarist general:** Confirmed. Father was General Mikhail Alekseyevich Domontovich of the Imperial Russian Army.
- **"Only woman in Lenin's first cabinet":** Confirmed. Kollontai served as People's Commissar for Social Welfare in Lenin's first Soviet government in 1917–1918; she was the only woman.
- **Kollontai died in Moscow in 1952:** Confirmed. She died March 9, 1952.
- **Stalin sending Kollontai to Oslo as ambassador:** Broadly accurate. Stalin's government appointed her to diplomatic posts starting in 1922–23 (Norway/Oslo was her first posting). The framing captures the political dynamic accurately, though she later served in Mexico and Sweden as well.
- **Oxfam 2020 unpaid care work figure ($10.8 trillion, three times global tech industry):** Confirmed. The Oxfam "Time to Care" report (January 2020) stated unpaid care work is worth $10.8 trillion annually, described as more than three times the size of the tech industry.
- **Kollontai leaving her son with her mother:** Confirmed in biographical sources.

## Notes

The opening photograph claim — "a photograph, taken in a Frankfurt courtroom" — is an evocative framing device. Worth noting that the 1914 trial was indeed at the Frankfurt Second Criminal Court for Luxemburg's September 1913 Bockenheim speech. The detail is precise and verifiable once corrected.

The paper marriage clause was removed from the Zurich sentence during correction because its placement implied the marriage happened at age 18 before university, when it in fact happened in 1898 (age 27) upon leaving Zurich.
