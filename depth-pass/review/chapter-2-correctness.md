## Checked and Clear
- **Atlantic City neighborhood existence and character**: Confirmed. Working-class, racially mixed, ~1,000 households. Consistent across Encyclopedia Virginia, Colley Avenue Historical Geography, Nonsite.org scholarly article.
- **"historian Forrest White"**: Confirmed. Full name Forrest R. "Hap" White, author of *Pride and Prejudice: School Desegregation and Urban Renewal in Norfolk, 1950–1959* (1992). Attribution of the "manufactured slum" technique to White's analysis is consistent with his documented argument.
- **1957 demolition approval**: Confirmed across multiple sources.
- **1951–1973 urban renewal dates**: Confirmed. Norfolk broke ground on Project One in late 1951; federal urban renewal program formally ended 1973.
- **20,000 displaced / "almost a tenth of the city's population"**: Confirmed verbatim by multiple scholarly sources including the Nonsite.org article and Encyclopedia Virginia.
- **Every racially integrated neighborhood targeted**: Confirmed by multiple sources as White's documented conclusion.
- **James Baldwin / "Negro removal"**: The attribution is accurate. Baldwin used this formulation publicly, including in a 1963 documentary filmed in San Francisco. The chapter uses it as a paraphrase ("called it what it was: Negro removal"), not a direct quote, which is appropriate.
- **Brown v. Board decision in 1954**: Confirmed.
- **Norfolk school closures in 1958**: Six schools closed September 27, 1958. Confirmed. (See flag above on scope of "public schools.")
- **Tidewater Gardens / 2021 / federal Fair Housing lawsuit**: Confirmed. Lawsuit filed April 2021; demolition began December 2020 and was active throughout 2021. "In the midst of" is accurate for 2021.

## Notes
- The chapter's argument that urban renewal was used to destroy integrated neighborhoods to preserve de facto segregation following *Brown v. Board* is well-supported by Forrest White's documented scholarship and corroborated by multiple independent sources.
- The James Baldwin "Negro removal" attribution is paraphrase, not quotation. The choice not to use quotation marks is correct given the phrasing.
- No invented thinkers, fabricated statistics, or unsourced claims were found. The chapter's analytical framing (capture, enclosure, legitimacy) is presented as framework, not historical claim, and is not subject to factual verification.
- The four flags above are genuine uncertainties — two geographic/numeric (verifiable with primary sources), one institutional (a framing question for the author), and one a case where the text overstates slightly in a way that is rhetorically useful but factually imprecise.
