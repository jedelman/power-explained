## Checked and Clear
- **DeGraffenreid v. General Motors (1976)**: Case year confirmed. The plaintiffs were Black women; the court refused to recognize their combined race/sex discrimination claim; the reasoning (Black men employed, white women employed, therefore no discrimination) confirmed accurate. Last-hired, first-fired seniority policy confirmed.
- **Crenshaw's 1989 paper**: Confirmed. "Demarginalizing the Intersection of Race and Sex: A Black Feminist Critique of Antidiscrimination Doctrine, Feminist Theory and Antiracist Politics," University of Chicago Legal Forum (1989). "Thirteen years later" after the 1976 case is arithmetically correct.
- **Crenshaw biographical**: Grew up in Canton, Ohio — confirmed. Cornell undergraduate — confirmed. Harvard Law School — confirmed (J.D. 1984). Description of arriving "at the intersection constantly" in her legal education — consistent with her own accounts.
- **Sylvia Wynter biographical**: Born in Cuba in 1928 — confirmed (Holguín, Cuba, May 11, 1928). Grew up in Jamaica — confirmed (family returned when she was ~2). Studied in London on a scholarship — confirmed (Jamaica Centenary Scholarship, King's College London). Novelist and playwright — confirmed. Work in the 1950s–60s writing Caribbean into literary existence — confirmed.
- **Wynter's concept of "overrepresentation"**: Attribution to Wynter confirmed. The argument that "Man" as European humanist figure became the overrepresented definition of the Human is consistent with her published scholarship.
- **Angela Davis biographical**: Born Birmingham, Alabama, 1944 — confirmed (January 26, 1944). Dynamite Hill — confirmed; named for KKK bombings of Black families who moved there. Brandeis scholarship — confirmed. Studied in Frankfurt with Adorno and Marcuse — confirmed; she studied at the Institut für Sozialforschung (Institute for Social Research) in Frankfurt with both. Fired by Ronald Reagan's Board of Regents — confirmed; Reagan (then California Governor) pressed the Regents; she was fired twice. Charged with murder, acquitted after international campaign — confirmed; acquitted June 4, 1972.
- **2.3 million incarcerated by 2008**: Confirmed. Prison and jail population peaked at 2.3 million in 2008; U.S. held the highest incarceration rate in the world.
- **Ruth Wilson Gilmore**: "Abolition geography" confirmed as Gilmore's framework and the title of her 2022 essay collection (Verso).
- **Mbembe named it necropolitics**: Accurate framing. Mbembe's necropolitics concept is documented as engaging with Foucault and the Black radical tradition, which includes Davis. The claim that "Mbembe was reading Davis, not only Foucault" is plausible given Mbembe's documented engagement with the Black radical tradition, though it is an authorial interpretive claim rather than a directly verifiable biographical fact.

## Notes
- The correction to the DeGraffenreid hiring date is the most consequential factual fix — the 1964 date likely crept in from the year the Civil Rights Act passed (which is what made discrimination claims possible), not from the actual hiring record. The corrected text specifies "before 1970 for assembly line work," which accurately reflects the court record.
- The Davis age correction is a clean arithmetic error with no ambiguity.
- The 200,000 incarceration figure warrants author attention — it's defensible as a state/federal prison figure but could mislead if readers understand "incarcerated" as the total correctional population.
- No fabricated claims, invented quotes, or hallucinated citations detected.
