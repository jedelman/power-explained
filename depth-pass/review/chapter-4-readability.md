## Changes Made

| Para 4 (Arizmendi students) | "...200 kilometers away — working fifty-five-hour weeks by day, studying at night." | "...200 kilometers away — they worked fifty-five-hour weeks by day and studied at night." | The original participial phrase ("working fifty-five-hour weeks by day, studying at night") attaches ambiguously to Arizmendi rather than to his students. The preceding clause has Arizmendi as subject ("he selected," "arranged for them"), so the dangling participle pulls the action back to him. A real reader pauses: was Arizmendi the one working 55-hour weeks? Switching to an explicit subject ("they worked") removes the ambiguity without changing meaning or rhythm. |

## Notes

This is one of the cleaner chapters in terms of readability. The prose does what it intends: builds through accumulation, lets facts land without commentary, and ends with the author's own stake in the argument. The voice is consistent throughout.

The only genuine reader-stumble was the pronoun ambiguity in the Arizmendi section. Everything else is intentional style.

One observation for the author: the Jackson, Mississippi statistics ("median household income $33,000, poverty rate 34% among Black residents") were not searched in the correctness pass. Unanchored poverty statistics become outdated quickly and would benefit from verification against a dated source. This is a correctness note, not a readability note.

The personal section (Norfolk restaurant) reads as a controlled break in register — honest and unguarded in a way that is structurally earned after five case studies. The sentence "It cost me my idealism and my faith in humanity" is the emotional peak and the most vulnerable line in the chapter. What follows earns it back without undoing it. That balance is working.
