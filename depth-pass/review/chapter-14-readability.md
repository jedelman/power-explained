# Readability Pass: Chapter 14 — "I Became the Land"

## Changes Made

| Location | Original | Revised | Reason |
|----------|----------|---------|--------|
| Para 5 (Burlington/CHT intro) | "the trust Bernie Sanders had helped seed in 1984, met in Chapter 4" | "the trust Bernie Sanders had helped seed in 1984, introduced in Chapter 4" | "Met in Chapter 4" is an unusual construction for cross-reference; "introduced" is unambiguous and reads naturally. |
| Para 4 (primitive accumulation) | "He named it primitive not because it was simple but because it was first" | "Marx named it primitive not because it was simple but because it was first" | Two intervening sentences about English enclosures and colonial land seizures put distance between "Marx" and the pronoun "He." Replacing with the proper name removes a momentary re-attribution step for the reader. |

## Considered but Left

| Location | Element | Why Left |
|----------|---------|----------|
| Para 2 (opening) | "the crossing at Hampton and Brambleton, built on top of a community demolished to build it" — "it" briefly finds "community" as nearest noun before the reader corrects to "crossing" | Changing would require restructuring the sentence, and the next sentence immediately resolves any confusion. The ambiguity is fleeting, not structural. Left intact. |

| Line 17 | "Walk the corridors around what is now the Eastern Virginia Medical School campus today" — "today" at end slightly awkward | The positioning gives "today" emphasis as a temporal contrast to the 1957 demolition. Deliberate. Left intact. |
| Para 53 (Plotkin) | Long sentence with em-dash appositive elaborating "mapping the terrain between the soul and the living world" | Complex but not tangled — the subordinate clause after the dash is clearly an elaboration. Reading aloud it flows. Left intact. |

## Notes

The prose in this chapter is in very good shape. The voice is consistent throughout — direct, grounded, moving between analytical and personal registers without losing authority in either. The structural arc (Atlantic City to land trusts to farmland to consciousness to George to biodiversity to Standing Rock/Weelaunee to return to Atlantic City) is tightly controlled.

The personal sections (paragraphs beginning "I know a little of what Sorenson is describing" through "I remembered my ancestors") are the most idiosyncratic in the book and intentionally resist editorial smoothing. Sentence rhythm there is deliberately fragmented and incantatory. These were left entirely untouched.

The two changes made were minor pronoun/word-choice fixes that eliminate reader recalibration without touching meaning or rhythm. No content was removed.
