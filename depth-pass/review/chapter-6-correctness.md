## Flags for Human Review

| Location | Claim | Uncertainty |
|----------|-------|-------------|
| Para 3 (Robinson biography) | "His father had come from Barbados, worked as a laborer. His mother cleaned houses." | Multiple biographical sources (BlackPast, CounterPunch, Wikipedia) identify Robinson's biological father as Frederick Hill, a local nightclub owner in Oakland — no Barbados connection found. His mother Clara Whiteside is mentioned but not as a house cleaner. His stepfather was Dwight Robinson. Jason may have a source not in the public record; verify before publication. |
| Para 3 (Robinson at Berkeley) | "He arrived at Berkeley in the early 1960s as the Free Speech Movement was forming" | Robinson arrived at UC Berkeley in Fall 1959 and completed his BA in social anthropology in 1963. The Free Speech Movement began in Fall 1964 — after his undergraduate years ended. He was politically active at Berkeley during the early civil rights era, but the FSM was not forming while he was there as an undergraduate. The temporal framing conflates two distinct periods of Berkeley activism. Consider revising to "the late 1950s and early 1960s" or similar, without referencing the FSM. |
| Para 5 (Black Marxism out of print) | "For most of the first decade after publication, it was out of print." | This claim is widely repeated in secondary literature but is difficult to verify precisely. The second edition was published by UNC Press in 2000. Verify the specific out-of-print window against a primary or well-documented source. |
| Para 17 (Palmares governance) | "governed by elected councils" | Historical sources describe Palmares as governed by a king/chief (Ganga Zumba, later Zumbi) who held executive authority and consulted a council of elders from various mocambos. The leader was in some accounts elected or selected by the council, but the structure is closer to a chieftaincy with advisory council than a council-governed polity. "Elected councils" may overstate the democratic-horizontal nature of Palmares governance. Verify against Jason's source. |
| Para 20 (Great Zimbabwe population) | "At its height, the city held perhaps fifteen thousand people." | The most widely cited traditional estimate is 18,000 people. Scholarly debate places the range from under 10,000 to 20,000. "Fifteen thousand" is within the range but is not the standard figure. Verify Jason's source for this number. |

## Checked and Clear

- **Cedric Robinson, Black Marxism (1983):** Publication year confirmed correct.
- **W.E.B. Du Bois, *Black Reconstruction in America* (1935):** Year confirmed correct.
- **"Wages of whiteness":** Du Bois's concept as described is accurately rendered (he used "public and psychological wage"; "wages of whiteness" is the standard shorthand for his argument in this book).
- **Walter Rodney biographical basics:** Born in Guyana, educated at UWI Jamaica and SOAS London, PhD at 24 — all confirmed.
- ***How Europe Underdeveloped Africa* (1972):** Publication year confirmed correct.
- **Rodney in Tanzania and the Caribbean:** Confirmed — he taught at University of Dar es Salaam 1966–1972 and was in Jamaica 1968.
- **Rodney's age at death:** 38 years old, killed June 13, 1980 — confirmed (born March 23, 1942).
- **Rodney assassination method:** Bomb in a walkie-talkie, Georgetown, Guyana, 1980 — confirmed. The device was detonated in his brother's car; "placed in a walkie-talkie" is accurate.
- **Rodney's government connection:** The Burnham government is confirmed as having arranged the assassination per Guyana's National Assembly findings.
- **Great Zimbabwe:** Construction began eleventh century, peaked fourteenth and fifteenth centuries — confirmed. Gold trade routes to Swahili coast, Arab world, India, China — confirmed archaeologically. No-mortar construction — confirmed. Refusal by European colonizers to credit Africans — confirmed, historically documented.
- **Atlantic slave trade, "estimated twelve million":** Confirmed; scholarly estimates range 12–12.8 million transported, with 12.5 million the Voyages database figure.
- **Palmares destroyed 1694 by Portuguese colonial forces:** Confirmed. Located in northeast Brazil (captaincy of Pernambuco, now Alagoas state). Population of around 20,000 — within the documented range. Lasted "nearly a century" — it existed roughly 1605–1694, approximately 90 years; accurate.
- **CLR James and the Haitian Revolution:** Confirmed — *The Black Jacobins* (1938) analyzed it as a class revolution; the characterization is accurate.
- **Claudia Jones, triple oppression:** Confirmed — her 1949 essay "An End to the Neglect of the Problems of the Negro Woman!" argued for triple oppression of race, gender, and class. The claim that she named this "decades before intersectionality had a name" is accurate (Crenshaw coined "intersectionality" in 1989).
- **"Racial capitalism" as Robinson's concept:** Correctly attributed.
- **"Black radical tradition" as Robinson's concept:** Correctly attributed.
- **Structural adjustment, IMF/World Bank, 1980s–1990s:** Accurate description of the policy mechanism.
- **Ghana cocoa marketing board cut under structural adjustment:** Directionally accurate; well-documented case in the structural adjustment literature.

The biographical details could come from an interview or unpublished source Jason has; if so, a footnote would protect the claim. The FSM timing is the more clearly verifiable factual issue — the dates simply don't align — and should be corrected or reframed regardless of source. The Du Bois section accurately represents his argument from *Black Reconstruction*, including the "wages of whiteness" framing. The Rodney and Palmares sections are solid throughout, with the governance structure flag being a matter of nuance rather than error.
