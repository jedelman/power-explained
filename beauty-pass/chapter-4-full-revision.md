## Section 1 — Mondragón / Arizmendi

(opens with v4 NE opening — three paragraphs — then continues
with revised version of existing prose)

The Basques had a word for what they did when a neighbor's roof
needed mending, or a road washed out, or a widow's harvest came
in alone. *Auzolan.* Neighborhood work. The community would
assemble, do what needed doing, and go home without payment, on
the understanding that the obligation was reciprocal and that
the account would never be perfectly settled because the account
was the community itself. They had been doing this from before
they had written records of doing it. They had a word for the
household that ran the household — *etxe* — which was not a
building but a unit of stewardship extending backward through
the dead and forward through the not-yet-born. They had a word
for the specific named neighbor whose obligation it was to be
present at births and deaths and crises — *lehen auzoa*, first
neighbor. Community was not an abstraction. It was a specific
house down the path.

By February of 1941, much of this had been suppressed or
scattered. Franco's regime had outlawed the Basque language in
public life. Mondragón — *Arrasate* in Basque — had been on the
Republican side in the Civil War, and the Basques had been a
center of resistance: Guernica had been bombed in 1937, and the
parish priest in Mondragón itself had been shot. The years that
followed were called by the people who lived through them *los
años del hambre*, the years of hunger. Spain's autarky kept
imports out, the Basque industrial valleys ran below capacity,
and the cooperative and self-help organizations that had long
woven through Basque life had, in the words of one history,
"died away after the war." What remained were the words: etxe,
auzolan, lehen auzoa. And the practices the words named, dormant
under suppression but not gone.

Into this valley, in February of 1941, a twenty-five-year-old
priest arrived. He had been sentenced to death by Franco's
forces during the war and escaped the firing squad through what
is recorded as an administrative oversight. His name was José
María Arizmendiarrieta. He would later write: *La transformación
del mundo pasa por la transformación del hombre.* The
transformation of the world passes through the transformation of
the human being. He would also write: those who choose to make
history have the advantage over those who wait for the results
of change. He was not bringing anything to that valley that was
not already there. He was bringing back a way of seeing what was
already there.

---

He had one eye — lost in a childhood accident. He read badly, in
a monotone his new congregation found intricate and difficult to
follow. His homilies were repetitive, hard to parse. The local
parishioners asked the bishop to replace him within weeks of his
arrival. The bishop didn't. And Arizmendi — as everyone called
him — stayed.

He had wanted to study sociology in Belgium. Instead he had been
assigned to a parish thirty miles from his own home town. He
understood something quickly: faith without the material
conditions for a dignified life was a performance. He started a
school.

He taught. He selected the best students and arranged for them
to study industrial engineering in Zaragoza, two hundred
kilometers away — they worked fifty-five-hour weeks by day and
studied at night. He quoted Attlee and Beveridge alongside
Catholic social doctrine. He told his congregation: "The most
important advantage of cooperation is defeating the loneliness
that turns life into hell." He did not take a salary. He
traveled by bike.

It took fifteen years from his arrival before the first
cooperative was founded. He did not rush. The school in 1943.
The polytechnic. The study circles. The careful selection of
young people who could carry what he was teaching. The patient
construction of human beings who could build the institutions
the valley would need. *The transformation of the world passes
through the transformation of the human being.* He had written
this not as slogan but as method.

In 1956, five of his former students — Luis Usatorre, Jesús
Larrañaga, Alfonso Gorroñogoitia, José María Ormaechea, and
Javier Ortubay — pooled their savings and skills and founded a
cooperative. They bought a small workshop, moved it to
Mondragón, and began making paraffin stoves. The cooperative
was called ULGOR, after the first letters of their surnames. It
would later become Fagor. Arizmendi blessed the foundation stone
of the new pavilion. He never held any position in the
cooperatives he spent his life building. He died in 1976, the
year after Franco, having spent thirty-five years in a single
valley building what he believed the Gospel and the etxe
together required.

What was the structure of what he built? It was the modernization
of something the valley already had. The cooperative form was
*auzolan* applied to industrial production. The federation
structure was the etxe's communal stewardship applied to a
manufacturing enterprise extending across many cooperatives. The
multi-generational obligation — the mandatory community
investment funds that lock the cooperative's wealth into service
of the future — was the etxe's principle of stewardship across
generations made formally institutional. Arizmendi did not invent
this. He recovered it. The novelty was real. The recognition was
older.

In 2024, the federation had 81 cooperatives employing 70,085
people, with €11.2 billion in annual sales. It is the largest
employer in the Basque Country and the fifth-largest private
employer in Spain. The highest-paid worker earns at most six
times what the lowest-paid worker earns. The wage ratio is set
democratically by the worker-owners themselves and is voted on
periodically.

The system is not utopian. Cooperatives have left the federation
— Ampo and Irizar in 2008, ULMA and Orona in 2022, each over
disputes about governance and autonomy. In 2013, Fagor
Electrodomésticos went bankrupt; 5,600 workers were at risk;
roughly 95% of them were absorbed into other Mondragón
cooperatives within weeks. No member-worker has been laid off
in an economic downturn in sixty-nine years of operation. Pope
Francis declared Arizmendi a "venerable servant of God" in 2015,
one step from canonization. His town is still debating it.

Nobody asked him to do any of it.

## Section 2 — Champlain Housing Trust

In 1981, the year Ronald Reagan took office and began the federal
withdrawal of funding for affordable housing and community
development, a thirty-nine-year-old democratic socialist named
Bernie Sanders was elected mayor of Burlington, Vermont, by ten
votes. He served four terms. In his second term, in 1983, members
of his administration brought him a proposal for a community
land trust — a nonprofit corporation that would own land
permanently while selling the houses on it to working-class
families, with a resale formula limiting how much the seller
could capture from appreciation.

Sanders had reservations. Why should the working class be
limited to shared equity when middle- and upper-class families
could build full wealth from their homes? Wouldn't this create a
second-class form of ownership? He was talked through it. The
city's land trust would not be a substitute for full
homeownership for those who could otherwise afford it; it would
be a route into housing for those who otherwise could not. And
crucially, the land's value — the speculative gain that
otherwise flowed to whoever happened to buy at the right moment
— would stay in the trust, available to the next family that
needed a home.

In 1984, the Burlington Community Land Trust was founded with a
$200,000 seed grant from the city, supplemented by a loan from
the municipal pension fund matched by Burlington Savings Bank
and federal HUD funds. It was the first community land trust in
the United States to be initiated by a municipal government.

It was opposed. A group backed by real estate interests,
Homeowners Against the Land Trust, picketed City Hall. Sanders
and other progressive officials were red-baited — accused of
seizing land and betraying property rights. The pushback was
loudest in the Old North End, the working-class neighborhood
where the trust was concentrating its early purchases. Some
incumbent homeowners worried about property values. The Trust
went ahead anyway.

The mechanism is precise. The Trust owns the land permanently
through 99-year ground leases. Homeowners own the houses. When
a house sells, a resale formula lets the seller recoup their
investment and a share of appreciation — but not the full
speculative gain. The land's value stays in the Trust. The next
buyer pays below market because they are not buying the land.

What this means, in practice, is that someone made a choice.
Someone who bought a Champlain house in 1991 sold it in 2003 for
less than the open market would have paid — because they had
agreed to the structure when they bought, because they had
decided that the affordability their neighbor would need
mattered more than the premium they could have taken. Then
someone else made that choice again in 2003, and again in 2015.
The neighbor they were providing for didn't exist yet when the
choice was made. That is the structure.

Burlington's housing market has gone through multiple boom
cycles since 1984. The Champlain Housing Trust's homes have
stayed affordable through all of them. Not through subsidy,
which is a program, and programs end when political will
fluctuates. Through governance: the community decided, once,
collectively and bindingly, that this land was not for sale.
That decision, made once, persists structurally, independent of
whoever is currently in office.

Today the Trust holds over 3,000 units of permanently affordable
housing across a three-county service area in Vermont. The
model has been replicated in hundreds of communities across the
United States. Forty-two years of evidence. The land that
Burlington took out of the speculative market in 1984 is still
out of it.

## Section 3 — Cooperation Jackson

Jackson, Mississippi: median household income around $33,000;
poverty rate approaching one in three among Black residents; a
state legislature that passed a law in 2023 expanding
state-appointed courts with jurisdiction over Jackson
specifically — a maneuver so naked that the NAACP called it a
modern form of plantation governance. The water system has
failed repeatedly. The state has refused to fund repairs at
levels even close to what its own engineers say are needed.

Cooperation Jackson was founded there in 2014, rooted in decades
of Black liberation organizing and in the Jackson-Kush Plan
developed by organizers in the Malcolm X Grassroots Movement.
It was connected to the election of Chokwe Lumumba as mayor in
June 2013 — Lumumba won with 86 percent of the vote despite
being massively outspent. The Republic of New Afrika had decades
of organizing in Mississippi behind him. The cooperative
economics platform he ran on was the public expression of work
that had been in motion for many years.

Lumumba was sworn in on July 1, 2013. He died on February 25,
2014, of heart failure, at the age of 66. He had been mayor for
eight months. His family commissioned an independent autopsy.
The organizing continued.

The cooperatives Cooperation Jackson built are deliberately
small. Landscaping. Organic farming. A community production
center with 3D printing facilities and a small fabrication
shop. A childcare cooperative. The strategy is not rapid scale.
It is the patient construction of a complete ecosystem —
cooperative production linked to cooperative finance linked to
cooperative education linked to community land, all held
together by people's assemblies and years of political
organizing.

What Cooperation Jackson proves is not size. It is the existence
of a method: building from the most marginalized rather than the
most advantaged, rooted explicitly in Black liberation history,
patient about the timeline. Building in conditions of maximum
hostility. Continuing when the person who carried the vision
dies. Chokwe Antar Lumumba, his son, ran for mayor in 2017 on
the same platform and won. The work has now passed through one
generation and is being held by the next.

## Section 4 — Kerala

Kerala is an Indian state of approximately 35 million people
with a per-capita income significantly below the national
average. Its life expectancy is roughly 77 years (men 75, women
80, per 2023 estimates) — the highest in India, matching
countries with incomes three to four times higher. Infant
mortality is India's lowest. Literacy was recorded at 94 percent
in the 2011 census, the highest in the country; subsequent
surveys put it higher still.

The explanation is not a mystery. Decades of communist-led
government that broke up the landlord class, invested in public
health and public education, and treated governance as
infrastructure for collective wellbeing rather than as
management of conditions for private accumulation. Strong labor
organizing that maintained wages above the national norm. A
state whose democratic institutions have been, over generations,
held accountable to the organized working class.

Kerala is not a commons in the strict institutional sense. It is
something else — what happens when the people who depend on
public institutions are also the people who govern them. The
distinction matters: it is not "the government doing things for
people" (the welfare state's model), and it is not "people
governing what they share without the state" (the strict
commons model). It is a state genuinely accountable to its own
population because that population is organized.

The numbers say what that produces. Seventy years of left
governance. A population that lives, on average, half a decade
longer than it would in comparable Indian states. A floor that
holds.

## Section 5 — Mutual aid 2020, and the fridges

In March 2020, mutual aid networks formed across the United
States in days. Neighborhood food distribution. Prescription
pickups. Childcare exchanges. PPE sharing. Built on group chats
and spreadsheets by people with no prior organizational
experience, without any institutional support, in the first
weeks of a pandemic that the federal government was actively
mishandling. Within a month, there were thousands of these
networks.

In Crown Heights, Brooklyn, a network called Crown Heights
Mutual Aid organized food distribution and didn't dissolve when
the acute phase passed. It converted — into ongoing food
support, into tenant organizing, into the sustained
infrastructure of neighbors who had found each other through
crisis and decided to stay found.

On sidewalks in dozens of cities, community fridges appeared.
Refrigerators stocked collectively, governed by the simple rule
that anyone could take what they needed and anyone could
contribute what they could. No application. No eligibility
determination. No caseworker. A fridge on a sidewalk, stocked by
the neighborhood, for the neighborhood. The first ones were
plugged in to power borrowed from sympathetic businesses. People
built awnings to protect them from rain. Volunteer schedules
formed and dissolved and reformed. Some fridges were broken,
unplugged by hostile neighbors, taken away by police. Many
weren't.

Some of these networks persisted. Not everywhere, and not at the
scale the underlying conditions require. But the capacity was
demonstrated. Millions of people experienced what it feels like
to be in relationships of mutual dependence and reciprocal care,
and many of them did not simply return to isolation when the
emergency passed. Some of what formed has stuck.

What the 2020 mutual aid wave proves is that the practice is not
extinct. People know how to do this. They had not forgotten.
What had been suppressed could be revived in days. The Basques
had a word for this — *auzolan* — and so did the Asturians (it
is *andecha* in their language), and so did communities in
Madagascar, in the Andes, in the American Plains, in
pre-enclosure England. The thing the 2020 networks built had
been built before, in other languages, with other names. It was
not new. The conditions had simply, briefly, made it visible
again.

## Section 6 — Chiapas

In 1994, on the day the North American Free Trade Agreement
took effect, the Zapatista Army of National Liberation rose
against the Mexican state in Chiapas. Thousands of indigenous
combatants — Tzotzil, Tzeltal, Tojolabal, Chol, Mam, Zoque —
took five municipal seats. The armed phase lasted twelve days
before a ceasefire was negotiated under domestic and
international pressure. The political phase has lasted thirty
years and is ongoing.

In 1996, the EZLN and the Mexican government signed the San
Andrés Accords, which would have constitutionally recognized
indigenous autonomy. The federal legislature passed a watered-
down version in 2001 that the Zapatistas denounced as a betrayal.
In 2003, the EZLN declared that it would implement the original
accords *itself*, in its own territory, without permission. On
August 9, 2003, in Oventic, the Zapatistas announced the
creation of five *caracoles* — snail-shells, governance centers
named after a spiral that turns inward and outward at once —
and the Juntas de Buen Gobierno, the Councils of Good
Government, that would coordinate them.

For twenty years, the caracoles administered health clinics,
schools teaching in indigenous languages, agricultural
cooperatives, restorative justice forums. The Juntas rotated
their members and operated on the principle of *mandar
obedeciendo* — to govern by obeying. The structure absorbed
international support and outside attention without losing its
indigenous grounding.

In November 2023, the Zapatistas announced that the Juntas de
Buen Gobierno and the autonomous municipalities were being
dissolved. Cartel violence had been growing in Chiapas; the
Sinaloa Cartel and the Jalisco New Generation Cartel were
fighting for control of border smuggling routes; the Mexican
state was either unwilling or unable to intervene. The
Zapatistas decided that the existing structure, with its
twelve regional councils, was too vulnerable to cartel pressure
and too concentrated to survive the coming era.

What they announced in its place was a more radical
decentralization. Instead of dozens of autonomous municipalities,
there would be *thousands* of Local Autonomous Governments
(GALs), one in each Zapatista community. Instead of twelve
Good Government Juntas, hundreds of Collectives of Autonomous
Government (CGAZ). The caracoles remain, but as community
centers, not governance hubs. Power was pushed downward, to the
village assembly. The explicit horizon, announced in the
communiqué, was *one hundred and twenty years — seven
generations*. "In the midst of collapse," Subcomandante Moisés
wrote, "we have to look far ahead."

This is not the failure of the Zapatista project. This is the
project doing what it has always claimed to be: a form of
governance that develops itself through governance, in response
to specific conditions, on its own timeline. The 1994 uprising,
the 2003 caracoles, the 2023 GALs — these are three iterations
of the same long practice, each adapted to the conditions in
front of it.

Thirty years after the uprising, schools teach in indigenous
languages. Community health clinics operate. Cooperative
agricultural enterprises run. Judicial systems based on
restorative principles continue. The communities have survived
thirty years of low-intensity military pressure, economic
blockade, paramilitaries, and now cartel war. They have
restructured themselves more than once. They have made
mistakes and named them in public. They have outlasted a
dozen governments that intended to outlast them.

What they have built has outlasted, and has continued to
restructure itself to outlast more.

## Section 8 — The closing litany

Arizmendi on his bike in the Basque mountains. Auzolan in
February of 1941, dormant under suppression and not yet returned.
Bernie Sanders, twenty years old at the March on Washington,
elected mayor by ten votes in 1981 and not letting the proposal
die in 1983. Eight farmers in black blouses in Valencia's
cathedral plaza, the Water Tribunal in session for a thousand
years. Zanjera farmers in Ilocos rebuilding bamboo dams after
the typhoon, the cook feeding everyone while the work gets
done, the obligation called *bayanihan* in their language but
operating in everyone else's too. The Champlain homeowner who
sold below market in 2003 for a neighbor who did not yet exist.
Chokwe Lumumba's campaign, and the eight months, and the
organizing that continued, and the son who picked it up. The
community fridge on the Brooklyn sidewalk, stocked by whoever
walked by that morning. The restaurant in Norfolk that ran for
five years. The Zapatistas dissolving their own structure in
order to keep building. The caracol.

---

These are not all the same thing. They operate at different
scales, in different conditions, with different histories and
different tools and different definitions of what they are
building. What they share is the structure Ostrom found in the
cases that worked: people governing something they depend on
together, through rules they developed themselves, in response
to the specific conditions they live with. None of these were
invented from theory. All of them were either continuations of
practices older than capitalism, or reconstructions of such
practices in conditions where they had been broken, or both.

The question people ask is: but has it actually worked? Has it
worked at scale? Under real pressure? Over time?

Mondragón at sixty-nine years. The Champlain Housing Trust at
forty-two. Kerala at seventy years of left governance. The
Zapatistas at thirty, restructured twice in their own lifetime
and now committed to a hundred and twenty more. The Valencia
Water Tribunal at a thousand. The Swiss alpine commons at seven
hundred. *Auzolan* from before recorded history, in continuous
practice in Basque villages and across the diaspora, into the
present moment.

The answer is in the record. It works. It has been working in
hundreds of places, across centuries, at scales from a sidewalk
fridge to seventy thousand workers to thirty-five million
people. The proof is not theoretical. It is present tense. It
is happening now. The wizard who knows how to look can see it.

## What this revision did, in summary

1. **Opens with the ancestors named in their own language.**
   Auzolan, etxe, lehen auzoa, los años del hambre. The chapter's
   structural argument (Arizmendi was recovering, not inventing)
   is shown by the prose enacting the recovery.

2. **Deepens the Sanders / Champlain section** with the actual
   political history: the ten-vote election, his initial
   reservations, the real opposition from Homeowners Against
   the Land Trust, the red-baiting. The structure's durability
   is more remarkable because it was contested at every step.

3. **Names the eight months of Lumumba's tenure precisely and
   honors the continuation.** Adds Chokwe Antar Lumumba's
   election in 2017 — the work passing through a generation.

4. **Fixes the Kerala figures.** Replaces the 75-year flag with
   2023 estimates, acknowledges the literacy figure is from
   2011 census with subsequent surveys higher, gives breathing
   room for the data without losing the structural argument.

5. **Adds auzolan/etxe to the mutual aid section** as recovery
   of suppressed practice, names parallel terms across cultures
   (andecha, bayanihan). The 2020 mutual aid wave becomes part
   of a longer record, not an isolated event.

6. **Updates Chiapas to 2023 reorganization.** The chapter now
   honors what the Zapatistas actually did: restructured their
   own governance because the old structure could not survive
   the new conditions. This *strengthens* the chapter's
   argument that commons institutions develop themselves
   through governance.

7. **Notes the failure modes honestly** (Mondragón cooperatives
   leaving the federation, Fagor bankruptcy with reabsorption,
   Lumumba's family commissioning an independent autopsy, the
   broken and unplugged fridges). The chapter no longer reads
   as utopian; it reads as actual.

8. **Closing litany absorbs all of the above.** Auzolan is in
   the litany. The Zapatista restructuring is in the litany.
   The continuation through generations is in the litany. The
   close ends with "The wizard who knows how to look can see
   it" — an axial door toward later chapters about how to learn
   to see.
