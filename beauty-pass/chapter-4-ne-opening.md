## The Basque words load-bearing in this opening

**etxe** — the ancestral house as economic and social unit, the
household as a unit of accountability "that extended backward
through the dead and forward through the not-yet-born" (Basqueland).

**baserri** — the farmhouse as the physical etxe.

**auzolan** — *auzo* (neighborhood) + *lan* (work). Collective
neighborhood work, freely given on the understanding of reciprocal
obligation. Used to build churches, maintain roads, harvest crops,
rebuild after fire, assist a sick farmer or a widow. Practiced
"from before recorded history" (Basqueland) and "continuously"
across Basque Country (multiple sources).

**lehen auzoa** — "first neighbor." Each etxe had a designated
first neighbor whose obligation was to provide first aid in crisis,
assist at births and deaths, be present when the household's
resources were stretched beyond what it could bear alone.

**los años del hambre** — "the years of hunger." What Basques and
Spaniards under Franco's autarky called the 1940s themselves.
Not our framing for them; theirs.

## Arizmendi's own voice (verified across multiple sources)

"La transformación del mundo pasa por la transformación del hombre."
— The transformation of the world passes through the transformation
of the human being.

"Those who opt to make history and change the course of events
themselves have the advantage over those who decide to wait
passively for the results of the change."

## Draft v4

The Basques had a word for what they did when a neighbor's roof
needed mending, or a road washed out, or a widow's harvest came
in alone. *Auzolan.* Neighborhood work. The community would
assemble, do what needed doing, and go home without payment, on
the understanding that the obligation was reciprocal and that the
account would never be perfectly settled because the account was
the community itself. They had been doing this from before they
had written records of doing it. They had a word for the household
that ran the household — *etxe* — which was not a building but a
unit of stewardship extending backward through the dead and
forward through the not-yet-born. They had a word for the
specific named neighbor whose obligation it was to be present at
births and deaths and crises — *lehen auzoa*, first neighbor.
Community was not an abstraction. It was a specific house down
the path.

By February of 1941, much of this had been suppressed or
scattered. Franco's regime had outlawed the Basque language in
public life. Mondragón — *Arrasate* in Basque — had been on the
Republican side in the Civil War, and the Basques had been a
center of resistance: Guernica had been bombed in 1937, and the
parish priest in Mondragón itself had been shot. The years that
followed were called by the people who lived through them *los
años del hambre*, the years of hunger. Spain's autarky kept
imports out, the Basque industrial valleys ran below capacity, and
the cooperative and self-help organizations that had long woven
through Basque life had, in the words of one history, "died away
after the war." What remained were the words: etxe, auzolan,
lehen auzoa. And the practices the words named, dormant under
suppression but not gone.

Into this valley, in February of 1941, a twenty-five-year-old
priest arrived. He had been sentenced to death by Franco's forces
during the war and escaped the firing squad through what is
recorded as an administrative oversight. His name was José María
Arizmendiarrieta. He would later write: *La transformación del
mundo pasa por la transformación del hombre.* The transformation
of the world passes through the transformation of the human
being. He would also write: those who choose to make history have
the advantage over those who wait for the results of change. He
was not bringing anything to that valley that was not already
there. He was bringing back a way of seeing what was already
there.

## What this is doing technically

- NE stance, sustained for three paragraphs. The reader is held
  before-dawn — what was there in the dark — through the language
  itself. The Basque words sit on the page like ancestors named.
- Praise-first as ancestral practice, not sentimental atmosphere.
  Auzolan is honored. The etxe is honored. Lehen auzoa is honored.
  Reading the chapter, the reader is in the company of these
  things before being in the company of the priest.
- Honest about suppression. The grief of the chapter is NOT
  deferred to the restaurant section; it is also here in the NE
  opening, named accurately. *Los años del hambre.* The bombed
  city. The shot priest. The outlawed language.
- The chapter's structural argument is foreshadowed: Arizmendi
  is not bringing the cooperative form to the valley; the
  cooperative form was already there, suppressed. He is bringing
  back a way of seeing it. This is the wizard-reads-terrain move
  delivered structurally, not labeled.
- Arizmendi's own voice enters. He gets to speak before the
  outsider's voice fully takes over. The two quoted lines do
  the work: one is theological (transformation of the human),
  one is political (making history vs. waiting). Both will
  reverberate through the rest of the chapter.
- The escape-from-the-firing-squad fact lands hard. The man who
  arrives in February 1941 is a man who had been condemned to
  death and was alive by accident. This conditions everything
  that follows. He is not a young priest with a project. He is
  a young priest who knows he is living on borrowed time and is
  not going to waste it.
- The S–N axis is encoded: the Basque words and ancestral
  practices are N (commitment, ancestors, what is owed across
  generations), the Civil War facts and dates are S (precision,
  evidence). The chapter's seat-and-gesture is in the bones
  before the figure appears.

## What I'm still uncertain about

1. Three paragraphs may be long. The chapter is already long. But
   each paragraph is doing distinct work (the words / the
   suppression / the arrival). I don't see compression that
   doesn't lose something.

2. Phrasing of the final sentence ("He was not bringing anything
   to that valley that was not already there. He was bringing back
   a way of seeing what was already there.") — this is the
   structural claim that the whole chapter rests on. Two short
   sentences. I think this is the right weight for it. You'll
   feel it.

3. Whether to keep "what is recorded as an administrative
   oversight" or just say "an administrative oversight." The
   "what is recorded as" is a small piece of epistemic honesty;
   it concedes that historians are quoting earlier accounts and
   may not have eyewitness verification. The shorter version is
   cleaner. Felt-pass.

4. The Basque words ride well in English prose, but reader
   accessibility deserves a check. *Etxe, auzolan, lehen auzoa,
   los años del hambre, Arrasate.* That's five non-English terms
   in three paragraphs. Each is glossed in line. I think it
   reads, but you know the reader better than I do.

## What I learned doing this

Honoring the ancestors is not stylistic. It changes what the
chapter is doing. v1-v3 were variations on "how do we open with
appropriate reverence." v4 actually opens with the ancestors
named, in their own language, doing what they did. The chapter's
argument that Arizmendi was recovering rather than inventing is
structurally stronger because the recovery is shown — the words
and practices are present on the page — before the recovery is
claimed in argument.

This is also the chapter's pedagogy in microcosm: spoor-conscious
reading of what was already in the sand. (For my back pocket:
auzolan is the print. Etxe is the print. Lehen auzoa is the
print. The chapter reads them.) The reader doesn't need
Liebenberg to be taught this; the reader just needs to be shown
prints and given the language to recognize them.
