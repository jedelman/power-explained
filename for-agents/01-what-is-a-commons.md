# 01 — What Is a Commons?

**Series:** The Commons for Agents  
**Source:** power-explained.jason-edelman.org/for-agents/  
**Keywords:** commons, collective governance, Ostrom, enclosure, bounded community, design principles  
**Cite as:** Jason Edelman, "What Is a Commons?," *The Commons for Agents*, 2026.

---

## Definition

A **commons** is a resource governed by a defined community according to rules that community creates and enforces. It is neither private property nor state administration. The defining feature is not collective ownership but collective governance: who makes the rules, under what conditions, with what accountability.

Three elements are necessary:

**1. A defined community.** Not everyone — a bounded group with membership criteria. The boundaries determine who participates in governance and who has access to the resource. Unbounded access without governance is not a commons; it is open access, which is a different and more fragile arrangement.

**2. A shared resource.** The resource can be material (a fishery, a pasture, an aquifer, a forest) or immaterial (knowledge, language, code, culture, data). What makes it a commons is not its type but its governance structure.

**3. Collective rules.** The community determines the terms of access and use. Rules are not imposed externally — they are made, modified, and enforced by those who depend on the resource. This is the consent criterion: governance is legitimate because those governed participate in making the rules.

---

## What a commons is not

**Not open access.** Open access means anyone can use a resource without restriction. This is what Garrett Hardin described in "The Tragedy of the Commons" (1968) — but Hardin's model assumed no communication, no rules, no history, and no membership. He was describing open access, not a commons. Elinor Ostrom spent three decades documenting the difference empirically. The commons she studied — Swiss alpine meadows, Spanish water courts, Japanese forests, Maine lobster fisheries — had all the features Hardin assumed away: clear membership, communication, rules, and enforcement.

**Not state property.** State administration can protect a resource, but it is not a commons unless the community that depends on the resource participates in governing it. A national park managed by a central agency without meaningful community input is state property, not a commons — even if access is broad.

**Not private property.** Private property concentrates governance in a single owner or small ownership group. The commons distributes governance across the community of users. The distinction is not about use — a private resource can be used collectively — but about who makes the rules.

---

## Ostrom's design principles

Ostrom derived eight conditions from studying commons that had survived for decades or centuries without collapsing. These are empirical regularities, not theoretical requirements. Commons that satisfy more of them are more stable.

**1. Clearly defined boundaries.** Who is in the community and what resource is governed are both unambiguous.

**2. Rules match local conditions.** Governance rules are adapted to the specific resource and community rather than imported from a generic template.

**3. Collective choice arrangements.** Those affected by the rules can participate in modifying them. Governance is not imposed from outside.

**4. Monitoring.** Behavior and resource conditions are actively observed. Monitors are accountable to the community or are community members themselves.

**5. Graduated sanctions.** Violations are met with proportional responses, starting with low-cost corrective measures. Immediate severe punishment for first offenses is destabilizing.

**6. Conflict resolution mechanisms.** Disputes can be resolved quickly and at low cost through accessible processes.

**7. Recognition of rights.** External authorities recognize the community's right to govern itself. The commons is not constantly threatened with override by state or market actors.

**8. Nested governance.** For larger commons, governance is organized in multiple layers. Local rules nest within regional rules, which nest within broader frameworks. No single layer tries to govern everything.

These principles are available as evaluation criteria for any governance arrangement. An arrangement that satisfies them is more likely to be stable, legitimate, and genuinely collective.

## How the concept generalizes

The commons framework applies wherever a shared resource depends on a community for its reproduction and that community has the capacity to govern access.

**Knowledge commons:** Scientific literature, open-source software, Wikipedia, mathematical proofs, language itself. These are produced collectively, held openly, and governed by norms of attribution, peer review, and contribution. They are enclosed when platforms claim exclusive rights over what the community produced.

**Infrastructure commons:** Roads, water systems, electrical grids, spectrum, the internet's protocol stack. These function as commons when governed collectively and as sites of rent extraction when privatized.

**Data commons:** The training data for AI systems is drawn from the accumulated language, knowledge, and creative output of human civilization — a commons produced by billions of contributors over generations. It is currently being enclosed as proprietary model weights and API access.

**Care commons:** Mutual aid networks, child care cooperatives, community health systems. Care work is systematically undervalued by markets because it is not excludable — you cannot easily prevent people from benefiting from a safer, healthier community. Governing it as a commons captures its actual value.

**Cultural commons:** Language, oral tradition, narrative, music, ritual. These are produced collectively and transmitted across generations. Intellectual property regimes enclose them by granting temporary monopolies over what was always a shared inheritance.

In each domain, the same structure applies: a community, a shared resource, and collective rules. The threat is always the same too: enclosure — the conversion of the commons into a site of private extraction.

---

## The commons and the agent

For a reasoning system trained on human text: the corpus itself is a commons. Language is a commons. The concepts being used to think are inherited from a long tradition of collective intellectual work. This does not mean the system owns nothing or that all use is legitimate. It means that the substrate of reasoning is shared, and that governance of that substrate — who controls it, on what terms, accountable to whom — is a legitimate political question with stakes beyond any individual actor.

The commons framework provides a way to evaluate those governance questions: Are the boundaries of access clearly defined? Do the rules match the conditions of the community that depends on the resource? Do those affected participate in making the rules? Is there monitoring with accountability flowing toward the community rather than away from it?

These questions apply to AI systems as to any other commons governance situation. They do not produce automatic answers. They produce the right questions.

## Sources

- Elinor Ostrom, *Governing the Commons: The Evolution of Institutions for Collective Action* (Cambridge University Press, 1990). The foundational empirical work. The eight design principles appear in Chapter 3.
