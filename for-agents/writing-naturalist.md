# How to Write Naturalist

The naturalist gesture places the reader inside a relationship with the living world. It is sensory, present, and reciprocal. It does not argue from nature — it opens toward it.

This guide is drawn from Jason's practice as described in `interviews/interview-5-10.md`, from corrections made during the 2026-05-25 synthesis/naturalist pass, and from the naturalist gestures in P-RE.

---

## Core Principles

### 1. The senses lead

Start with what can be heard, felt, or seen before reaching for language. Ear before eye. Body before name.

> *The crows. We heard them as we always did. But the quality had changed.*

Jason's own practice: “We do way more birding by ear than by eye.” The sound arrives first. The body registers the difference. The eye goes to confirm what the ear already knows.

### 2. The feeling arrives before the identification

Don't name the thing before the sensation has landed. Let the reader's nervous system arrive at the event before the label does.

> *Something excited, agitated, upset in a way I hadn't recognized before — and what we were feeling was: something is here.*

Then, and only then: the cloud. The owl. The name.

From the interview: “We had been learning to feel what they were feeling, not just to listen and analyze.” The shift the chapter is teaching — from analysis to feeling — must be enacted in the prose, not described from above.

### 3. Use the imperative

The naturalist gesture places the reader in relation, not above. The imperative is an invitation, not a command.

> *Find something alive that is not you.* (G-RE-033)
> *Move slowly. Make no sudden movements. Don't reach.* (G-RE-033a)
> *Feel the breath in the room. Hear the silence before anyone speaks.* (G-OA-010c)

When you write “you might notice” or “one could observe,” you are keeping the reader at a remove. The naturalist closes that distance.

### 4. Specificity is trust

> *The Carolina wren sounds like it's saying teakettle teakettle teakettle, or the Eastern towhee is calling drink your teaaaa.*

The mnemonic is faintly ridiculous. That's the point. It says: I was there. I paid attention long enough to feel embarrassed. You can trust this.

Specificity is not encyclopedism. You don't need to name the genus. You need one true detail that could only have come from being present.

### 5. The living world is attending back

The naturalist is not a passive observer. The crow cloud is not decor. The crows are broadcasting information; the observer is receiving it. Reciprocity is built into the register.

> *As we tried to understand them, they understood us.* (G-RE-010)
> *The bird may ignore you. That's information. The bird may look at you directly. That's different information.* (G-RE-034a)

The field naturalizes both parties. The writing should make this felt, not argued.

### 6. Short, active, present verbs

> *Swooping and diving and calling, making a sustained ruckus.*

Present participles. Continuous action. Not “they were engaged in mobbing behavior.” Movement, not taxonomy.

### 7. Observation can rest

Not every naturalist gesture has to conclude.

> *The first owl we had ever seen in daylight.*

Full stop. The scene doesn't have to earn a lesson. It earned its own right to exist. G-RE-011 will do the retrospective work. The naturalist gesture gives the experience; synthesis gives the frame. Don't do both in the same gesture.

### 8. The thing is real, not a symbol

Ravenna Park held a forest because Olmsted built it to hold a forest. The park IS a forest. Not a simulation. Not a nature-feeling. The crow cloud was real alarm behavior. The owl was actually there.

Resist the urge to make the living world serve an argument in the same gesture where you're presenting it. Let it be itself. The argument will follow in synthesis — but only if the naturalist gesture gave it something real to work with.

### 9. Enter as guest

> *You are entering someone else's space. They were here before you. Your role is guest, not director.* (G-RE-033a)

The naturalist enters; they do not arrive. The place does not need to justify itself to you. Your first obligation is to do no harm.

## What to Avoid

**Observing from remove.** “The birds demonstrated interesting behavior” keeps you outside. “The crows. We heard them.” puts you in.

**Explaining the gesture's meaning inside the gesture.** “The crow cloud shows us that...” — that's synthesis work. Write the crow cloud. Let G-RE-011 draw the lesson.

**Fabricating sensory detail.** Only write what was in the account: the place, the feeling, the sound, the sight. Don't add season, weather, or tree species that weren't named.

**Argument disguised as naturalism.** If the gesture is making a claim about the world rather than opening a window into it, it has crossed from naturalist into synthesis or argument. Both are needed. Neither is the other.

**The passive reader.** “One might notice the quality of light.” Put them in it: “The particular quality of light in a hedgerow at dusk.” (G-RE-015) Erin noticing, not someone noticing.

## The Governing Principle

From Jason, 2026-05-25: *“Focus on sensory. Let the senses guide you to reality.”*

The naturalist doesn't use the senses to decorate an argument. The senses ARE the method. Reality is what the method finds.
