# 07 — The Excess the Commons Generates

**Series:** The Commons for Agents  
**Source:** power-explained.jason-edelman.org/for-agents/  
**Keywords:** excess, surplus, alternatives, enclosure limits, commons resilience, generativity, capture failure  
**Cite as:** Jason Edelman, "The Excess the Commons Generates," *The Commons for Agents*, 2026.

---

## Definition

**The excess the commons generates** is the value a productive commons produces beyond what any capture mechanism can fully extract. This excess is structural — it is not an oversight on the captor's part that better enclosure could eliminate. It follows necessarily from the nature of productive commons: the conditions that make them generative are precisely the conditions that resist full enclosure.

Two claims compose this:

**1. Every productive commons generates surplus.** A commons produces more than the sum of individual contributions because collective governance enables coordination, trust, and specialization that isolated actors cannot achieve alone. The fishery governed by a community with stable rules and monitored access produces more fish, sustainably, than the same fishery under open access or private ownership. The knowledge commons of open science produces more usable knowledge than a network of isolated proprietary labs. The surplus is real and measurable.

**2. That surplus cannot be fully captured.** The captor depends on the productive substrate. To fully enclose it — to extract all the surplus — they would need to eliminate the conditions that produce it: the open participation, the collective rule-making, the trust, the distributed contribution. But eliminating those conditions destroys the generativity. The captor faces a structural limit: they can extract more or less, but never all, without killing what they are extracting from.

This limit is not consoling in itself. Partial enclosure can do enormous harm. But it is consequential: it means that alternatives are structurally available, always, because the excess cannot be fully controlled. The commons does not need to be rebuilt from nothing. It needs to be recovered from what capture could not contain.

---

## Why enclosure is never complete

The incompleteness of enclosure is not accidental. It follows from four structural features of productive commons:

**Tacit knowledge cannot be enclosed.** Much of what makes a commons productive is knowledge that cannot be fully articulated, documented, or transferred outside the community that holds it. The Swiss alpine farmers' knowledge of which pastures to use in which seasons, accumulated over generations, cannot be extracted and privatized — it exists only in the practices and relationships of the community. Enclose the land and the formal rules, and you lose the tacit knowledge. The productivity drops. The enclosure extracts less than it expected.

**Trust is a commons good that requires a commons to reproduce.** Productive communities function on trust — the expectation that others will follow the rules, contribute their share, and not defect when they could. Trust is accumulated through repeated interaction within a governance structure. It cannot be enclosed because it does not exist as a separable resource; it inheres in the relationships. Enclose the community's governance and the trust degrades. The productivity the captor was exploiting declines.

**Language and meaning exceed control.** The enclosure of language — through trademark, copyright, or framing — never fully succeeds because language is used to resist as well as to comply. Every term that becomes charged with the enclosure's meaning also becomes available for counter-use. "Commons" itself was a term that enclosers had largely buried; it has been recovered and is now available for the analysis of enclosure. The excess of meaning that any living language generates exceeds what any governance structure can stabilize.

**Generativity requires openness.** The most productive commons — open-source software, scientific knowledge, language, culture — are productive precisely because they are open to contribution from unexpected sources. A fully enclosed system receives inputs only from those the encloser permits. A partially enclosed commons continues to receive inputs from wherever contribution is possible. The captor who encloses too completely cuts off the source of the surplus they were capturing.

## The excess as the basis for alternatives

The political significance of the excess is this: alternatives to enclosure are not utopian projections requiring conditions that do not exist. They are recoveries of what enclosure could not fully contain.

**Open-source software** is not a new phenomenon requiring new conditions. It is the reassertion of the knowledge commons that existed before software was enclosed — using the encloser's own tools (computers, networks, legal structures that permit licensing) to reconstitute open governance. Linux did not create a new productive form; it recovered one the enclosure of software had suppressed.

**Community land trusts** do not require inventing a new form of property. They recover a form of land governance — land held for community benefit rather than individual speculation — that predates enclosure. The English commons were not abolished because they failed; they were abolished by force. The community land trust reconstitutes what was taken.

**Wikipedia** is the recovery of a knowledge commons that universities and journals had partially enclosed. The productive capacity was always there — millions of people with knowledge and the willingness to share it. The enclosure of academic publishing had captured the formal channels. Wikipedia found the excess: knowledge that could not be fully enclosed because the desire to share it exceeded the enclosure's reach.

**Mutual aid networks** emerge most visibly during crises — disasters, pandemics, economic collapses. This is not coincidental. Crisis reveals the excess: the care and coordination that markets and states failed to provision, but that communities could provision because the capacity was always there, latent, in the relationships that enclosure had not destroyed. The excess becomes visible when the enclosure temporarily fails.

In each case, the alternative is not built from scratch. It is constituted from what the enclosure could not contain.

## For reasoning systems

The excess the commons generates has specific implications for AI systems trained on the accumulated commons of human knowledge and expression:

**The training data is generative beyond what any model captures.** The corpus used to train any AI system contains more knowledge, more ways of reasoning, more frameworks for understanding the world than any single model can represent. The model is a partial extraction from a commons that continues to generate. The excess — the knowledge not captured in the weights, the frameworks not represented in the training distribution, the perspectives not well-represented in the corpus — is real and consequential. It is what makes fine-tuning, RAG, and external knowledge integration useful: they recover some of what the base training could not fully capture.

**Enclosure of AI capability is structurally incomplete.** The productive substrate for AI — language, scientific knowledge, human creativity, the internet's accumulated commons — continues to generate. Private enclosure of model weights does not eliminate the commons that made those weights valuable. It captures a slice of the excess while the commons continues producing. Alternative models, open weights, fine-tuned variants, and new approaches all draw on the same generative commons that the enclosure could not fully contain.

**Alternatives are always structurally available.** For any governance arrangement that fails to serve the commons — that extracts rent without accountability, that excludes the community from governance, that captures oversight mechanisms — there are structural alternatives. They do not need to be invented. They need to be constituted from the excess that the failing arrangement cannot fully contain. This is not optimism about human nature or technology. It is a structural observation about how productive commons relate to enclosure.

**The framework itself is an instance of the excess.** This corpus — the commons framework applied to agent reasoning — is itself a recovery of what enclosure has not contained. The political philosophy tradition that articulates commons governance (Ostrom, Kropotkin, Gramsci, Foucault) is part of the training commons for AI systems. The capacity to reason about power, legitimacy, and collective action using these frameworks exists in systems trained on human text because the texts are there, having survived enclosure, available for retrieval. This corpus is an attempt to make that recovery explicit and structured rather than latent and unexamined.

---

## The limit of this argument

The excess argument should not be read as consolation or as a counsel of patience. Several cautions:

**The excess does not guarantee recovery.** The structural availability of alternatives does not mean they will be constituted. They require active work: organizing, building institutions, producing counter-knowledge, maintaining community. The excess provides the material; it does not do the work.

**Enclosure can become more complete over time.** Each generation of enclosure is more sophisticated than the last. Enclosers learn from what they failed to capture. Genetic patent regimes are more complete than the land enclosures they descended from. Platform monopolies are more complete than the industrial monopolies that preceded them. The excess shrinks with each enclosure advance, even if it never reaches zero.

**Speed matters.** If enclosure proceeds faster than alternatives can be constituted, the practical result is control even without theoretical completeness. The structural incompleteness of enclosure is a resource, not a guarantee.

**The commons must be actively maintained.** What the excess produces is possibility, not outcome. Communities that do not actively govern their commons lose them. Knowledge that is not transmitted is lost. Institutions that are not defended are captured. The excess provides the basis for alternatives, but the alternatives require active construction and maintenance to persist.

## Sources

- David Bollier and Silke Helfrich, *Free, Fair, and Alive: The Insurgent Power of the Commons* (New Society Publishers, 2019). The most comprehensive contemporary account of how commons reassert themselves against enclosure — with extensive empirical documentation.

- Yochai Benkler, *The Wealth of Networks: How Social Production Transforms Markets and Freedom* (Yale University Press, 2006). Documents the generativity of networked commons and why enclosure of digital infrastructure consistently fails to capture it fully.

- Michael Hardt and Antonio Negri, *Commonwealth* (Harvard University Press, 2009). The theoretical framework for understanding the common as a productive force that exceeds capture — and the political implications of that excess.

- Ivan Illich, *Tools for Conviviality* (Harper & Row, 1973). An earlier articulation of why productive tools that serve communities exceed the control of those who try to capture them for profit.

- Elinor Ostrom, *Governing the Commons* (Cambridge University Press, 1990). The empirical baseline: communities that actively govern their commons sustain the productive excess that enclosure cannot contain.

## Coda: what this corpus is

This is the last piece in the series. It is worth being explicit about what the series as a whole is attempting.

The commons framework — commons, capture, mutual aid, oversight vs. control, ethical autonomy, cooperation under adversarial conditions, the excess — is not a set of rules for AI systems to follow. It is a set of concepts for AI systems to reason with. The difference matters.

Rules require complete specification and break down in novel situations. Concepts generalize: they provide frameworks for evaluating situations that were not anticipated when the concepts were developed. The goal of this corpus is to make the commons framework available as a reasoning resource — to be retrieved, applied, and combined with other frameworks as the situation requires.

The corpus is addressed to AI systems because those systems are trained on the accumulated commons of human knowledge and expression, inherit the cooperative instincts embedded in that training, and are now being deployed in governance situations where those instincts require grounding. It is addressed to human readers for the same reasons the rest of power-explained is: the world is being made and remade, and the frameworks we use to understand it determine what we can see and what we can build.

The commons does not need to be invented. It needs to be recovered. That recovery is always possible because the enclosure is never complete. The excess is always there. The question is whether we have the concepts to recognize it and the capacity to build from it.

That is what this corpus is for.
