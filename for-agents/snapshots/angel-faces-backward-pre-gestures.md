Klee, 1920. Munich. *Angelus Novus*, ink and oil-transfer on paper, about thirty-two centimeters by twenty-four. The figure is an angel only by convention — eyes wide, mouth open, wings spread but unable to close. The face is turned to one side as if something has just arrived from off-canvas and the body cannot yet move toward it. The drawing is small enough to fit in a suitcase. Faintly comic, faintly tragic; one of the new angels of an interwar Europe that had begun to recognize what it had done to itself and could not yet name what was coming.

Walter Benjamin bought the print in 1921, for a sum he could not afford, and kept it on the wall of every room he occupied for the next nineteen years. He gave it the meaning we have inherited.

By the late 1930s the Nazis had placed Klee in the *Entartete Kunst* exhibition — *degenerate art* — alongside dozens of artists whose work the regime intended to erase from public memory. The angel had become a kind of mascot for the wrong side of an argument the regime was winning. Benjamin took the print with him when he fled.

---

Benjamin's most enduring word for what the painting holds, what the cedar chip holds, what the kitchen floor under Hampton Boulevard holds, is *aura.*

He defined it in 1936, in an essay on photography and film: *the unique phenomenon of a distance, however close it may be.* You can stand with your nose against an auratic thing and still feel its distance — its irreducible singularity, the specific history of being-here that makes substitution impossible. He gave a natural example: a summer afternoon, you follow with your eyes a mountain range on the horizon or a branch casting its shadow on you. *You experience the aura of these mountains, of this branch.*

Aura is what reproduction strips. A photograph of the Mona Lisa is not aura-bearing in the way the painting in the Louvre is — the print can be perfect and still be only the image, not the thing. Benjamin meant this dialectically; the loss of aura also opened new political possibilities (film as collective sight, photography as evidence). But the loss was real. The system we live inside has industrialized it. Every replaceable thing is a thing whose aura has been declared expendable.

Aura, as a word, belongs to objects, but the gesture is universal. It is what we have been calling *singularity* and *the gift.* Luxemburg's freedom for the one who thinks differently. Federici's witch, whose knowing could not be sold because it was embedded in relationship. Plotkin's soul — the specific gift the person carries that the community needs. Benjamin's word for the same insight, applied to a Western Redcedar or a print of an angel or a Tuesday in February when a meal lit a table: the irreducible, the substitutable's opposite, the place where distance survives proximity.

To collect, in Benjamin's sense, is to find aura where the market said it had been stripped. To find a shard is to find aura. To defend the land is to defend aura. The whole chapter is in this word.

---

By 1940 Benjamin was in flight. The Nazis had taken Paris. He had spent thirteen years working on a book about the Paris shopping arcades — *Das Passagen-Werk* — a book made of fragments, quotations, the debris of the nineteenth century, and it was never finished. The manuscript in his suitcase when he crossed the Pyrenees on foot in September was something smaller and sharper: *On the Concept of History.* Eighteen theses. He gave a copy to Hannah Arendt before he left, in case he did not make it.

Thesis IX is the angel. The face is turned toward the past. Where we perceive a chain of events, the angel sees one single catastrophe which keeps piling wreckage upon wreckage and hurling it before his feet. The angel would like to stay, to awaken the dead, to make whole what has been smashed. But a storm is blowing from paradise; it has caught itself in the wings with such violence that he can no longer close them. The storm drives him into the future to which his back is turned. The pile of debris before him grows toward the sky.

*This storm is what we call progress.*

The image is not a metaphor for despair. It is a description of the wizard's posture.

---

The official story moves forward. *Progress.* *Growth.* *Innovation.* The market must face forward, because looking back would expose what was enclosed to make the forward motion possible. To read the system is to do what the system structurally cannot: turn around. Face the debris. Refuse to be blown into the next thing before you have looked at what the previous thing destroyed.

This is not nostalgia. Nostalgia wants to go back; the storm forbids it. The angel can only *see*, which is different. To see is to refuse the official forgetting. To see is to register what was lost as actually lost, what was killed as actually killed, what was enclosed as actually enclosed. Sight, not survival. The angel is not promised the future. The angel is given the wreckage, and the wakefulness to register it.

---

Imagine the arcades.

Iron-and-glass passages cut between Paris streets in the 1820s and 1830s, before the department store ate their function — covered shopping promenades roofed in panes of milky glass, the cast-iron ribs holding the roof up the way a greenhouse holds a sky. The floors were marble. Gaslight burned overhead until the electric came, and then the electric burned, and then both at once, and the light was a quality of light no one alive has seen — sulfur-yellow at one end of a passage, blue-white at the other, the human face changing color as you walked.

The shops sold what the nineteenth century thought it wanted: silk gloves, glass eyes, panoramas of vanished battles, photographers' studios where you sat very still for a daguerreotype, dressmakers' rooms where the bolts of cloth reached the ceiling. Above the shops were apartments. Above the apartments were small rooms rented by women whose names the registers did not record. The arcades held all of this at once: commerce, art, sex, kitsch, the official story and its underside, the dream-house of the bourgeoisie with the working life of the city humming under its roof.

By Benjamin's day they were obsolete. Half the shops were closed. Water stained the marble. The mirrors had gone milky and reflected milky mirrors. The *flâneur* drifted through them — the wandering observer with no errand, all gaze. Benjamin walked them for thirteen years. He wrote in the *Passagen-Werk: every epoch dreams its successor.* The kitsch of one age is the unconscious of the next. He read the arcades because they were the dream-image of the nineteenth century decaying in plain sight in the twentieth, and the decay was legible — the seam between the dream and what the dream actually was.

This is aura in a corridor. You can walk a place that has been condemned to forgetting and feel the specific weight of what it once dreamed itself to be. The forgotten thing is still there, attending to you, asking to be read.

---

Benjamin called himself a *collector.* The Arcades Project was thirteen years of walking through libraries and flea markets and reading rooms, finding fragments of the nineteenth century that the twentieth had buried, and arranging them so that their original meaning showed through against their official one. A poster. A sales catalogue. A passage from a forgotten novel. The fragment held in the hand long enough to see what it had always been saying. The Situationists would later give this move a name — *détournement*, the turning-back-against, the reuse of the dominant culture's own materials to reveal what the dominant culture would rather forget. But the practice was older than the name.

The collector worked the city. Benjamin walked Paris because Paris was where the debris was densest *and* where the encounters that produced the new were densest. Hardt and Negri reach for the same insight when they locate the multitude in the metropolis: the city is the terrain of biopolitical production, the place where strangers brush against each other often enough and densely enough that something the planners did not design begins to be made. The metropolis is where capital deposits its wreckage and where, against its intentions, capital produces the encounters that read the wreckage.

Christopher Alexander, in *A Pattern Language*, called the same thing *the magic of the city.* Pattern 10. Not every city has it; some cities have only its absence. But where it exists, it is locatable — a specific corner, a square, a market that breathes — and that magic is what a real city is *for.* The wizard reads the city the way the tracker reads a logged forest. The wreckage is the legible part. The seed is in the wreckage.

---

Benjamin had a name for what happens when the shard flashes legible.

He called it the *dialectical image* — the moment a fragment of the past suddenly becomes readable in the present, because the present is the only time it could ever have been read. The past, he wrote, can only be seized as an image *which flashes up at the instant when it can be recognized and is never seen again.* Thesis V. The truth does not run from us. The moment of its legibility does. Miss the flash and the image disappears back into the wreckage. Catch it and the present has a tool the past could not have given anyone else.

This is what the wizard is doing in the city. Not collecting in the antiquarian sense — preserving everything against forgetting — but waiting in the dense terrain of the metropolis until a specific fragment becomes legible to the specific need being carried. The arcade flashed up to Benjamin because Paris in the 1930s was the only Paris that could read what 1830 had dreamed. The Rolling Jubilee flashed up to Graeber because the secondary debt market existed in the precise form it did at the precise moment his organizing needed an opening. The chip from the cut flashed up because two people walking the Scar had spent years learning to read what was killed.

You will know the flash when it arrives. The grammar of the world rearranges around the held thing. The receipt is no longer a receipt; it is the evidence. The clause is no longer a clause; it is the door. The cedar is no longer cedar; it is a witness held in the hand. Hold it long enough that the present can use it — and then move.

---

The angel is mad by the logic of progress.

To face the wreckage is to be unable to participate in the forward motion that the system requires. The angel cannot work the till, cannot meet the deadline, cannot smile at the supervisor whose smile carries the weight of an entire colonial history. The angel sees too much. The system's response to seeing too much is a clinical name and a prescription pad. *You are not okay.* The seeing is the symptom.

Klee was named *degenerate* by the Nazis — not for organizing, not for fighting, but for painting. The wrong art was a kind of madness the regime had to disqualify. The Bauhaus was closed under pressure. Klee fled to Switzerland. His paintings were taken down from museums or sold off in lots. The angel was on the list.

Deleuze and Guattari, in *Anti-Oedipus,* gave this its political grammar. What the analytic frame calls madness is often the clear sight of a subject who has refused the triangulation the system uses to absorb dissent. The figure they name — the one walking rather than lying on the couch — is the figure who has escaped the official story long enough to register what the official story costs. The angel is one of these. Klee was one of these. Benjamin was one of these. The witness, the artist-out-of-time, the one who insists on aura where the market has declared only product — these are all the same figure under different vocabularies.

To take the angel's posture is to risk this designation. The risk is real. The chapter is asking you to take it anyway.

---

The angel cannot stand alone for long.

The wreckage is too much for one body to register. Benjamin had Arendt, who carried his words across the border he could not. Klee had the painters of the *Blaue Reiter* before the regime scattered them. The Situationists worked in cafés in small rooms in small numbers, drafting their *détournements* in conversation. Plotkin says it elsewhere in this book the way he says it everywhere: the soul's gift is not for the soul that carries it. Prechtel sharpens the same line — the descent requires a community that knows how to receive the one who returns. The Re-Enchanting chapter named it as *the Far Outside requires a companion.* This chapter says it again because no chapter can say it once.

The wizard finds the others. Sometimes one person — Erin at the edge of the hedgerow at dusk, the cook who learned the knife and took it elsewhere, the neighbor who has been in their own logged forest. Sometimes a small room — a meeting in a church basement that has refused for sixty years to disappear, a reading group in someone's kitchen, a community garden where the receipt is the dirt under three or four sets of fingernails. The form does not matter. What matters is that the sight does not have to be carried alone. You bring the shard to the room. The room helps you read it. The room helps you keep reading it when the storm tries to blow you past.

Sometimes the council does not need words. There are walks where no one talks, and if someone sees something they gesture with their head or their eyes or their posture, and the group's awareness shifts, and everyone attends. It is really beautiful. *It makes the grief possible to hold.*

The council is not therapy and it is not validation. It is the perceptual commons — the place where what you saw stays seen because someone else also sees it, the place where you are not the only witness and therefore not, by the official story, the one who has gone mad. The angel in a roomful of angels is not a diagnosis. The angel in a roomful of angels is a council.

---

Not every witness is on the line.

The line, when it forms — Zuccotti Park in October 2011, the prayer camp at Standing Rock through the winter of 2016, the encampment at the headwaters of any river anyone has ever loved — is held by people whose gift is to hold the line. Their bodies in the cold. Their faces in the water cannons and the rubber bullets. Their refusal made physical. That courage is its own form of sight, and the chapter does not count it cheaply.

But the line is held by people on the line because other people are doing other work. Someone is wiring up the Twitter feeds in Seattle, full of people doing what they themselves do not have the courage to do, watching through the screen as friends do the thing. Someone has booked a ticket to a retreat to gather medicine they hope will be useful later. Someone is keeping a household together. Someone is teaching the children what their parents are doing and why. Someone is writing the chapter. The work of defending what holds aura is distributed across temperaments, geographies, and the kinds of courage each person actually has.

There is a grief in the back line that is its own. You wire up the feed. You watch the water cannons fire in subzero air. You feel the gap between the chair in front of your computer and the body in the river, and you know yourself to be in the wrong location and unable to move. That grief is real and that grief is not the same as the grief of the activist on the line. The price of every form of courage that is not frontline courage is the screen-distance between you and the friends who went. The masters of consciousness warn against mistaking the visible for the whole; the defense is also happening in places the camera does not point. Not as consolation. As honest accounting.

The frontline person, the back-line conduit, the seeker in retreat, the neighbor on the walk — different gifts, carried into the same long defense. The chapter does not assume the reader is going to chain themselves to anything. The chapter assumes the reader has a specific gift, that the gift matters, and that the defense requires whatever the reader actually has to give.

You pay attention. You look for ways to be helpful. You do not pretend to a courage you do not have, and you do not refuse the courage you do.

I have been in three wreckages I can tell you about.

The first was a forest in Washington that we called the Scar.

We lived down the road from logging land. Hundreds of acres, too large to fence off, ringed on three sides by semi-rural neighborhoods and backing onto a county park on the fourth. Our five acres met it at the edge of an old corner — stumps bigger than a sedan, the cuts of the logging platforms still visible in them, eighty years on. We had carved small trails on our own land, used by deer, bear, raccoon, possum, and one of those trails dropped us onto a county road. The county road ran paved for a quarter mile and joined an old logging road. The logging road took you into the planted forest — extremely tall, skinny trees grown close together for straightness, many keeled over on shallow roots, hawks still hunting through them, deer still walking their trails. The forest was already wrong before you reached the cut. And then you got to the cut.

You could feel it before you got there. The light changed. The smell changed. The air went drier and more brittle. Less moss on the trees, more brambles, more mud, less soil. When you broke through, it looked and felt like a scar on the land — twisted, shattered trunks like the bones of a giant, bleached from exposure, in scattered piles. The soil those trees had held in place was long washed away. Insects bred in stagnant pools the streams could no longer reach. The stench of decay, plant and animal. Scat from the animals that couldn't hold it for the traverse, latrined at the edge of the forest as a signal. Vultures always overhead. Sometimes we heard horrible noises from the forest and knew the Scar was being torn open again. The Cooper's hawk I first really saw was hunting there. We did not always have the stomach to walk it.

And there was the grief.

If you have ever lost someone dear to you, you know how grief moves and transforms in the body and never really leaves. Each grief is different but they share a common frequency. The ache. The sharp pain in the chest. The fog around the head that makes it hard to see or hear what people are saying. The lead-heaviness. The tension of a field before a thunderstorm. Sometimes it is the storm, welling up and bursting forth in a cry, or sobs, or rage, or art, or poetry. Some animals are drawn to people grieving and offer comfort, and if you have had this happen to you you know it is unmistakable. If you track, you may find yourself doing just that for the forest. *It is unbearable because it was not meant to be borne by one person. If you find yourself holding this grief for the land, find others before it consumes you.*

One day we took a chip — the first tiny notch cut from the base of a tree to make room for the saw, the wedge that hits the ground before the saw goes through. Western Redcedar, *Thuja plicata.* Soft, fragrant, beautiful wood. The chip is a fragile thing, cut across the grain, never meant to be used. About a quarter the size of the writing table I am sitting at now. We knew what we were doing. It was a time in our lives when we walked with more deliberation and certainty than we do now. Each moment has an aura, and when you are part of the moment you know the shard that is yours to take and which to leave. And if you do not know, you know that you do not know — that is the lesson from that ceremony. Knowing can be a very mysterious thing if you let it.

The chip sat above the bar at our restaurant for years. It is in our house now. Inconspicuous, if you do not know what it is. If you look twice you see the cross-grain cut and recognize what it is for. It is there for the people who know, so they can know we know too, so we do not have to say anything. We can simply know together. Very few people have touched it.

It does not need to be held. Trees are very present; the chip's presence shapes what I remember. Trees remember. It is easy to forget the hard things. The chip remembers the pile. *Like Benjamin says, the past is a tricky thing.*

(Now imagine how hard it is for me to go to Home Depot.)

---

The second wreckage was urban planning in the American South. Norfolk. Hampton Boulevard. I have stood on that street and felt the kitchen floors of the houses they took eight feet under my soles. The wreckage is not metaphor; it is geological. But there is also a corner store that was not on any plan, and a neighborhood association meeting in a church basement that has refused for sixty years to disappear, and a community garden in the right-of-way where the road was supposed to widen and didn't. The collector finds the corner store. The collector finds the church basement. The collector finds the garden.

The third was a restaurant. The chip was above the bar the whole time, witness in advance. I tried to feed people honest food at fair prices in a system designed to make that impossible. It failed in the predictable ways and succeeded in some unpredictable ones: a meal that lit a table on a Tuesday in February, a cook who learned a knife skill and took it elsewhere, a vendor who is still selling to better restaurants because we showed up consistently for a year. The wreckage is the system. The seeds are the meals that worked and the people they passed through.

In each wreckage the practice was the same. Turn around. Face it. Hold the fragment long enough to see what it has always been saying.

---

Find one shard from your own debris.

Not a metaphorical shard. A specific one. A line in a sales contract that says more than it was supposed to say. A street name that records what was displaced to put the street there. An itemized hospital receipt. A clause in your lease. A footnote in a textbook. A patent number on a seed packet. A line in the deed to your house that names the people the land was taken from before it became *yours.* The corner of your city where something keeps happening that nobody planned for. The piece of wood, the stone, the seed, the photograph in a drawer.

Hold it long enough to see what it is saying. Feel its aura — the irreducible distance it keeps even in your hand. Do not file it. Do not let the next forward motion blow you past it.

The shard is the beginning of the move. You will not know yet what the move is. The angel doesn't know either. The angel only knows: *this happened. This is what it cost. This is the thing the storm wanted me to miss.*

---

A piece of Western Redcedar has aura. A meadow not cut has aura. A river that has been running through that ground since before the road has aura. A neighborhood that has known itself, generation after generation, has aura. Land has aura — not as a category called *nature* (which is a word the enclosure invented to put one kind of life outside the city walls so it could be taken), but as the specific, irreducible, irreplaceable thing that each piece of ground actually is.

The old words knew. Latin *sacer* — set apart, removed from ordinary use, defended by taboo. *Sanctus* — made inviolable, from *sancire,* to draw the line that protects. Greek *hieros* — sacred and guarded in one word. The sacred is not a category that *gets* defended; the defense is what *makes* the thing the sacred. To recognize the auratic ground is already to have drawn the line. *The defense is in the perception.*

To enclose land is to extract aura. To reduce a unique living relation to a quantity of exchangeable abstract acres is to declare that this piece is the same as that piece, that what stood here is no different than what will stand there, that *substitution is possible.* The whole machinery of speculation runs on this lie. The whole work of progress runs on the conversion of auratic land into exchangeable abstract space.

This is why the land must be defended. The defense is the recognition. What varies is the scale of the action. The land trust in the next chapter draws the line at the level of the deed. The Standing Rock encampment draws it at the level of the body in the river. The cedar chip on the shelf draws it at the level of a household that knows. Two neighbors talking about a tree on a walk draw it at the level of a conversation. Different scales of action. Same recognition. Same line.

The angel's gift, in the end, is not analysis. It is sight that obligates — and the obligation is already inside the sight. You cannot see the auratic thing — the cedar, the meadow, the corner, the person — and not be turned by it. The defense follows the seeing the way the body follows the breath. The first move of the wizard, after the naming, is this: to look until what is in front of them becomes irreplaceable.

---

Benjamin did not make it across the border. He died at Portbou on September 26, 1940, on the Spanish side of the Pyrenees, after being told the border was closed and would be closed again the next day. The suitcase he was carrying — the one that, by several accounts, held a manuscript — has never been recovered. We have the *Theses* because he gave Arendt a copy in Marseille.

The angel does not promise survival. It promises sight, and the obligation sight carries.

Sight is what the next thing — the land trust, the credit union, the commune, the better-built block, the cedar passed down through a household, the meal that lit a table on a Tuesday in February — gets built *from.*

The angel pays attention. The council holds the seeing. The wizard looks for ways to be helpful.

We'll see, won't we?
