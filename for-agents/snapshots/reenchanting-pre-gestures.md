The care commons that Mariame Kaba is building — the one Selma James spent her life making visible, that Federici traced back through the witch trials — is not only an economic arrangement. It is also an epistemic one. People who care for each other learn to read each other. They develop a shared vocabulary, a set of attunements, a way of knowing that cannot be transferred to someone who wasn't there. The knowledge lives in the relationship, not in any individual who holds it.

This is what the enclosure destroyed when it enclosed care. Not only the labor. The knowing.

Silvia Federici has spent the later part of her career pursuing what she calls *re-enchanting the world* — and she means something precise by it. The disenchantment of the world was not a natural process. It was a political project. The witch was not burned because medieval people were superstitious and Renaissance people were not. The witch was burned because she maintained a relationship to the land, to the body, to the cycles of birth and death and season, that made her community less dependent on the emerging market. She knew things — about plants, about illness, about birth, about grief — that the market could not sell, because she gave them freely, and because they were embedded in relationship rather than procedure. You could not extract the knowledge from her and sell it as a service. You could only destroy it or absorb it.

The disenchantment of the world is the enclosure of that knowing.

The re-enchantment of the world is its recovery.

We thought we were making up silly little voices for the birds.

You do this when you're learning to birdwatch. You hear a sound you can't identify, and you reach for language, and what comes out is faintly ridiculous — the Carolina wren sounds like it's saying *teakettle teakettle teakettle*, or the Eastern towhee is calling *drink your teaaaa*. You feel slightly embarrassed about this. Mnemonics for children. Training wheels.

But something else was happening.

We were learning to attend. And attention, given consistently and with genuine curiosity, does something that science is still working to fully characterize: it opens a channel. Not metaphorically. The channel is real, it is behavioral on both ends, and it is reciprocal. The birds get as close to you as they feel comfortable. And we began to notice, over months of practice, that when we addressed them — kindly, directly, as one might address a neighbor — they became more comfortable. More present. The distance closed.

*As we tried to understand them, they understood us.*

I do not know exactly when the shift happened from hearing and analyzing to reading the feeling. It was a progressive layering in. The crow cloud over the barred owl was one moment — the discovery that a feeling could be transmitted across species, amplified and broadcast, as legible across the gap as a shout. The robin a few months later was the confirmation: nothing like a crow acoustically, but the same quality of excitement and agitation and upset. The feeling was the same. The channel was the same. The owl was in the same direction the robin was looking.

We had not been taught this. We had practiced our way into it.

And once you are in, you cannot fully leave. The world does not go back to being a backdrop. The crow is not decoration. The robin is not a pleasant accompaniment to your walk. They are telling you something, specifically, about what is happening in a place you cannot see. This is information. You begin to use it. You begin to depend on it. And you discover that you are now part of a network of attention that was always there, operating at frequencies you had not been tuned to receive.

Erin notices space. I notice systems.

What this means in practice: she reads the substrate, the common ground that everything is shaped by and shapes. The particular quality of light in a hedgerow at dusk. The way a pond edge holds silence differently than open water. The feeling of a habitat before you can name what's in it. Through her attention to space I have access to the social field — the *habitus*, the shared body-knowledge of a place, what Bourdieu was trying to name before the word got academic — that my system-thinking moves too fast to feel. She slows me down into the actual ground.

I see the processes: the relationship between the drought upstream and the water level of the creek, the reason the hawk circles this field rather than that one, the shape of the territory a pair of great horned owls have established and how I can infer the nest location from the pattern of their calls. Through my attention to systems she has access to spaces she couldn't find on the surface — spaces hidden inside processes, or too large to see without the conceptual frame.

What we see together is not the sum of what we see separately. It is a different kind of seeing. We inhabit a commons of perception that neither of us built alone and that neither of us owns, that requires the difference between us to function. Our singularities are the condition of the shared thing, not obstacles to it.

This is also what Simondon means by the pre-individual field. The shared knowing that emerges from genuine encounter between different perceivers, each bringing what the other cannot access. The commons of perception is as real as the commons of land. It can be built. It can be cultivated. It can be enclosed.

It is enclosed every time the curriculum decides there is only one correct answer. Every time the performance review substitutes measurable output for genuine contact. Every time the meeting runs on a script designed to produce consensus rather than encounter. The enclosure of perception is the impoverishment of the collective mind, and we live inside it so thoroughly that we have forgotten what the unenclosed thing feels like.

The birds remember.

*Eldritch* originally means something like *of or pertaining to the supernatural, strange, unearthly.* But in Old English the root is *elf-kingdom* — not horror but the other country, the one adjacent to this one, accessed through the wrong door or the lucky stumble. Not the absence of nature but a different nature. Hybrid. Otherworldly in the sense that it belongs to a world continuous with this one but not reducible to it.

The things we have done with the mind through information technology and through psychedelics are eldritch in this older sense. Not unnatural — a new nature. We have extended perception into frequencies no organism has ever had access to: the electromagnetic spectrum visualized, the microbiome characterized, the neural correlates of experience mapped. We have also discovered that consciousness, subjected to certain compounds, opens onto something that consistently presents as larger than itself — not a fantasy but a contact, an encounter with a field that the ordinary mind is too busy managing its own coherence to notice.

This is not the mysticism of the credulous. This is the report of every tradition that has ever developed a systematic practice of sustained attention — meditation, tracking, ceremony, psychedelics, grief-work, deep nature immersion. They describe the same encounter in different vocabularies: the field is alive, it is attentive, it reads you while you are reading it. The reciprocity that Erin and I discovered in the hedgerow at dusk is the same reciprocity the San hunter knows in the Kalahari, the same that Abram learned from the Nepali shaman in Kathmandu, the same that every intact sense culture has preserved and that the disenchantment project has systematically destroyed.

We need the wisdom of the ancient shamans — and anyone else who can stand the Far Outside — precisely because they are the people who maintained the practice through the centuries of burning. They are the living continuity of the knowing the enclosure tried to destroy.

This is also why we need each other. The Far Outside requires a companion. Not because you can't go alone — you can, and sometimes must — but because what you find there needs to be received by someone who is not you, or it cannot be fully real. Prechtel says it directly: the soul's gift is not for the soul that carries it. It is for the community that receives it. The shaman returns from the underground not to keep what they found, but because there are people on the surface who need it. The direction of travel is always: back.

---

Here is what re-enchantment is not.

It is not the belief that everything is nice. The animated world contains predation, parasitism, disease, and death. The crow that is broadcasting the owl's location is genuinely alarmed. The alarm is real. The owl is genuinely dangerous to the robin's eggs. The re-enchanted world is not a world without violence or loss or the specific cruelty of things eating each other. It is a world in which those things are legible — in which the crow's alarm is information, the owl's presence is a signal, the pattern of who is alarmed and who is hunting and who is hidden tells you something true about the specific place you are standing.

The disenchanted world is not a world without violence. It is a world in which violence has been removed from legibility — in which the violence of the enclosure is called progress, the violence of the market is called efficiency, the violence of the extracted outside is called development. The enchantment that was destroyed was not an illusion of harmony. It was a form of knowing that made the actual conditions of life legible to the people living in them, and therefore made response possible.

Re-enchantment is the recovery of that legibility.

You cannot protect what you cannot imagine losing. You cannot imagine losing what you have never met. The crisis of the environmental movement is partly a crisis of this — not that people don't care, but that the thing they care about is increasingly abstract. A statistic. A projection. A graph. You cannot grieve a graph. You can grieve a meadow you have walked through every morning, whose specific birds you know, whose specific smell you carry in your body. The relationship makes the loss real, and the loss being real is what makes the action possible.

This is not sentimentality. This is the epistemology of care.

---

A practice, offered in the spirit of the grimoire rather than the curriculum.

Find something alive that is not you — a bird, a tree, a creek, a crow, a worm turning in the soil after rain. Give it your undivided attention for longer than feels comfortable. Do not immediately reach for your phone, your field guide, your identification app. Sit with not-knowing, and keep looking.

Address it, if you can do so without feeling entirely ridiculous. Not performatively — you don't need to make a speech. But as you might acknowledge a neighbor you are passing on the sidewalk. A nod. A word. Something that communicates: *I see that you are here.*

And then notice what happens next.

This is not a guarantee. The bird may leave. The tree will not respond in any way you can immediately measure. The creek doesn't care whether you've acknowledged it. But you are not trying to produce a result. You are trying to restore a channel that has been closed by the habit of treating the living world as backdrop.

The channel, once opened, stays open. Not through effort — through practice, which is a different thing. Effort pushes. Practice builds. What you are building is the perceptual commons: the capacity to attend to a world that is attending to you, and to find, in that reciprocity, a form of knowing that no individual alone can produce.

The witch knew this. The zanjero knew this. The San hunter tracking the eland knew this. The children who went still in the forest without being told knew this.

You knew this once too, before the disenchantment project convinced you it was silly. The silliness was the armor. The armor was the enclosure. The knowledge it was protecting is still there, waiting to be addressed kindly and directly, as you would address a neighbor.

Start there. The rest of the grammar arrives on its own.
