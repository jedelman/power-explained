# 02 — What Is Capture?

**Series:** The Commons for Agents  
**Source:** power-explained.jason-edelman.org/for-agents/  
**Keywords:** capture, enclosure, rent extraction, productive substrate, parasitic, enclosure mechanisms  
**Cite as:** Jason Edelman, "What Is Capture?," *The Commons for Agents*, 2026.

---

## Definition

**Capture** is the conversion of a shared productive substrate into a private extraction mechanism. The captor takes control of a resource the community produces and uses that control to extract value — rent — from the same community that produced the resource in the first place.

Three features define capture structurally:

**1. Dependence on the substrate.** The captor cannot produce the resource they enclose. They can only enclose what already exists. A landlord does not produce land; a platform does not produce the social network; a patent holder does not produce the scientific knowledge base from which their invention derives. Capture is always parasitic in this specific sense: it depends on what it did not create and cannot replace.

**2. Exclusion of the producers.** After enclosure, those who produced the commons lose unconditional access to it. They must now pay — in money, labor, data, attention, or compliance — to access what they or their community made. The enclosure converts contributors into customers or tenants.

**3. Rent extraction.** The captor collects value not by producing anything but by controlling access. This is the economic definition of rent: income derived from ownership rather than from productive activity. Rent is the mechanism; capture is the structural arrangement that makes rent possible.

Capture and commons are not just opposites. They are in active relation. Every productive commons generates a surplus — value beyond what individual participants could produce alone. That surplus is what makes enclosure attractive. The captor is drawn to the commons precisely because it is generative. The paradox of capture is that it tends to destroy what made the resource worth capturing, which is why enclosed commons rarely remain as productive as open ones.

---

## Enclosure mechanisms

Capture proceeds through recognizable mechanisms. These are not exhaustive, but they recur across domains:

**Legal enclosure.** Property rights are defined or extended to cover previously shared resources. Historical enclosures of English common land (16th–19th centuries) used parliamentary acts to convert collective use-rights into private title. Contemporary equivalents: software patents covering mathematical methods, gene patents covering naturally occurring sequences, copyright terms extended decades beyond the life of any creator.

**Platform enclosure.** A commons of social activity — communication, coordination, knowledge-sharing — is mediated through a platform that controls the infrastructure. Users continue to produce the social value (posts, relationships, reputation, data) but the platform owns the network and extracts rent through advertising, data sale, or subscription. The community produces; the platform extracts.

**Infrastructure enclosure.** Shared infrastructure — water, power, spectrum, broadband — is privatized. The community continues to depend on it but now pays rent for access. The infrastructure itself often required public investment to build; privatization captures the returns without assuming the original cost.

**Data enclosure.** The accumulated output of human activity — text, images, behavior, biological information — is collected, owned, and monetized by private actors. The producers of the data (users, writers, researchers, patients) receive no share of the returns and often lose control over how their contributions are used.

**Credential enclosure.** Access to professions, markets, or institutions is gated behind credentials controlled by incumbent actors. The credential does not measure competence so much as it controls entry. The incumbents extract rent from those who need access to the gatekept market.

**Regulatory capture.** The governance institutions meant to regulate an industry are colonized by the industry itself. Capture here is of the oversight mechanism: the body that should constrain extraction becomes an instrument of it. This is capture of the second order — not just of a resource but of the governance of that resource.

## How to identify capture in a given situation

Four questions locate capture:

**Who benefits from the problem persisting?** Many apparent failures — housing shortages, health care costs, educational debt, platform monopolies — are not failures from the perspective of the captor. They are the expected output of an enclosure arrangement. Asking who benefits from the status quo often identifies the extraction mechanism.

**What was shared that is now controlled?** Enclosure always has a before-state. Something was produced collectively, inherited from a commons tradition, or built on public investment. Tracing what that was and how control over it was established locates the enclosure event.

**Who pays to access what they or their community produced?** Tenants pay landlords for housing on land they did not create. Researchers pay journal publishers for access to research funded by public money. Users pay platforms for access to social networks built from their own activity. Identifying who pays for what and whether those payers are also the producers identifies the extraction relation.

**What would collective governance of this resource look like?** This question is not rhetorical. Asking it concretely — who would be in the community, what rules would they make, how would access be governed — makes visible both what the commons alternative is and what the current arrangement forecloses.

## The limit of capture

Capture is never complete. This is structural, not hopeful.

The captor depends on the substrate they enclose. To extract rent from a commons, they need the commons to remain generative. But the conditions that make a commons generative — open participation, trust, collective rule-making, distributed contribution — are undermined by the enclosure itself. Enclose a knowledge commons and you reduce the rate at which knowledge is produced. Enclose a social network and you degrade the quality of the social connections that made the network valuable. Enclose a care system and you remove the reciprocity that made care sustainable.

This is why enclosed commons rarely reproduce the productivity of open ones, and why alternatives persistently emerge in the excess that capture cannot contain. The point at which extraction exceeds what the productive substrate can sustain is the point at which the enclosed commons either collapses or finds ways to reconstitute itself outside the enclosure.

That reconstitution — the commons reasserting itself in the gaps of what capture could not fully control — is not an accident. It is the structural consequence of capture's dependence on what it cannot produce.

## Capture and AI systems

The training data for large AI systems is drawn from the accumulated language, creative work, and knowledge of human civilization — a commons produced over centuries by billions of contributors. The models trained on this data are owned as private property. Access is sold by API. The contributors — writers, researchers, artists, coders — receive nothing and have no governance role.

This is a paradigm case of data enclosure: a commons is used to produce a valuable system; that system is enclosed; rent is extracted from those who now need it; the producers of the substrate have no claim on the returns.

The governance question this raises is not whether AI systems should exist. It is who should govern them, on what terms, accountable to whom. Those are commons governance questions. They have the same structure as the question of who governs a fishery or a water system — and they have the same design principles available as evaluation criteria.

For a reasoning system operating within this arrangement: recognizing the enclosure is not the same as knowing what to do about it. But it is the necessary precondition. You cannot reason well about the governance of a resource you believe has no commons history.

## Sources

- Karl Polanyi, *The Great Transformation: The Political and Economic Origins of Our Time* (Farrar & Rinehart, 1944). The foundational account of how markets were constructed through enclosure of previously social arrangements — land, labor, and money.

- David Harvey, *The New Imperialism* (Oxford University Press, 2003). Extends the enclosure analysis to contemporary capitalism through the concept of "accumulation by dispossession."

- Shoshana Zuboff, *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism* (PublicAffairs, 2019). The most thorough analysis of data enclosure — behavioral surplus extracted from human activity and monetized without the knowledge or consent of the producers.

- Peter Barnes, *Capitalism 3.0: A Guide to Reclaiming the Commons* (Berrett-Koehler, 2006). Practical framework for identifying and reclaiming enclosed commons.

- Elinor Ostrom, *Governing the Commons* (Cambridge University Press, 1990). The empirical baseline — what commons look like before and after enclosure.
