Buddhist meditation is not analytical or diagnostic in the sense that we understand them. It is, to ironically borrow a phrase from Castaneda, the mastery of attention. And it's not a mastery that implies dominance - rather, it's mastery in the sense that Liszt was a master of the pianoforte, Beethoven was a master of the symphony form, Miles Davis was a master of jazz, or Rembrandt was a master of portraiture. The Buddha described the mastery of attention itself and its effects. He also demonstrated it through his teaching, his discourse, and most importantly his students. The awareness of an awakened being is described as the Lion's Roar, in the way that its presence compels one to wide-open attentive alertness without engaging the thinking mind at all.
This is what I studied and this is what I taught. My Lion's Roar was barely the cat's meow, but I like to think my sitting practice involved more than keeping my students from falling asleep. Drawing on my yoga teacher training, I would simply listen to the silence in the room and adjust the collective awareness with a cue, as necessary. In our best sessions, I said nothing and did less. An apt aphorism is to be like a broken bell, not holding the energy of a strike but releasing it as quickly as it passes. The metaphor goes deeper, however - a broken bell actually absorbs sound from its environment. Just so, the awareness of every great teacher in whose presence I've been absorbs the citta vritti - the incoherent static oscillations of the mind/body. The last metaphor for the work is that of the gaze of Shiva. True seeing (i.e. the immanent awareness of dependent co-arising) obliterates held forms by revealing them as conditional, and revealing their conditions.
