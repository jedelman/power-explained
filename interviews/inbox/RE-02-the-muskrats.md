And it's not a secret we can articulate, like 'how to see a muskrat'.
It's far more complex than that. We just notice that now we can see them.

> The muskrats are the chapter's best example of this and I didn't use them —
I didn't have enough to work with.

> Tell me the muskrat story properly.
Where, when, what you had read or learned just before,
the first sighting, and then what changed after.

The muskrats showing up wasn't planned. We had known they were there for years - we're trackers after all - but we just hadn't laid eyes on them. And then one day, when the water was low, I saw a sheen out of the corner of my eye, turned instinctively, and we saw a chunky little body shuffling and snuffling around amongst old oyster shells. The glossy sheen of its coat reminded me of otter but it was too small, round, and waddly. We knew immediately it was a muskrat. And we just stopped. Stopped our walk and our conversation - which was light enough to let in a muskrat anyway - and just blissfully enjoyed finally seeing this little creature in broad daylight. People with busy minds just walked past us; children followed our gaze, instinctively, as they do, and saw as well - but no one stopped to tell them what they were seeing and we know better than to approach other peoples' children unbidden. So we were just there for a moment, shining the lantern of our awareness on this frankly adorable muskrat, for probably a good 15 or 20 minutes. Time flows differently in the wild, and the wild is often not very far from you at all.
