There are a few coffee shops we frequent because they double as salons. If the right group of people at the right time are there and in the right mood, you'll get a cross-pollinating caffeinated frenzy of leftist (sometimes) theory or just the usual bar bullshit without the inebriation. I'm known in these circles as someone who reads theory, so I have a few friends that will check what I'm reading and follow up, or propose topics of their own. We'll riff when it's slow - they're often behind the bar - and keep conversations going over the course of months or years. It's been a really nourishing intellectual practice, if frustrating. But I prefer it to bars for the same reason the Viennese did. And I understand why the CNT advised against coffee - because it's very easy to get very excited about theory and not actually build anything.
At one point, as we were talking, some sort of meta-awareness kicked in. We weren't talking about production of subjectivity, we were talking about housing policy. We were mapping out how different factions held both different interests and different assumptions, but how they weren't symmetric; the assumptions that a faction held wasn't always in line with their interests. I've been trying to point out that that's what hegemony is to them, but for the first time I could see the way it was produced. It was a lot like the transition from seeing the track to seeing the presence of the foot making the track. In this case, I could see the jackboot.
I didn't interrupt the conversation - I let it flow. But I could hear between our words the autonomous processes, the interaction between experience and interpretation, that consistently reinforced the ruling class perspective - that the middle class and the Left have no political legitimacy or mandate to govern, but they have agency, so they must be blamed for all outcomes, meanwhile the Right has the mandate and no agency, so they can't be held responsible for any outcomes but are entitled to effect it. I saw it being made.
The next thing I attempted to do was to interrupt its production, introducing ideas and perspectives to friends that were incompatible with the hegemonic idea. But what I didn't expect was for those friends to shrug. Here I was with what seemed to me like a master key. We can produce our own subjectivity! We can use a combination of thought, action, and reflection to remake our historical situation at a small but expanding scale! How cool is that? And it just didn't land for anyone. I thought I was a bad communicator, but I realize that I was attempting to one-shot a task that properly takes generations. That's a big part of why I'm writing this book
and realized you had already become the person who knew this.
