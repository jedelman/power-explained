The Commons
Is the Ecology

Ecosystems don't collapse randomly. They collapse when the commons that maintained them gets enclosed. The inverse is also true — and that's the hopeful part.

For most of human history, forests were managed by the communities that lived in them. Fisheries were governed by the people who fished them. Water flowed under rules negotiated by the villages that drank from it. Not perfectly. Not without conflict. But sustainably — often for centuries, in living relationships with the ecosystems they depended on, because they depended on them.

Then came enclosure. The long process — beginning in England in the 15th century, accelerating globally through colonialism, continuing right now through the privatization of seeds and spectrum and water and genetic code — by which shared resources held in common were transferred into private ownership. Separated from the communities that maintained them. Transformed from relationships into assets. From places into commodities.

This is not a metaphor. It is the mechanism. Ecological collapse is, in most cases, what happens downstream of enclosure.

Why private ownership destroys what common ownership maintained

Here's the simple version: when a forest is held in common by the people who live around it, those people have a reason to maintain it that goes beyond this year's revenue. Their children will need it. Their water comes from it. Their identity is bound up in it. Commons governance typically includes rules about how much can be extracted, enforced by the community, because the community has a long-term stake in the forest's survival.

When the same forest is enclosed — sold to a timber company, claimed by a state, privatized by a developer — the new owner has a fundamentally different relationship to it. The incentive structure changes. Now the question is: what return does this asset generate, and on what timeline? If the answer is "maximum timber revenue in the next decade," the centuries of sustainable management that preceded private ownership don't survive the first financial quarter.

This isn't about bad people making bad decisions. It's about what different ownership structures produce. Private ownership of an ecosystem, by an entity whose obligations run to shareholders rather than to the ecosystem and the community that depends on it, predictably produces extraction and collapse. This is the design, not a bug.

Elinor Ostrom won the Nobel Prize
for proving what communities
had known for centuries.

In 2009, Elinor Ostrom became the first woman to win the Nobel Prize in Economics. Her life's work was a refutation of one of the most influential — and most wrong — ideas in modern economics: the "tragedy of the commons," which claimed that any shared resource would inevitably be destroyed by individual self-interest.

Ostrom spent decades studying real commons — Swiss alpine meadows managed for centuries, Japanese forests, irrigation systems in Spain and the Philippines, Maine lobster fisheries, Kenyan grazing lands. What she found was that communities routinely develop sophisticated governance systems for managing shared resources sustainably, that these systems work better than either privatization or state management in many contexts, and that the "tragedy" was not inevitable but was specifically produced by the breakdown of commons governance — often by enclosure. The tragedy of the commons isn't a natural law. It's what happens when you destroy the commons.

Rojava grew trees during a war

In the Autonomous Administration of North and East Syria — the Rojava experiment in democratic self-governance — ecology is written into the founding charter as a core principle alongside democracy and women's liberation. Not as a policy preference. As a structural commitment.

In practice: communal control of agricultural land, cooperative farming, and reforestation projects — maintained through years of active military conflict, economic blockade, and international isolation. While being bombed by Turkey and blockaded by multiple neighboring states, the communes were planting trees. Because the commons treats land as something to be maintained for the people who live on it, not extracted for whoever currently holds title to it.

The commons isn't the past — it's what people are building right now

The solarpunk imagination — the vision of a future that is lush rather than scorched, abundant rather than scarce, communal rather than isolated — is not fantasy. It's a description of what commons governance produces when it works. And people are building it, right now, in cities and towns and rural communities everywhere.

These aren't small things. They're early infrastructure for a different relationship between communities and the living systems they inhabit.

Community Land Trusts
Remove land permanently from speculative markets. Hold it in common for housing, agriculture, ecological preservation. Over 250 active CLTs in the US.

Community-governed fisheries — from Maine lobstermen's territorial governance to Pacific Islander traditional marine management — outperforming private and state management on sustainability.

The frame shift: from stopping to growing

Environmental movements have spent decades — necessarily, correctly — in defensive mode. Stop this pipeline. Prevent this clearcut. Block this development. These fights are worth fighting. They've saved real things. The legal and advocacy infrastructure built through that work is part of the commons in its own way.

But defense alone can't win a regenerative future. You can prevent the worst deforestation and still live in a world that's slowly becoming less habitable. The commons frame offers a second mode that runs alongside defense: construction. Not just stopping extraction — building the ownership structures and governance systems that change the incentive landscape at the root.

A community land trust doesn't stop a developer by fighting in court. It stops the developer by removing the land from the market entirely, permanently. A community energy cooperative doesn't just generate clean power — it changes who owns the power infrastructure and therefore who controls energy decisions in the future. A seed library doesn't just preserve genetic diversity — it reclaims the means of food production from the corporate enclosure of intellectual property.

These are the same fights. Defense and construction are the same movement, running in parallel, each making the other more effective.

Thirty years of forest commons in Chiapas

The Zapatista communities in Chiapas, Mexico have been managing their territories autonomously since 1994. The forests they govern have been maintained, not extracted, because the communities that live in them have long-term stakes in their survival — as water sources, as climate protection, as places their children will inherit.

This isn't romantic primitivism. It's a practical demonstration that commons governance produces different ecological outcomes than either private extraction or state administration. The forests are still there. The communities are still in them. Read the full Zapatistas case study →

What enclosure looks like now

Enclosure hasn't stopped. It's accelerating, taking forms that don't always look like fences around fields. Water privatization — the transfer of municipal water systems to private operators — is happening across Africa, Latin America, and increasingly in the US. The Rohingya in Myanmar were driven from their land partly to open it to extraction. Seed patents by Bayer-Monsanto enclose the genetic commons of agricultural diversity accumulated over ten thousand years of farming. "Land grabs" — large-scale purchases of agricultural land in the Global South by sovereign wealth funds, private equity, and corporate agriculture — have transferred tens of millions of hectares out of smallholder and commons governance in the past two decades.

Each of these is an ecological event as well as an economic one. When water is privatized, the incentive shifts from maintenance to revenue. When seeds are patented, the incentive shifts from diversity to yield optimization for single-crop monoculture. When land is grabbed, the incentive shifts from community food production to export commodity agriculture. The ecological outcomes of these shifts are not surprises. They are predicted by the logic of enclosure.

Understanding this doesn't make climate change less urgent. It makes the solution less mysterious: rebuild the commons, and the ecology follows.

Where to plug in

Grow the commons you can reach

The most powerful thing you can do for the ecology is not necessarily what produces the best Instagram content. It's identifying a piece of your local commons — land, water, energy, seeds, knowledge — and working to deepen community control over it.

Find the community land trust in your area and support it, join it, or help start one if it doesn't exist. Look for community energy cooperatives and join the board. Donate seeds to and borrow seeds from your local seed library. Join the repair cafe or start one in a library that doesn't have one. Find out who governs the water in your area and make that governance more democratic.

And when you're doing the defense work — the pipeline fight, the forest protection campaign, the regulatory hearing — ask what comes after the defense. What ownership structure, what governance form, removes this piece of the commons from the market permanently? Defense plus construction is the long game. Either one alone is not enough.
