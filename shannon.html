For People Building the Future — Claude Shannon — A Mathematical Theory of Communication (1948)

The Channel
and the Common

Shannon proved that information has a physics. A channel is shared infrastructure. The question isn't whether signal flows — it's whether the architecture is built to let it. The internet answered that question once. Platform enclosure is trying to answer it again.

01

What Shannon was actually solving

In 1948, Claude Shannon published a paper at Bell Labs that solved a problem telephone engineers had been wrestling with for decades: how do you send a message reliably through a channel that distorts it?1 Wires introduce static. Amplifiers add noise. The signal you send is not the signal that arrives. How much of this can you correct for, and how much is irreducible?

Shannon's answer was mathematical and exact. He defined information — not as meaning, not as content, but as a measurable quantity: the amount of surprise in a message. A coin flip carries one bit of information. A message you already know carries none. A channel has a capacity — a maximum rate at which information can travel through it reliably, regardless of what you do. Push harder than the channel allows and errors accumulate faster than any correction scheme can fix. Stay under capacity and reliable transmission is always achievable, in principle, for any channel and any message.2

This was not a result people expected. The idea that noise could be beaten — that errors were correctable in principle, not just in practice — was startling. The idea that channels had hard limits was equally startling. Shannon proved both simultaneously with the same framework.

What he was solving looked like an engineering problem. What he had actually done was found the physics underneath communication itself.

02

A channel is shared infrastructure

A channel, in Shannon's framework, is not owned by any particular message. It is the medium through which messages pass — neutral to content, indifferent to sender, governed only by physics. Its capacity is a property of the channel, not of whoever happens to be using it. You don't get more capacity by paying more or knowing the right people. The physics doesn't negotiate.

This is a commons description. A channel is shared infrastructure with a defined carrying capacity, governed by rules that aren't anybody's property, producing value — the reliable transmission of information — that nobody made and nobody owns. Shannon's math didn't just describe telephone wires. It described the structural logic of any medium: radio spectrum, fiber optic networks, the internet's packet-switching backbone.3

Bell Labs itself had this structure — and it mattered. AT&T was a regulated public utility, which meant it operated under a consent decree that kept it out of the computer business.4 In exchange for its monopoly on telephone service, it was barred from turning its research into products it could sell. Bell Labs had enormous resources, some of the sharpest researchers in the world, and no direct commercial pressure on what they studied. Shannon worked there for years, juggling and unicycling and developing information theory without a product roadmap. The transistor, Unix, C, the first communications satellites — all Bell Labs, all commons-produced knowledge that the institution couldn't enclose even if it wanted to.

When AT&T was broken up in 1984 and the consent decree constraints lifted, Bell Labs began its long decline. The commons conditions that produced the knowledge were dismantled in the name of market efficiency. The knowledge that required them had already escaped into the world, but the institutional structure that made it possible was gone.

The pattern

Commons-structured institution produces knowledge. Knowledge escapes into the world. Institution is restructured toward enclosure. The knowledge it could have produced under commons conditions does not get produced. We don't notice the absence.

What we don't build in the Bell Labs that never was is the invisible cost of the enclosure. Shannon's paper exists. The version of Shannon working under commercial pressure at a product-focused lab — we don't know what that produces, but we know it isn't a mathematical theory of communication.

03

Entropy is not decay

Shannon borrowed his central concept from physics: entropy. In thermodynamics, entropy measures the number of ways a system can be arranged — its disorder, loosely speaking, though disorder is a misleading word.5 A gas with molecules spread evenly across a room has high entropy not because it's chaotic but because there are many more arrangements that look like "spread evenly" than arrangements that look like "all crowded in one corner." Entropy is a count of possibilities, not a judgment about mess.

Shannon took this idea and applied it to messages. A message drawn from a source where every symbol is equally likely has maximum entropy — maximum information content per symbol. A message that is entirely predictable has zero entropy — no new information, because you already knew what was coming. The formula Shannon derived for information entropy is mathematically identical to the formula Boltzmann derived for thermodynamic entropy. This is not coincidence and not metaphor. Information has a physics. The universe runs on the same math as a telephone wire.

The universe runs on the same math as a telephone wire. Information isn't a metaphor for physical processes — it is a physical process.

The standard story about entropy is that it means things fall apart — that the universe tends toward disorder, that everything degrades. This is the enclosure story about commons: left to themselves, shared resources deteriorate. The tragedy of the commons is presented as entropy in action, the natural tendency of ungoverned systems toward collapse.

But entropy doesn't mean degradation. It means information. A high-entropy system is one with many possible states — a system that can carry a lot of signal. A low-entropy system has been forced into a narrow range of states: predictable, constrained, less able to carry information because most of the possibilities have been eliminated. Enclosure is entropy reduction by force. It doesn't prevent degradation — it prevents possibility.

04

How form finds itself

Jean Baudrillard, writing in 1968, made an observation about the evolution of technical objects that feels strange until it clicks.6 Objects, he argued, evolve toward their functional form — not because a market selects for them, not because a designer gets clever, but because the function itself has a logic that gradually strips away everything irrelevant to it. The chrome fins on 1950s American cars are ornamental excess, a sign of status pasted onto an object that doesn't need it. European cars of the same era were already shedding the excess, converging toward what a car actually is. The form wants to find itself. Given enough iterations and enough pressure, it does.

Deleuze and Guattari make a related argument about evolution itself.7 Evolution doesn't proceed up a ladder from simple to complex, from primitive to advanced. It moves across a plane of variation, selecting forms that cohere with their environment. The eye didn't evolve because eyes are the goal — it evolved because light-sensitive structures had selective advantages in environments that contained light, and the form that best used that environment propagated. The form and the environment found each other. Neither designed the other.

Packet-switching — the architectural principle of the internet — was not a market victory. It was not the result of a competition between rival network designs that ended when the best one won. It was a convergence toward the form that matched the physics of distributed information transmission.8 A network that breaks messages into packets, routes each independently, and reassembles them at the destination is more robust to failure, scales better with traffic, and uses channel capacity more efficiently than a circuit-switched network. These are not opinions. They follow from Shannon's math. The internet found the form that the physics was pointing at.

Open protocols — TCP/IP, HTTP, SMTP — are not politically neutral choices that happened to work out. They are morphological solutions: the architecture that matched the medium. The decision to make them open, ungoverned by any single owner, without rent-extraction built in, was not charity. It was the architecture that let the form be what it was.

05

Enclosure as noise

Shannon's framework has a precise concept for what platform enclosure does to a network: it introduces noise. Not random static — structured noise. Interference that degrades specific signals while amplifying others. A walled garden doesn't reduce the channel's carrying capacity in the aggregate; it redirects it. Your message travels, but only to destinations the platform permits, only under surveillance the platform profits from, only if the platform's interests don't conflict with your message reaching its destination.

Channel logic

Capacity is a physical property. The channel carries what you send.

Errors correctable in principle. Reliable transmission achievable at any rate under channel capacity. The physics is indifferent to content.

Platform logic

Capacity is a commercial property. The channel carries what you pay for.

Errors introduced by design. Throttling, algorithmic amplification, shadow-banning. The physics repurposed as a filter for profit.

The internet didn't become what it became because of platform enclosure. It became what it became before the enclosures were complete, while the open-protocol commons still dominated. The World Wide Web that Tim Berners-Lee published in 1991 ran on HTTP, which was free, and HTML, which was free, over TCP/IP, which was free, on a network that didn't know what you were saying and didn't care.9 That is the channel condition. That is the architecture Shannon's math was pointing at.

What followed — the browser wars, the portal era, the platform consolidation, the surveillance advertising complex — is not the internet maturing. It is enclosure colonizing the commons that built the internet, extracting rent from the channel it did not build and could not have built under enclosure conditions. Facebook didn't build the internet. It moved into a neighborhood the commons built and started charging admission.

06

Build for the channel

If you are building something — software, infrastructure, a protocol, an institution — Shannon's framework gives you a way to think about what you're doing that the market framework does not. You are not inventing from scratch. You are finding the form that the function is pointing at. The physics already knows what the answer is. Your job is to get out of the way of it.

This is what Gödel's incompleteness theorem established for formal systems: the outside is structural, not contingent. The commons is not a political aspiration that good systems might eventually converge on. It is what emerges when you stop injecting noise. Every distributed system tends toward the open-protocol solution the way every evolutionary line tends toward the form that fits the environment. Enclosure fights the physics. It can win for a while. It cannot win permanently without destroying the channel it depends on.

Elinor Ostrom's research on actual commons governance found the same pattern from the other direction: commons that survived weren't the ones with the most restrictions on use. They were the ones with the lowest governance overhead relative to the scale of coordination they achieved — the ones that found the institutional form that fit the resource. Low noise. High channel capacity. The form that matched the physics.10

What this means practically

Open protocols over platforms. A protocol that nobody owns and anyone can implement is channel architecture. A platform that mediates all communication is noise injection with a business model.

Commons governance over enclosure. The governance overhead that Ostrom found in successful commons isn't bureaucracy — it's the error-correction layer. It's how the channel stays clean.

Build for the form, not the market. The form the physics is pointing at and the form that maximizes near-term extraction are not the same form. One of them is still there in fifty years.

Shannon spent his career at Bell Labs finding the forms that communication was pointing at. He wasn't building products. He was doing what mathematicians do when they're working well: finding out what was already true. The information theory he found is still true. The channel capacity theorem still holds. The architecture it recommends — open, distributed, governed by physics rather than ownership — is still the one that works.

The enclosures will keep trying to close the channel. They always have. They have never succeeded in closing it permanently, because the form the physics points at keeps reasserting itself in new substrate. The commons is not fragile. It is what keeps coming back.

  1. 1 Claude E. Shannon, "A Mathematical Theory of Communication," Bell System Technical Journal 27, nos. 3–4 (July and October 1948): 379–423, 623–656. Republished with a postscript by Warren Weaver as The Mathematical Theory of Communication (University of Illinois Press, 1949). The 1949 book is the accessible entry point; Weaver's postscript attempts a plain-English account. For biography, Jimmy Soni and Rob Goodman's A Mind at Play: How Claude Shannon Invented the Information Age (Simon & Schuster, 2017) is thorough and readable. The juggling and unicycling are documented. ↩
  2. 2 The channel capacity theorem — now called the Shannon–Hartley theorem for continuous channels and Shannon's noisy-channel coding theorem for discrete channels — establishes that for any channel with a given capacity C (measured in bits per second), and any transmission rate R below C, there exists an encoding scheme that achieves arbitrarily low error probability. This was the surprising half of the result: you cannot exceed capacity, but below it you can always achieve reliability. Shannon proved existence without constructing the codes, which is characteristic of his approach. Practical error-correcting codes that approach channel capacity took decades to develop and are the basis of all modern digital communication. ↩
  3. 3 The application of Shannon's framework to spectrum policy and internet architecture is well developed in Yochai Benkler's The Wealth of Networks: How Social Production Transforms Markets and Freedom (Yale University Press, 2006), particularly chapters 4–5. Benkler argues that the choice between commons and property regimes for spectrum and infrastructure has direct effects on the diversity and resilience of communication. Shannon's math underlies the technical arguments even when Benkler doesn't cite it directly. ↩
  4. 4 The 1956 consent decree settling United States v. Jon Gertner's The Idea Factory: Bell Labs and the Great Age of American Innovation (Penguin Press, 2012) documents the institutional conditions in detail. The transistor's 1947 invention by Shockley, Bardeen, and Brattain at Bell Labs — licensed widely under the consent decree — is the most consequential single product of the commons-structured research institution. ↩
  5. 5 Ludwig Boltzmann derived the entropy formula S = k log W in the 1870s–1890s; the full formulation appears in his 1877 paper "Über die Beziehung zwischen dem zweiten Hauptsatze der mechanischen Wärmetheorie und der Wahrscheinlichkeitsrechnung." Shannon's entropy H = −Σ p log p is mathematically equivalent (with different constants). The story of Shannon consulting John von Neumann about what to call the quantity — von Neumann supposedly saying "call it entropy, since no one knows what entropy really is, so in a debate you will always have the advantage" — is probably apocryphal but has been widely repeated. Whether or not Shannon needed the strategic cover, the mathematical equivalence is genuine and foundational. ↩

Baudrillard draws on the design critic and historian Abraham Moles. Note that the later Baudrillard of simulation and hyperreality is a different Baudrillard than the one writing here; The System of Objects is empirical and sociological in a way his 1970s–80s work is not. ↩

Both arrived at the same architecture by different routes, which is the convergence pattern. The ARPANET, built on packet-switching from 1969, was a government-funded commons infrastructure.   10. 10 Elinor Ostrom, Governing the Commons: The Evolution of Institutions for Collective Action (Cambridge University Press, 1990). The design principles Ostrom extracted from successful commons — clearly defined boundaries, rules matched to local conditions, collective choice arrangements, monitoring, graduated sanctions — are best understood as the minimum governance structure needed to maintain channel integrity. Too little governance and the channel degrades through over-use; too much and the governance overhead exceeds the channel's productive capacity. Ostrom won the Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences in 2009, the first woman to do so. ↩
For People Building the Future — Series

The form is already there.
Your job is to stop blocking it.

Shannon found the math. Ostrom found the governance. Berners-Lee built the prototype. The architecture that works has been demonstrated, proven, and repeatedly re-demonstrated every time someone builds commons infrastructure instead of a platform.

The enclosures are real and the damage they do is real. But they are noise in a channel that keeps reasserting its form. The question for builders is whether you're helping the form find itself or adding more noise.

The internet is not broken. It has been interrupted. There's a difference.
