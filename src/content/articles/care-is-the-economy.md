You have been told that the economy is production and exchange. Markets and labor. Wages and consumption.

That is a lie. The economy is care. All production depends on a workforce that has been fed, clothed, sheltered, healed, and taught. All of that happens in the domain of care — and it is still, mostly, done by women, without compensation, treated as love rather than work.

The economy runs on care. Capitalism cannot exist without it. But capitalism cannot pay for it, because if it had to pay market rates for all the care work currently being extracted without compensation, the entire profit structure would collapse. That is the secret at the center of the system. And it is also where the leverage is.

## What care work actually is

Cooking. Childcare. Teaching. Healing. Elder care. Emotional labor. Cleaning. Maintaining relationships. Community building. All of this is work. All of it requires skill and knowledge and effort. All of it is necessary for any society to function.

For centuries, it was organized as commons — shared responsibilities within communities, split according to what people knew and could do, with no accounting of debt or obligation. You helped because the community expected it and because the community would help you when you needed it.

Capitalism [enclosed care](/what-they-burned). Moved it first into the private household, where it was performed by women without compensation. Then into the market, where it became a service to be bought and sold. Then into the state, where it became bureaucratized and insufficient. The transition from each form to the next preserved the extraction while changing its administration.

## Why it's in crisis

Care work is crisis-prone in ways that other economic sectors aren't, for a structural reason: it cannot be made more productive without ceasing to be care. You cannot automate the relationship between a sick person and someone who tends them. You cannot streamline grief. The things that make care valuable — attention, presence, responsiveness to the specific person — are exactly the things that resist the efficiency logic of markets.

This means care work is systematically undervalued relative to its actual social importance, because markets value what can be made more productive, and care cannot. It also means that as societies age and care needs grow, the gap between what care costs to provide well and what the market will pay for it widens. The care crisis is not a failure of the care sector. It is a structural feature of treating care as a commodity.

## Building care as commons

Some communities are rebuilding this. Childcare collectives where parents share responsibility and costs. Care cooperatives where the work is shared and compensation is distributed fairly. Community kitchens that feed neighborhoods and rebuild the knowledge of cooking. Care networks that track who needs what and who has capacity to help, without monetizing the relationships.

[Cooperation Jackson](https://cooperationjackson.org) in Mississippi. The care-based economics of indigenous communities that never fully commodified care to begin with. The timebanks emerging in post-austerity communities across southern Europe. The mutual aid networks that appeared after COVID and haven't fully dissolved. These are not experimental alternatives. They are recoveries of a governance form that predates the enclosure.

The work is intimate and local. It requires building trust with your neighbors. It requires time. It requires willingness to make your needs visible and to help others with theirs. It cannot be scaled to infinity or profited from. It only works if people care about each other.

That constraint is not a limitation. It is a design feature. The care commons is inherently anti-extractive, because what makes it work — genuine attention, ongoing relationship, mutual obligation — is precisely what extraction destroys.

And it is the most revolutionary thing you can do, because it breaks the fundamental tool that capitalism uses to bind you: your desperation and isolation.
