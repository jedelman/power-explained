All wealth comes from land. All labor happens on land. All life depends on land. Yet in the capitalist system, land is treated as a commodity—something to be bought and sold to the highest bidder, extracted until it's exhausted, abandoned when it's no longer profitable.

That is not necessary. Land can be held in trust. Governed collectively. Managed for the benefit of the people who live on it, the generations that will come after, and the non-human life that depends on it.

## What enclosure of land actually meant

The enclosure of common lands in Europe was not about efficiency. It was about control. It was about converting the peasantry into a landless working class that had to sell their labor to survive. It was about making land into a speculation asset, breaking the relationship between people and the territory they depended on.

We are still living in that system. Land that could feed a community is held by investors who have never set foot on it. Housing is speculative. Farming is controlled by corporations. Indigenous territories are stolen systematically. The relationship between people and land—which in traditional commons was about responsibility and long-term stewardship—is replaced by the calculation of maximum extraction and profit.

This is destroying both people and ecosystems. Communities displaced for dams. Forests clearcut for short-term profit. Soil depleted. Water polluted. Species extinct.

> **Land is not an investment.**
> **It is the basis of life.**

## How land becomes commons again

Some models exist. Community land trusts that separate land ownership from housing ownership, making housing affordable permanently while breaking the gentrification cycle. Indigenous land governance returning territory to the people who have cared for it for generations. Public lands managed for collective benefit rather than extraction. The Zapatistas' communal land governance in Chiapas. The forest commons in India maintained by indigenous communities despite state and corporate attempts to enclose them.

The work is specific: it requires changing law, resisting state and corporate enclosure, building governance structures that keep land in commons while allowing people to live on and work that land. It requires understanding what grows and what this particular land needs. It requires long-term commitment—generations, not quarters.

And it changes everything. When land is held in commons, housing becomes affordable. Food becomes local and abundant. Ecosystems recover. Communities have roots.
