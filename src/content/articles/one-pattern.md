You have read four series. Different thinkers. Different centuries. Different continents. If you stop and look at what's underneath all of it, there is one pattern.

Enclosure — the conversion of something held in common into something controlled for private benefit. And the counterpart that appears just as consistently: the commons. Resources governed collectively, by the people who depend on them, for shared benefit.

This isn't a metaphor being stretched across domains. It is the same structural move, recurring in different materials. Land. Labor. Care. Knowledge. Governance. The pattern holds.

## What each series was actually about

**Series I** began with the basic argument: the template most cities run on was designed for a world of scarce information flowing upward. It produces the same outcomes regardless of who runs it — not because officials are corrupt but because the system is designed to extract information and return decisions, not to use local knowledge. [Hardin was wrong](/hardin-was-wrong) about the commons. [Ostrom](/ostrom) showed it works, under conditions that can be described and replicated. The commons is not a failure mode. It is a governance form that keeps getting enclosed.

**Series II** moved to labor. [Luxemburg](/the-outside-capital-needs) showed that capitalism has always depended on an outside it cannot produce itself. [Kollontai](/who-pays-for-the-next-shift) named what that outside is mostly made of: the reproductive labor that keeps the workforce alive between shifts, performed primarily by women, without compensation, treated as nature rather than work. [Federici](/what-they-burned) traced when and how that labor was enclosed: the witch trials and the enclosures of early modern Europe were not separate events. They were part of the same structural transformation — one that drove people from common land into wage labor and simultaneously reorganized care and healing from communal functions into private female labor. The engine was hidden by making it invisible.

**Series III** asked what to do. The honest answer from the historical record is that seizing the state and concentrating power to fix things from above has reliably reproduced the domination it was supposed to end. The Russian Revolution generated two different organizational forms: the distributed, self-organizing soviets that arose from below, and the centralized party hierarchy that captured them. The party ate the revolution — not because of individual betrayal, but because of a structural feature. The alternative — building the world you want now, in the interstices of the current one — is not a consolation prize. It is a specific claim about how change at scale actually happens.

**Series IV** pushed back on the frameworks themselves. Racial capitalism is not capitalism plus racism added on top. Race is how capitalism organized the production of its outside — the geography of whose labor is extracted at what price, whose land is enclosed and under what legal cover. Underdevelopment is not a condition; it is something done to places, deliberately, through trade structures and debt arrangements designed to keep the outside producing for the inside. [Arundhati Roy](/the-commons-they-never-stopped-building) reported from the enclosures as they happen — specific people, specific places, specific consequences. And the commons being built anyway, under eviction orders.

## The same move, different materials

Look at what was enclosed across all four series:

**Land.** Common fields, forests, and fisheries governed collectively for generations — enclosed by legal instruments into private property, driving people into wage labor to survive.

**Care.** Healing knowledge, midwifery, community childcare — reorganized out of communal networks and into private households and, later, licensed professions and markets. Made invisible so it could be extracted without compensation.

**Governance.** The capacity of communities to manage their own affairs — enclosed first by colonial administration, then by the nation-state, then by the vanguard party claiming to act in the people's name.

**Knowledge.** The accumulated intellectual commons — scientific knowledge, traditional practices, cultural production — enclosed into intellectual property, credentials, and now AI systems trained on humanity's expression without consent.

**Credit.** The social commons of mutual obligation and trust — enclosed into commodity money and extractive finance, weapons deployed against the communities that built them.

The pattern is not coincidental. Each enclosure enables the others. Enclose the land and people must sell their labor. Enclose their labor and they must buy back their care. Enclose their care and they must buy back their knowledge. Enclose their knowledge and they must buy back their governance. The accumulation of enclosures is the accumulation of dependence.

## The commons is not what was taken

In every case, people keep building commons anyway. In the cracks. Under eviction orders. In the spaces capital doesn't know how to colonize yet. The maroon communities. The Black church. The forest commons maintained by indigenous peoples. The mutual aid networks that appear after disaster. The open-source movements. The community land trusts.

The system is built on enclosure. But enclosure is not inevitable. It requires constant work. The moment you stop defending it, the moment you walk away from the categories it imposes, the moment you start building with your neighbors, the commons begins to emerge again.

The commons is not what was taken. It is what people keep building, despite everything.

The next series is about building it deliberately.
