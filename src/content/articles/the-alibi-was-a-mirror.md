A psychiatrist. In the French colony of Algeria. In the middle of revolution. Frantz Fanon treated patients—colonizers and colonized—and in their symptoms, he saw colonialism as a psychological structure. Not just an economic system. Not just political domination. A way of being that colonialism produced in the bodies and minds of everyone it touched.

The colony doesn't just extract wealth. It produces a particular kind of human being—and then requires that person's cooperation in their own subjection.

## How the colony produces its subjects

The colonizer builds an image of the colonized—lazy, irrational, primitive, naturally suited to subjection. The colonized person is then trapped in an impossible bind: the colonizer says "this is what you are" and uses violence to make that image stick. Resist and you confirm that you're violent and irrational. Comply and you confirm that you're suited to subjection.

The alibi—the story the colonizer tells—is that this reflects an actual difference in human nature. The mirror—what Fanon sees—is that the colonizer is looking at themselves. The violence, the irrationality, the inability to be human under these conditions—these are all produced by colonialism itself, and then attributed to the colonized as proof that colonialism is justified.

> The colonizer projects their own violence
> onto the colonized as nature.
> The mirror shows it back:
> **it was always the colonizer's reflection.**

## What breaks under that pressure

In his work as a psychiatrist, Fanon saw the cost. The psychological damage of being placed in a category that denies your humanity. The strategies people develop to survive that denial. The ways that resistance itself becomes pathologized—your fight for freedom called psychosis, your refusal to accept the category called delusion.

What Fanon documents is not just the injustice of colonialism. It's the way colonialism reaches into the colonized person's self-understanding and forces them to collaborate in the structure that's destroying them. And he shows that the only way out of that bind is through the colonized person breaking free of the need for the colonizer's recognition. Becoming human not by proving it to the colonizer, but by taking freedom, by building something new.
