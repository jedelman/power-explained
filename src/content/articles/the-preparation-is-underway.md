The preparation for a different world is already happening. Not in the future. Now. In communities that are taking back land. In cooperatives that are replacing profit with mutualism. In networks that are sharing care, knowledge, and resources without going through markets or states.

This is not a plan waiting to be implemented. It is already being built.

## What exists now

[Community land trusts](/land-is-the-foundation) have taken thousands of homes out of speculation and made them permanently affordable across decades of hot real estate markets. Champlain Housing Trust in Burlington, Vermont — the largest CLT in the country — has maintained affordability through four decades of price increases that priced everyone else out. Dudley Street Neighborhood Initiative in Boston transformed a neighborhood written off by capital into one that its residents control.

[Credit unions](/money-has-to-be-a-commons) — financial cooperatives owned and governed by their members — serve 130 million Americans. They consistently offer better rates, lower fees, and more equitable lending than investor-owned banks, because they are oriented toward member benefit rather than extraction. Community development financial institutions extend this into communities conventional finance has abandoned: lending where redlining created deserts, financing what the algorithm rejects because the algorithm was trained on the logic of extraction.

Cooperative farms across the upper Midwest maintain soil health and build community wealth while corporate agriculture depletes both. [Cooperation Jackson](https://cooperationjackson.org), rooted in the history of Mississippi civil rights organizing, is building a solidarity economy ecosystem — cooperative production, cooperative housing, cooperative health — in one of the most economically abandoned cities in the country.

The workers who built Mondragon in the Basque Country in the 1950s — starting with a school, then a cooperative, then a network of cooperatives, then a bank to finance them — built the largest worker-owned cooperative network in the world over sixty years. They didn't have a favorable environment. They had Franco's dictatorship. They built anyway.

## What the work requires

Building commons requires learning. Learning how to govern collectively — which means learning to tolerate disagreement, build consensus without suppressing dissent, maintain accountability without hierarchy. This is harder than it sounds and more learnable than it seems. Every functioning cooperative had to figure it out. None of them started knowing how.

It requires time. Commons are slower than extraction because they have to be accountable to everyone who depends on them. That is not a bug. It is a feature. It keeps the system from capturing all the wealth and heading for inevitable collapse.

It requires risk. There is no guarantee. The state will push back when commons institutions threaten the tax base or the labor market or the property market. Capital will push back when commons institutions threaten its supply of cheap labor or its access to extraction. The infrastructure of enclosure is vast and powerful. But the only way to guarantee failure is to not try.

It requires community. You cannot build commons alone. You cannot build them in isolation. You have to find your people. You have to show up. You have to commit to the unglamorous work of showing up over time. The commons is not built by the inspired few. It is built by the people who are still there after the inspired few have moved on to the next thing.

## The pattern of how change happens

The changes that matter most are not announced. Linux didn't announce itself as the future of operating systems when it was a hobby project in 1991. Mutual aid networks don't announce themselves as infrastructure when they start as neighbors helping neighbors. Community land trusts don't announce themselves as the solution to the housing crisis when they start as a small nonprofit with one piece of land.

They prove it by working. Quietly, repeatedly, at small scale, long enough to develop the institutional knowledge of how to maintain themselves. And then, when the conditions change — when the enclosure produces a crisis visible enough that people are ready to see alternatives — the proof of concept is already there.

The conditions are changing. The housing crisis is visible. The care crisis is visible. The climate crisis is visible. The enclosure of AI is visible. The preparation is already underway. The question is not whether to start — that has been answered by the people who started decades ago. The question is whether to join.
