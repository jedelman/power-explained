The preparation for a different world is already happening. Not in the future. Now. In communities that are taking back land. In cooperatives that are replacing profit with mutualism. In networks that are sharing care, knowledge, and resources without going through markets or states. In people saying no to enclosure and yes to commons.

This is not a plan waiting to be implemented. It is already being built.

## What's happening now

Community land trusts have taken thousands of homes out of speculation and made them permanently affordable. The [Champlain Housing Trust](https://www.getahome.org) in Burlington, Vermont — the first municipal land trust in the United States, seeded in part by Bernie Sanders as mayor in 1984 — has maintained affordable housing through multiple real estate cycles while comparable market-rate housing has become unaffordable. The community captures the land's appreciation instead of individual speculators.

Credit unions serve 130 million members in the United States alone, operating as financial cooperatives governed by their depositors. Community development financial institutions have deployed tens of billions of dollars into communities that conventional finance had written off as not worth serving. These are not pilot programs. They are mature institutions with track records.

[Cooperation Jackson](https://cooperationjackson.org) in Mississippi is building a network of worker cooperatives, a community land trust, and a community production center in one of the most economically marginalized communities in the United States — rooted explicitly in the history of Black radical organizing in Mississippi and in the theoretical framework of solidarity economy. The [Mondragon](https://www.mondragon-corporation.com) cooperative network in the Basque Country employs 80,000 people in worker-owned enterprises spanning manufacturing, retail, finance, and education. The Kerala cooperative movement has built one of the most comprehensive social infrastructure systems in the developing world.

Indigenous land governance is being reconstructed across North America, Australia, and Latin America — not as nostalgic return but as the practical recovery of governance systems that solved in practice what modern land management keeps failing to solve: how to manage a territory for the long-term benefit of the people and ecosystems that depend on it.

## What the work requires

Building commons requires learning. Learning how to govern collectively — how to make decisions that stick when everyone affected has a say, how to resolve conflict without hierarchy, how to manage risk without privatizing it. All of this is teachable. Formal bodies of practice have developed around each of these questions.

It requires time. Commons governance is slower than extraction because it has to be accountable to everyone who depends on it. That is not a bug. It is a feature. It keeps the system from capturing all the wealth and heading for inevitable collapse.

It requires risk. There is no guarantee. The state will push back. Capital will push back. The infrastructure of enclosure is vast and powerful. But the only way to guarantee failure is to not try.

And it requires community. You cannot build commons alone. You have to find your people. You have to show up. You have to commit to the unglamorous work of showing up over time.

## The argument this series has made

Five series. One argument with a shape.

The city doesn't work because it's designed to extract information and return decisions, not to use local knowledge. The commons works, under conditions that can be described and replicated, and it keeps getting enclosed. Capitalism depends on an outside it cannot produce — care, land, community — which it extracts without replacing. Revolution from above reproduces domination. Building from below works. The frameworks missed what the colonized already knew. And the preparation for something different is already underway.

The world you want to live in will not be built by people waiting for permission. It is built by people who start, now, where they are, with the people they have, in the conditions they're actually in.

The commons is not what was taken. It is what people keep building, despite everything.
