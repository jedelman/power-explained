The outside that capitalism depends on is not a natural condition. It is not the product of geography or history or the neutral workings of economic law. It is produced — continuously, through specific mechanisms, by specific decisions made by specific people who benefit from its existence.

This chapter asks how that production works. How does it come to be that certain people end up on the outside — providing the surplus that capital extracts without compensation, absorbing the costs that capital externalizes, treated as the background condition against which real economic activity happens?

## The human as a project

Sylvia Wynter spent decades developing an argument that Western modernity produced a specific version of the human — what she called "Man" — and then universalized it. The version of the human that emerged from the European Renaissance and the colonial encounter was particular: rational, autonomous, propertied, male, white. It was presented as the human as such.

The effect of universalizing this particular version is that everyone who doesn't fit it is located outside humanity rather than outside a specific conception of it. The colonized person is not classified as a different kind of human with different characteristics. They are classified as less than fully human — as closer to nature, as pre-rational, as requiring external governance and management. This is not incidental to colonialism. It is the mechanism by which colonial extraction is made legible as not-extraction. You cannot steal from someone whose relationship to property is not recognized.

Wynter's argument matters because it shows that the outside is not produced by economic mechanisms alone. It is produced by a classification system that determines who gets to count — whose claims to land are legally recognized, whose labor gets named as labor, whose governance arrangements are treated as legitimate, whose ways of knowing get to be called knowledge.

The commons is the explicit alternative to this classification system. The commons does not ask who is fully human before determining what governance rights they have. It asks who depends on the resource. Dependency is the criterion, not classification.

## The intersection problem

The mechanism of the outside becomes more visible when you look at what happens at the boundaries between categories.

In the 1970s and 1980s, both feminist analysis and anti-racist analysis were producing important work on the structure of oppression. But each framework had a blind spot: feminist analysis tended to use "women" as a unified category, and anti-racist analysis tended to use "Black" as a unified category. When Black women tried to use either framework to understand their specific situation, they found that neither fit.

Kimberlé Crenshaw's intervention, which she named intersectionality, was to point out that the categories themselves were the problem. "Women" in feminist analysis was implicitly white; "Black" in anti-racist analysis was implicitly male. The experience of Black women — the specific ways in which they were located at the intersection of multiple systems of classification and extraction — was invisible to both frameworks because neither had been built to see it.

This is not just a theoretical point. Crenshaw developed it through a specific case: a group of Black women workers at General Motors who were being laid off while the company claimed it was not discriminating against either women (because it still employed women — white women in clerical positions) or Black people (because it still employed Black people — Black men in factory positions). The discrimination against Black women was structurally invisible to both the race-discrimination framework and the sex-discrimination framework. To see it required a framework that could account for the intersection.

The outside is not a single thing. It is produced at multiple intersections of classification — race, gender, class, colonial status, ability — and the people located at those intersections experience forms of exclusion and extraction that cannot be captured by analyzing any single dimension in isolation.

## The gap in the system

Some people fall through every category the system uses. They are not in the wrong database. They are in the gap between databases.

The system of social provision — healthcare, housing support, income maintenance, legal protection — is organized around categories. You qualify for a program or you don't. You have documentation or you don't. You fall into a recognized status — worker, dependent, disabled, elderly — or you exist in a space that no program was designed to serve.

The gap is not accidental. Every system that allocates provision by category produces people at the boundaries of categories who do not qualify for anything. And the people most likely to be at those boundaries are the people whose situations are least represented in the political processes that set the categories — the people whose claims on the system were not considered when the system was designed.

Understanding the gap structurally changes what you look for. The question is not "what went wrong?" — as if specific individuals fell through by accident. The question is "what does this gap tell us about whose needs were assumed to be central when the system was designed?" The gap shows you the shape of the exclusion.

The commons addresses this differently. Commons governance begins with the question of who depends on the resource, not the question of who qualifies for provision. The difference is not administrative. It reflects a fundamental difference in what the institution is for. Is it for the people whose needs were anticipated when it was designed? Or for the people who are actually here?
