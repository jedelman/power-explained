Let's start with agreement: the people who looked at capitalism and said "this needs to go" were not wrong.

They were looking at child labor in textile mills. Workers dying in coal mines at thirty. Empires extracting entire continents to enrich a handful of European countries. A system that kept producing famines in places that were producing plenty of food. Wages that didn't keep pace with productivity for generation after generation. The enclosure of common lands that had sustained communities for centuries, driving people into cities to sell their labor or starve.

Their analysis: this isn't a bug. It's the feature. The system works exactly as designed — the design is just not for us.

That analysis holds. A hundred and fifty years of reform, regulation, and social democracy have softened the edges. They have not changed the architecture. The system still runs on an outside — on labor, land, and resources it encloses, extracts from, and moves on from when depleted. The outside is still mostly people who aren't white, aren't in the Global North, and don't get to set the terms. The math Luxemburg identified in 1913 is still the math.

## The evidence that accumulated

Marx's analysis of capital was published in 1867. In the century and a half since, the evidence has continued to accumulate.

Wages in the United States have decoupled from productivity since roughly 1973. In the three decades after World War II, as productivity rose, wages rose with it. After 1973, productivity kept rising and wages stopped. The surplus that workers created went somewhere — into profits, into executive compensation, into the financial sector — and not to the workers who produced it. This is the basic Marxist analysis playing out in the data.

The Global South's relationship to the Global North continues to be organized by the same mechanisms of extraction that characterized formal colonialism, now dressed in the language of trade and development. Transfer pricing by multinationals moves profits from low-income countries to tax havens. Debt service to rich-country creditors has, in many years, exceeded aid flows into poor countries. The IMF's structural adjustment programs of the 1980s and 1990s dismantled the public institutions of debtor countries, opening their markets to foreign capital at the cost of domestic economic development. The extraction is continuous; the form changes.

The ecological crisis is capitalism's most visible failure. The accumulation logic that drives the system requires continuous growth — continuous conversion of natural systems into economic products, continuous externalization of environmental costs onto future generations and onto communities without political power. Climate change is the ledger entry for two centuries of that externalization, coming due. It cannot be resolved within the existing economic framework, because the existing economic framework requires the behaviors that produce it.

## Why the prescription failed

The basic critique is widely understood. The problem isn't the diagnosis. The problem is the prescription.

When the analysis is that capitalism is irredeemably broken and must be replaced, the next question is: by what, and through what process? The answers the mainstream socialist tradition gave in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries were: by socialism, through seizure of the state. The means of production, collectively owned. The state, controlled by the party of the working class. The surplus, distributed according to social need rather than accumulated as private profit.

The theory was coherent. The practice was catastrophic — not in every instance, but reliably enough to constitute a pattern that demands explanation. The Soviet Union began with genuine revolutionary energy, real commitment to workers' power, and significant early achievements in industrialization and literacy. It ended with the Gulag, with the terror, with a bureaucratic apparatus that reproduced the domination it was supposed to end, just under different management.

This is not because the people were bad. It is because the structure was wrong.

Taking state power and using it to reorganize the economy runs into a specific problem: the state is itself a concentrating structure. It accumulates decision-making capacity at the top. The people at the top develop interests in maintaining their position. The mechanisms that were supposed to keep the state accountable to the workers — the party, the vanguard, the leading role of the proletariat — became the mechanisms through which the state insulated itself from accountability.

The next piece looks at this in specific historical detail. Here the point is more general: the left was right about the problem and wrong about the solution, and the wrongness of the solution was not accidental. It followed from a misdiagnosis of what the state is and what it does.

## What was missed

The state is not a neutral instrument that can be captured and redirected. It has a structure — hierarchical, concentrating, self-reinforcing — that tends to reproduce certain kinds of outcomes regardless of the intentions of the people who operate it. A state organized to manage a capitalist economy for the benefit of the capitalist class can be reformed around the edges, but its basic architecture shapes what is possible and what is not.

What was missed was the relationship between means and ends. The form of organization you use to pursue your goals is not separable from the goals you achieve. A hierarchical party that seizes state power and reorganizes the economy from above will produce a hierarchical society organized from above — regardless of its stated goals, regardless of the intentions of its founders, regardless of the genuine commitment to equality of many of its members.

The alternative — organizing from below, building the institutions that embody the world you want rather than waiting to capture the institutions that exist — was always available. It was also always less dramatic, slower, requiring more patience and less certainty. And it had practitioners: the council communists, the anarcho-syndicalists, the cooperative movements, the mutual aid traditions. The next piece is about what they knew.
