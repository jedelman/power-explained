Here is what that looked like, the first week. One of us was a quiet man — the stoic mountain type, arms crossed, chin down, a half smile that meant he was glad to be there and would not be saying so. I grew up around men like him; I knew he was all right. But it seemed to me he needed a hug — not a thought, a feeling — and one morning as we gathered, I opened my arms and he came in for a bear hug. Two guys glad to see each other. Afterward he carried himself differently: hands in his pockets instead of crossed over his chest, chin up, a full grin surfacing and going. Weeks later he told me it was the first time anyone had hugged him in years, and that he hadn't known how badly he needed it. He had forgotten he could. And he had spent those weeks surrounded by people hugging — it was happening to all of us, all the time, which is exactly why I almost missed it landing on him.
