Here is how you can watch that reading happen. Birds keep exactly the distance they need to feel safe — no farther, because flying is work — and around a group of people that distance is usually about twice the size of the group. When I taught summer camps, my favorite trick was to wear the kids out with enough fun that they actually wanted to sit still for a minute. I made the stillness a game too, the way I had been taught. What I did not always tell them was the rest of it: that a body gone quiet and a mind gone calm read to a bird as safe. And most days, after a few minutes, a few of the bravest — usually chickadees — would drop in close to inspect this group that had fallen silent, decide we were safe, or weird and interesting, or some chickadee blend of the two, and say so to the others, who came nearer. The look on the kids' faces when a sit actually worked is one of the most magical things I have seen: the universe itself, in the form of a small bird, leaning in to say *you're all right.* And when we forgot ourselves and went loud again and the birds lifted away — that disappointment was a powerful teacher.
