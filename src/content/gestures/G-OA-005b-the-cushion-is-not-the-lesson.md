The cognitive dissonance was blinding. From the outside, from inside the career frame, my life had ended. I was done, finished, a cautionary tale. From where I actually stood, my life had just begun — or resumed, as if it had been waiting under the other one the whole time. And I have to be honest about why I got the gentle version of the floor, because not everyone does and the difference is not virtue. It helped that we had been aggressively building a nest egg for years. It helped that it was burnout and not a medical emergency that put me down. It helped enormously that I was in 2015 Seattle and not 2020 Norfolk. I was very, very lucky, and a reader on a harder floor than mine should hear me say it plainly: the five acres and the named chickens are not the lesson. They were the cushioning. The lesson is underneath the cushion, and the reason I am willing to spend this many words on my own soft landing is that the lesson is available even to the people who land hard — maybe especially to them, though they pay more to learn it.
