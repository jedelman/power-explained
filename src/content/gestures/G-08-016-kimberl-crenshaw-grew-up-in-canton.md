plain: >
  Kimberlé Crenshaw kept meeting the same problem. She met it in her classes.
  She met it in the legal cases she read. The law could see race. The law
  could see sex. But it could not see her. The law's groups were built
  around other people. It had no way to talk about both at once.

  One case showed this clearly: DeGraffenreid v. General Motors. The law on
  race came mostly from cases about Black men. The law on sex came mostly
  from cases about white women. If your harm came from just one of these,
  the law worked. If your harm came from both at once, the law could not see
  it. No one made the gap on purpose. The law was just built so the two
  could not be talked about together.

  This gap is not random. It leaves out the people who are hurt the most.
  These are the people harmed by two things at once. Look at the past. The
  labor movement helped white workers and shut out Black workers. Some who
  fought for white women's right to vote made deals with people who backed
  segregation. The disability rights movement was built around white,
  middle-class disabled people. Each time, the group was built around its
  most powerful members. The people caught in the middle fell through. Who
  fell through was not an accident. It showed whose life had shaped the rules.

Kimberlé Crenshaw arrived at the intersection constantly — in her coursework, in the cases she studied, in the frameworks she was being trained to use. The law could see race. It could see sex. It consistently could not see her — not because she was invisible, but because the categories it used had been built around other people's experiences, and the combination was unspeakable within the available frameworks.

DeGraffenreid v. General Motors made the structure visible as structure. The legal framework available — race discrimination law developed primarily around the experiences of Black men, sex discrimination law developed primarily around the experiences of white women — was adequate for people whose oppression arrived through one axis. For people whose oppression arose from the combination, the framework was invisible. Not because nobody tried to fit them in, but because the architecture made the combination unspeakable.

The gap is not random. It systematically excludes the most vulnerable — the people whose oppression is produced by the combination of categories that each single-axis framework addresses separately. The labor movement that organized white workers and excluded Black workers. The feminist movement that fought for white women's suffrage while forming alliances with segregationists. The disability rights movement built around white, middle-class disabled people. In each case, the framework was built from the most advantaged among its intended constituency. The people at the intersections fell through — and the pattern of who fell through was not coincidental. It reflected whose experience had shaped the framework's construction.
