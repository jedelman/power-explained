Paris has been doing this too. Anne Hidalgo has been mayor since 2014 — almost exactly Colau's tenure. She has closed the Seine quays to cars. She has pushed the fifteen-minute city, removed parking spaces by the thousand, and built bicycle infrastructure where before there was only traffic. Paris on a Sunday in central arrondissements is recognizably different from Paris a decade ago — the plane trees of the Haussmannian boulevards arching over more pedestrians now, fewer cars.

Hidalgo was born in Spain. Her family came to France from Franco's Spain when she was a child. She knows the lineage. But her position is not Colau's position, and the contrast is the lesson.

Hidalgo governs inside the French centralized state. The mayor of Paris is constrained by national authority in ways the mayor of Barcelona — operating inside the Catalan autonomous community — is not. Her political base is the French Socialist Party, not a citizen platform built from assemblies. She is a career politician advancing a progressive program from inside an existing party, against opposition from drivers and conservatives and the right press, and the program has produced real change. But the next mayor can put the parking spaces back. The structure that produced Hidalgo can also produce her opposite.

What Colau had — what Hidalgo does not — is a coalition rooted in the neighborhoods that elected her, with cultural infrastructure older than her mayoralty and longer-lived than it. The Barcelona that Colau changed has an immune system. What Hidalgo is building in Paris is a different kind of power — vulnerable in a different way.
