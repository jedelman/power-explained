A 2020 Oxfam analysis estimated that if unpaid care work worldwide were paid at minimum wage, it would be worth $10.8 trillion annually — more than three times the size of the global tech industry. That labor exists. It is being performed right now, by hundreds of millions of people, overwhelmingly women. It is not in the GDP. It is not in any price. Under every forest is a fungal web threading root to root, older trees feeding seedlings, the whole stand trading sugar and water and warning in the dark — and a forester can log a hillside for years and read the numbers as healthy, because the network keeps delivering out of reserves no ledger ever counted. Care is that web, run under the economy. The $10.8 trillion is the standing crop you can see. The mycorrhizae is everything holding it up, and it has the same terrible property: when you sever it, the canopy stays green for a season, two seasons, a generation — productive enough to hide the wound — and then the forest that was fed for free goes down all at once, and no one can say which cut was the one that did it.
