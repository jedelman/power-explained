In Ghana, structural adjustment required cutting the cocoa marketing board — the state body that had given Ghanaian cocoa farmers some negotiating leverage against international commodity prices. The farmers who had grown cocoa for generations found themselves selling directly into a market controlled by a handful of multinational traders, at prices set in London. The leverage was gone. The extraction continued.

There is a way to take from a field that takes the harvest, and a way that takes the field. The colonial period took the harvest — cocoa, labor, ore — and held the taking in place by force. Structural adjustment took the field. The marketing board was the seed bank: the stored capacity to start again on your own terms. The public services were the moisture in the ground. Burn those out and the land doesn't just yield less — it loses the power to regenerate, season after season, however hard the people on it work. An artificially depleted ground does not recover on its own. Whoever burned out the seed bank owes the seed. That is the case for reparations: not charity for a misfortune — restitution for a removal.
