Michael Taussig finds the same mirror in the image. In *Shamanism, Colonialism, and the Wild Man*, he reads the Putumayo rubber boom — the atrocities against Indigenous people at the turn of the twentieth century — and names the violence the *culture of terror*, the *space of death*. The terror was the medium of extraction, not its failure. And the colony produced something alongside the bodies: the *wild man*, the savage and magical other onto whom the West projected everything it would not face in itself. The wildness was manufactured, then pinned back on its victims as their nature. But Taussig follows the projection further. Decades later, a shaman — named in the book as José García — harnessed that "wild" image the West had cast onto him, and made it a power to heal. The colonizer's own spell, turned back.
