**Abdullah Öcalan** — *democratic confederalism · democratic autonomy*

He spent thirty years building the Kurdish liberation movement — organizing, fighting, surviving, watching comrades die — and trying to build a state. He arrived, from inside that effort, at a conclusion no amount of reading alone could have produced: the state was not the solution. When Turkish forces captured him in 1999 and imprisoned him on Imrali Island, they gave him something unintentional — time to think. In prison he read widely: feminist theory, the Black radical tradition, the history of Kurdish women's organizing. He found Bookchin, whose Social Ecology named the architecture he had been moving toward. What emerged was democratic confederalism: a confederacy of self-governing communes refusing both the nation-state form and the vanguard party. He dropped the demand for a Kurdish nation-state and replaced it with the demand for democratic autonomy — self-governance through communal institutions, not a state apparatus.
