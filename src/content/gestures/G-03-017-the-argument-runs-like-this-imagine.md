The argument runs like this: imagine a pasture open to all. Each herder benefits fully from adding one more animal. The cost of overgrazing is shared. So each herder, acting rationally, adds animals until the pasture is destroyed. The commons cannot govern itself. Privatize or control.

He was not describing any actual commons that had ever existed. He was describing what a commons would look like if you removed everything that makes it work — the communication, the shared norms, the history of relationship, the governance structure, the cook. He described a collection of anonymous strangers with no connection to each other or the land, called it a commons, and concluded it was inherently unstable.
