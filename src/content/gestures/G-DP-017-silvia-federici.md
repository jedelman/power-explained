**Silvia Federici** — *the witch hunts · the enclosure of care*

She was born in Parma, Italy, in 1942, and came to the United States in 1967 for graduate work in philosophy. She arrived in the New York of the early-1970s Wages for Housework campaign, organizing for the recognition of cooking, cleaning, childcare, and the care of the sick and elderly as the reproductive labor that makes wage work possible — and began asking the question that became *Caliban and the Witch* (2004): when had care become invisible as work, and where did the arrangement come from? The answer ran back through the witch trials. Between roughly 1400 and 1700, tens of thousands were executed as witches across Europe and the colonies — the great majority women, many of them the healers, herbalists, and midwives whose knowledge made communities less dependent on the emerging market. Her argument: it was policy.
