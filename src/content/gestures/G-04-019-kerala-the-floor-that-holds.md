Kerala is an Indian state of approximately 35 million people with a per-capita income significantly below the national average. Its life expectancy is roughly 77 years (men 75, women 80, per 2023 estimates) — the highest in India, matching countries with incomes three to four times higher. Infant mortality is India's lowest. Literacy was recorded at 94 percent in the 2011 census, the highest in the country; subsequent surveys put it higher still.

The explanation is not a mystery. Decades of communist-led government that broke up the landlord class, invested in public health and public education, and treated governance as infrastructure for collective wellbeing rather than management of conditions for private accumulation. Strong labor organizing that kept wages above the national norm. Democratic institutions held accountable, over generations, to the organized working class.

Kerala grows its wealth the way Kerala grows pepper: in tiers. The homegarden is the state's signature landscape — coconut and areca in the canopy, pepper vines climbing them, banana and coffee in the middle shade, ginger and turmeric and tuber at the ground, every layer yielding without any layer being stripped. You harvest from all of it, continuously, for generations, because nothing is clear-cut and the soil is never sent away. The other way to farm a hillside is to level it for one cash crop, take the maximum the ground will give, and move on when the dust blows — two good harvests, then a desert. Seventy years of left governance is the first kind of farming applied to a people: the landlord class broken up so the canopy could not monopolize the light, health and education fed back into the ground every season, wages kept above the line that lets a household put down roots. The floor is the kept soil. A floor that holds.
