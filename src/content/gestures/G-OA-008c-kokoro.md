Here is what it gave me, and it is the thing Foucault's analysis cannot reach. The discipline that has actually protected me my whole life is not the physical discipline he describes so precisely — not the coordination, not the technique. The techniques were real and I learned them, but they were never the point. The point was a thing my tradition calls *kokoro* — roughly, indomitability, the discipline of the spirit. I was taught, thoroughly and over years, how not to give up. Not how to overcome, not how to seek the advantage — those are techniques, those are Foucault's territory — but how, when the feeling of defeat starts to creep into the mind, to *cast it aside*. And after thirty years I am only now grasping how completely different a strong spirit is from a rigid body. They are not on the same axis at all. I have won fights I should have lost — real, physical ones, where I was outmatched — on spirit, not skill. There have been moments in my life when spirit was the only thing that kept me on the earth.

That is the answer to what discipline is for, and it is why armor and discipline are opposites. The armored body is rigid because it is afraid; the rigidity is the fear, frozen. *Kokoro* is the opposite of fear and the opposite of rigidity both — it is what is left standing in you when the body is failing and the fear has arrived and you decline, anyway, to collapse inward. I was also taught when to use it, and I will tell you about that part with a deliberate gap in it, because I took an oath. The training is for protection. That is the whole of the instruction I will pass on, and one of the very few oaths I have made that I will not break — not because a contract binds me, contracts are worth the paper they are printed on, but because the spirit that the oath protects is, as far as I can now tell, the literal source of being alive. Connection to spirit sounds like woo until you have a practice that puts you in contact with it and observe that the practice has definite effects. And here is the part that matters for everything else in this book: it does not matter which practice gets you there. However you arrive at the thing — a dojo, a fire in the woods, a meditation cushion, a kitchen full of people who will not let you quit — you will always recognize it once you have touched it, and you will always recognize it in someone else.
