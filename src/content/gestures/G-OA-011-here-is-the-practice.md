So here is the practice, and it is not a regimen, because the floor taught me to distrust anyone who hands a collapsed person a regimen, and it is not a device either — it cannot be bought, sealed, or sat inside; it is not the box. It is older and plainer than any of that. I taught it for years, before I had a word of the politics in this book, back when the only tradition I worked out of was the one that sits down and pays attention.

Buddhist meditation is not analytical and it is not diagnostic, not in the way we usually mean those words. It is — to borrow a phrase, with some irony, from Castaneda — the mastery of attention. And I mean *mastery* the way Liszt mastered the piano, Beethoven the symphony, Miles the trumpet, Rembrandt the face: not mastery as dominance, not power over a thing, but the fluency you earn by returning to the thing ten thousand times until it moves through you without resistance. The Buddha described the mastery of attention and its effects, and demonstrated it — through his teaching, through his discourse, and most of all through what happened to his students. The awareness of an awakened being is called the Lion's Roar: a presence that pulls everyone near it into wide-open, alert attention without engaging the thinking mind at all.

That is what I studied and what I taught, and I will be honest about my own range: my Lion's Roar was barely the cat's meow. But I like to think the sitting did more than keep my students from falling asleep. Drawing on my yoga teacher training, I would mostly just listen to the silence in the room and adjust the collective awareness with a single cue when one was needed. In our best sessions I said nothing and did less. The aphorism I keep is to be like a broken bell — one that does not hold the energy of the strike but releases it as fast as it arrives. The metaphor goes deeper than it first seems, though, because a broken bell does not only fail to ring: it *absorbs* sound from the room around it. Just so, the awareness of every great teacher I have ever sat with absorbs the *citta vritti* — the incoherent oscillations of the mind and body, the same static this book named in its first movement and has been tracking ever since, the thing Reich called armor and the yogis called the unstilled mind. The teacher's open attention takes it in and lets it pass, and yours, sitting near, learns to do the same.

The last image for the work is the gaze of Shiva. True seeing — the immanent awareness of dependent co-arising, of how each thing is held in being by its conditions — obliterates a held form by revealing it as conditional, and revealing the conditions that hold it. The form may reconstitute the instant the gaze moves on; that reconstitution is the physical explanation of karma. But the seeing, continued — open, non-objective, non-dual — accomplishes its release in layers. And none of this is magical. It is only that awareness of the rising and passing away of all phenomena — beginning with something as small as your own breath — turns out to be a compounding thing, a cascade that builds on itself. Even in a fifteen-minute lunch-break sit, in an unoccupied meeting room in Bellevue, under fluorescent light, with your shoes still on.
