The clearest contrast is Naples.

Napoli is the same kind of place as Barcelona in the ways that matter: a Mediterranean port city, working-class spine, anarchist and socialist traditions, a deep food culture, a population that has lived under and survived various empires. By the metrics that should produce a Barcelona, Naples should be one too.

It is not. Naples is the place people leave. The population of the metropolitan area has been declining for decades; young Italians from the South flee north — to Milan, Turin, Rome — for work that does not exist at home. Barcelona is the inverse: the place people come to. Returning Catalans, Spaniards from inland regions where opportunity has collapsed, mobile workers from across Europe.

The contrast cuts deeper. Naples hosts the United States Sixth Fleet — Naval Support Activity Naples, operational headquarters for American military presence across Europe and Africa, in place since the 1950s. Imagine the United States Navy basing the Sixth Fleet in Barcelona. You cannot. The two cities are positioned in the global system as fundamentally different kinds of places — one as a site of extraction, the other as a site of life.

The reasons are everything this chapter has named. Naples had no equivalent of the 1979 transition. The Italian state never devolved power to regional governments in the way Spain did, and the Mezzogiorno never got the political space Catalonia got. Naples has the same depth of memory as Barcelona. It did not get the institutional ground to convert that memory into governance.

Same Mediterranean. Same sun. Different fates.
