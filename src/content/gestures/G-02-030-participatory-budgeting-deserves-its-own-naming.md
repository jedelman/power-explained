Participatory budgeting deserves its own naming. The Brazilian city of Porto Alegre invented the form in 1989: residents of a neighborhood decide directly what portion of the municipal budget gets spent on what. Money allocated by the people who will live with the consequences. Not a town hall meeting where people speak and then officials decide — a real allocation, binding on the city.

The form spread. Porto Alegre's experiment was studied, copied, adapted across dozens of cities globally. By the early 2000s it was a known practice with measurable results: reduced corruption, more equitable infrastructure spending, higher civic participation. The tool existed. It was waiting.

When Barcelona's coalition came to power, it had a tool kit. In 2017, the city built the Decidim platform and released it as open source — federated, modular, available to any city that wanted to use it. The tool turned: built to be given away. Decidim gave participatory budgeting digital scale: every neighborhood could deliberate, propose, vote. Even small allocations changed something. Practice the thing and you become someone for whom the thing is normal.
