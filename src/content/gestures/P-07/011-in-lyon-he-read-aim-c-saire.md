In Lyon he read Aimé Césaire — the Martinican poet who had argued that the violence colonialism exported to the colonies had always been preparing to return to Europe, that fascism was colonialism come home. He read Sartre, Hegel, Merleau-Ponty. He was building a framework. *Black Skin, White Masks*, published in 1952, is that framework in its first form: a young Black intellectual analyzing, from inside, what it does to a person to be raised in a culture that names you inferior, to internalize that naming, to spend your life in the impossible project of proving it wrong to people whose recognition you do not need and cannot stop wanting.
