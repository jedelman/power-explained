Or it's the highway that cut the neighborhood in half in 1965, the one that justified bulldozing people's homes by pointing out they weren't entitled to them, and left a wall of concrete through a community that is still, sixty years later, divided by it. Or the factory that moved its operations across the border and left behind a generation of workers who are now fifty-five and too old to retrain and too young to retire. Or the app that knows everything about you — who you talk to, where you go, what you want — and gives you nothing for it — except access to people who you can now, conveniently, only reach through the app.

They are connected. This book is about how.
