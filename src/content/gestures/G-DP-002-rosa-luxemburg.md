**Rosa Luxemburg** — *freedom for the one who thinks differently · the prediction*

She had been arguing with Lenin since at least 1904 — he writing from exile in London, she in Berlin — over the question that would define the next century: what does a revolution actually require. Against his vanguard of professional revolutionaries she set the soviets themselves, the organic self-governing structures workers had built without being told to. In the summer of 1918, forty-seven years old and held in the women's prison at Breslau for opposing the war, she fed the birds she had named through the bars of her window and wrote *The Russian Revolution* — praising the Bolsheviks' courage and predicting, in the same pages, exactly how their revolution would fail. She was shot in January 1919, five months later; her body was thrown into the Landwehr Canal. The essay was published posthumously in 1922. Stalin demonstrated its accuracy for thirty-five years.
