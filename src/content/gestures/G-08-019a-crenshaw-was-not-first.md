Crenshaw was not first. The Combahee River Collective had named the same structure twelve years earlier, in a different register.

A Black feminist lesbian socialist collective, founded in Boston in 1974 — named for the Combahee River raid of 1863. Harriet Tubman planned that raid and guided the gunboats up the river herself, the first woman in the Civil War to plan and lead an armed raid; more than seven hundred people came out of slavery in a single night. She had spent the decade before it walking people north on foot, in the dark, and never lost one. The reading of the terrain was hers, not the army's — she ran the scouts, mapped the Confederate mines in the river, moved unseen and brought the unseen out behind her. This is the tracker, in the flesh — the craft turned against the men who thought they owned the ground. The lineage carried in the naming itself. Their 1977 statement said it plainly: *the major systems of oppression are interlocking.* If Black women were free, the destruction of all the other systems of oppression would have to follow — because the freedom of those at the intersection of every excluded category cannot be partial.

They were not arguing for a place in the existing categories. They were rupturing the categories so something else could be. The reading was collective. The wizard work was the practice itself — consciousness-raising circles, mutual aid, political education — before there was a theoretical name.
