There's a moment in the Washington Interlude, early in this book: a deer walks into camp, and twenty five-year-olds drop simultaneously into a stalking gait. What I didn't say there is that we had spent the week playing games — games designed to cultivate exactly that quality of attention. Low and slow. Weight placed. Eyes soft, taking in peripheral movement rather than fixing on a point. We had practiced the gait before the deer arrived. When the deer arrived, the children had somewhere to put what they'd been building. The preparation met the moment.

That's the argument this chapter is actually making.
