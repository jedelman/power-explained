Freire's method was the same practice taught as a curriculum. Not: here is the correct analysis of your situation. But: look at this word you already know. What does it contain? What does it touch? Stay with it. The conscientization wasn't installed — it was what happened when people were given the conditions to attend to their own experience without the curriculum that had been busy teaching them not to.

A tracker will tell you that what you can know depends on what you are standing on. In a wetland, the fine silt takes everything — the raccoon's hand-prints, the print of something small that came down to drink, the dragging belly of something that passed before dawn; the ground is so receptive that the smallest life shows up in it, and a truck that drives through registers, correctly, as a catastrophe. On pavement there is almost nothing to read. The surface refuses the impression. You can track on pavement — but only the heavy things, only the machines. Hardt and Negri call consciousness itself a commons — the accumulated shared capacity to perceive, interpret, and act on the world — and it is a commons in exactly the wetland's sense: a receptive ground, kept fine by use, in which the smallest movements of a life can register. Like any commons, it must be built, nourished, and defended. The enclosure of consciousness is not metaphorical: it happens through the curriculum that teaches which questions are answerable, through the media environment that substitutes outrage for attention, through the pace of work that leaves no time for the quality of presence that witnessing requires. Each one pours a little more pavement over the silt. The coup that imprisoned Freire understood this precisely. You don't need to suppress the conclusion if you can enclose the attention that would produce it. Leave the ground unable to take an impression of anything lighter than a truck.

This is the enclosure underneath all the other enclosures. The one that makes the rest possible. When the capacity to perceive clearly is itself enclosed, every other enclosure becomes easier to administer and harder to contest. It is also what the system most needs to take.

What becomes possible when enough of us cultivate that attention — not just hold the right positions, but actually see? Not a revolution in the sense of a single rupture — something harder to suppress, because it doesn't live in a single organization that can be dissolved or a single leader who can be imprisoned or killed. It lives in the accumulated capacity of people who have been present to what is actually happening, who have built or defended or joined something that operates on different assumptions, who have attended carefully enough to know the difference between the simulation and the thing. Roy standing in the water. Freire's student with the pen. The children moving through the forest because we had spent a week preparing them to notice.
