**Carlo Ginzburg** — *the benandanti*

Italian historian, born in Turin in 1939; founder of microhistory, the practice of reading a single small case — often a single trial — closely enough that a whole world shows through it. In *The Night Battles* (1966) he reconstructed the *benandanti*, a Friulian folk tradition whose practitioners believed they went out at night in spirit to do battle to protect the harvest, and showed how the Inquisition gradually reframed them as witches. *Ecstasies: Deciphering the Witches' Sabbath* (1989; English 1991) traced a substrate of shamanic cults beneath the European witch persecutions. Where Federici names the witch hunts as the enclosure of women's and folk knowledge, Ginzburg shows that knowledge had a living core the Inquisition overwrote — the witch as the enclosed knower. His father, Leone Ginzburg — philologist, historian, co-founder of the Einaudi publishing house, anti-fascist — was murdered by the Gestapo in 1944; his mother was the novelist Natalia Ginzburg. The pointed contrast to Eliade, who lent his prestige to fascism.
