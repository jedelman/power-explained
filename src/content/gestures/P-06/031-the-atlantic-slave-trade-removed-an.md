The Atlantic slave trade removed an estimated twelve million people from Africa over roughly four centuries. The world those people came from — the cities and trade networks and governance structures, the craft knowledge fitted into walls without mortar, the political administrations governing millions — went with them. The demographic cost was larger than the number suggests: for every person transported, others died in raids, in coastal holding pens, in the crossing. In the regions most intensively exploited, population growth reversed for generations. The economic impact compounded the demographic — the trade restructured African political economies around slave-raiding rather than production, destroyed the social cooperation that sustained development, created dependencies on European trade goods that made African producers import-dependent. The trade didn't just remove people. It reorganized the continent around European demand.
