Why these neighborhoods specifically? White's analysis is precise: the targeting aimed to wipe out the city's transition neighborhoods where indistinct color lines had failed to produce two distinct school communities, one Black and one white. The Brown v. Board decision in 1954 had made explicit school segregation unconstitutional. The city's response was to destroy the integrated neighborhoods that would have produced integrated schools — to maintain de facto segregation by eliminating the residential conditions that made integration possible. When that wasn't sufficient, Norfolk simply locked its public schools in 1958 [FLAG: six white middle and high schools were closed on September 27, 1958 — not all public schools; approximately 10,000 students were displaced, roughly one-third of whom received no schooling during the closure], leaving thousands of students with nowhere to go.
