Every spring, night herons return to Norfolk, Virginia. They come up from Central America and raise their young in the willow oak trees, and they stay until fall. They're quiet and a little awkward on the ground — heavy-bodied, short-legged, built for stillness rather than speed. But look at one closely, at the red eye and the white plumes and the absolute patience of a bird that can stand motionless for an hour waiting for a fish — and something in you recognizes that it is noble, and ancient, and utterly itself.
