Norfolk holds neighborhood association meetings. I went to several. The neighborhood I'd bought into — Chelsea — is complicated in a way that matters. Walk through it at three in the afternoon and it is overwhelmingly Black. The MHI shipyard workers coming off shift, the people at the 7-Eleven, the bodies moving through the streets. But look at property ownership, residency, voter registration — it's a different picture. The people whose presence constitutes Chelsea's daily life don't live there. They live scattered across the suburbs, where the affordable housing is. And there is no bus stop in Chelsea. Not one. No public transit at all. The dispersion is not incidental. A workforce that cannot anchor itself to a neighborhood cannot constitute a constituency in that neighborhood. The people who are there every day, whose labor built the place, whose presence makes it what it is — they have no neighborhood meeting to show up to. They are extracted from the place without being of it.

So the people at the neighborhood association meetings were overwhelmingly white. And what I saw on their faces when they talked about the neighborhood — their neighborhood, the one they lived in — was fear. Not of anything specific. Of the fact of it. Of the demographics they could see but not account for, the history they sensed without knowing, the 7-Eleven that outperformed every small business in a two-block radius because the workers coming off shift knew better than to come into a wine bar. Fear of the thing they were, technically, a part of.

Then I'd see the same people at the dive bar across the street on a Friday night, laughing, relaxed, fine.
