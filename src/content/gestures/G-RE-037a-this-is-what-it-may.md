This is what it may look like when the channel opens.

We had known they were there — trackers know — but we had not laid eyes on them. Then one afternoon, when the water was low, something in the corner of my eye: a sheen. I turned instinctively. A chunky body, shuffling and snuffling among old oyster shells. The coat said otter, but the shape was wrong — too small, too round, too waddly. A muskrat. Frankly adorable. We stopped — walk, conversation, everything — and stood there for fifteen or twenty minutes while it went about its business. Time flows differently in the wild, and the wild is often not very far from you at all.

After that, we knew where to look. There are at least two of them now. A small constructed wetland nearby — a triumph in its own right. We check it periodically. We hope they have a burrow, a good winter, babies.

They were always there. Something changed when they showed themselves. Something in the texture of happenstance.
