Alexandra Kollontai named what was happening from inside the government: the party had substituted itself for the working class. She argued it in print — *The Workers' Opposition*, published in Pravda in January 1921, made the case that the unions themselves, not the party's bureaucrats, should build the new economy, and that a party that could no longer hear its own workers was already becoming the thing it had overthrown. The Tenth Congress answered in March: it banned the pamphlet and dissolved her faction in the same stroke. Rather than wait to be expelled, she wrote to Stalin and asked to be sent abroad. From 1922 she served in the Soviet missions — Norway first, one of the first women in modern diplomacy — kept useful, kept visible, and kept permanently away from the rooms where what she had named was completing itself.

She made Gramsci's diagnosis in real time, eight years before the prison notebooks — not from a cell but from inside the apparatus, while it could still have been heard. She outlived him by fifteen years, watching from embassy windows while the prediction ran its course. The silencing did not need a prison. A sufficiently distant embassy would do.
