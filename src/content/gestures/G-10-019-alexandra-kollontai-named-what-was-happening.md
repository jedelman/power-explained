  Alexandra Kollontai worked inside the government. She saw what was happening
  and said it out loud. The party had taken the place of the working class. It
  acted for the workers instead of letting them act. She wrote this down in a
  book called The Workers' Opposition.

  The party did two things at once. It banned her book. It broke up her group.

  Then the party sent her to another country. She was still useful. She was
  still seen. But she was far away from the rooms where the thing she warned
  about was coming true.

  She said the same thing Gramsci would later say. She said it eight years
  before he wrote his prison notebooks. He wrote his from a jail cell. She said
  hers from inside the party, while people could still hear her. She lived
  fifteen years longer than he did. She watched it all happen from far away.
  The party did not need a jail to silence her. Sending her far away was
  enough.

Alexandra Kollontai named what was happening from inside the government: the party had substituted itself for the working class. She argued it in print — *The Workers' Opposition* — and the party answered by banning the argument and dissolving her faction in the same stroke. Then it sent her abroad — kept useful, kept visible, and kept permanently away from the rooms where what she had named was completing itself.

She made Gramsci's diagnosis in real time, eight years before the prison notebooks — not from a cell but from inside the apparatus, while it could still have been heard. She outlived him by fifteen years, watching from embassy windows while the prediction ran its course. The silencing did not need a prison. A sufficiently distant embassy would do.
