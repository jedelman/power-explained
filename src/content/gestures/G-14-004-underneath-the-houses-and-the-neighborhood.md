  Below the houses and the people and the way they lived together
  was land. It was real land in a real city. The people who lived on
  it owned it. They used it and shared it in their own ways, built up
  over time.

  Then the city took the land. The city used eminent domain. That is
  a law that lets the city take land. The city also called the area a
  slum, but that was not really true. And the people there did not
  have enough power to fight back. When the land was taken, the base
  of their whole life was taken too.

Underneath the houses and the neighborhood and the social fabric: specific parcels in a specific city, held by the people who lived on them, organized by use and relationship and history. When the city took that land — through eminent domain, through the manufactured slum designation, through the simple fact that the people living there lacked the political power to resist — it took the foundation.
