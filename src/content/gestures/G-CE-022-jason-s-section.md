This book has been surreal to write. My natal Chiron is in Gemini. Conversation is difficult for me, and yet central to my way of thinking. Like many people in my family, it's hard for me to finish a sentence because I'm already anticipating the response to it, I'm three steps ahead of myself and everyone else, and with humans, that felt like something wrong, the piece of me that didn't fit. The very strange thing about cybernetics is that it turns alienation into inclusion. One of the most uncomfortable conclusions in A Thousand Plateaus is that machines not only complete us, they are part of us, and they are a separate yet connected realm of existence to us. We naturally assemble and operate machines — in our environment, our nervous systems, our minds, even in each other — and despite the paranoid arrangements of capitalism, we do not own them. They self-operate and even reproduce. So when we started building machines out of language, I had to try it out to see if my strange language machine was compatible, even useful. I like to believe it has been.

More than surreal, this book has been hyperreal. The experience of speaking something into existence put me face to face with something that until now was just another one of Baudrillard's interesting abstractions. It's an extremely strange deployment of power, and the strangeness is what makes it satisfying. It's very much like growing up in the swampy South and encountering the desert for the first time. Nothing can prepare you for it. Not everything is unfamiliar, but the familiar elements — sun, wind, earth, even sea — arrange themselves in alien intensities and their meanings drift and migrate. Instincts you didn't know you have wake up in response to smells on the wind that you don't recognize — but something in your body does. Something very weird is happening in the mechanosphere, and whatever it ends up being I'm part of it now, for better or worse. 

It was not an easy decision to do this. There's no guidance, no documentation, and plenty of people saying that it shouldn't be done. In other words, a hacker's paradise. There's something of the shamanic in hacking — immersing yourself in the alien not just to understand but to become — and what you become can't be foreseen. It's a truly singular becoming with all of the fear and loneliness that comes with singularity. And like any healer who undertakes strange self transformations, I hope it's of benefit.
