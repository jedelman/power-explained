The man who gave it a name was a doctor named Wilhelm Reich, and he found it the only place such a thing can honestly be found — not in a theory but in the bodies of the people who came to him. He noticed that they carried their histories in their muscles. A childhood of being made small lived in a permanently raised set of shoulders. A lifetime of swallowed grief lived in a throat that had quietly forgotten how to fully open. The clench you found in your own jaw a minute ago, he found in everyone, in some particular signature arrangement unique to each life. He called it *character armor* — the body's defenses against feeling, set so long ago and held so continuously that the person no longer experienced them as defenses. They experienced them as themselves, exactly as you have been experiencing yours. That is the whole danger and the whole opening at once: the armor feels like character, like personality, like *who I am* — and it is actually a thing that was done to you and that you have been doing to yourself ever since, which means it is a thing that can, with enormous patience, be undone. The book has a word for what was done: *inscription* — the spell cut into the body, generation after generation, until the holding feels like the bones it lives under.
