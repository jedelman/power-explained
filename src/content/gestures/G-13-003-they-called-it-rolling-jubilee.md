They called it Rolling Jubilee — named for the biblical tradition of debt cancellation, the periodic zeroing-out of obligations that ancient societies understood was necessary to prevent debt from becoming permanent domination. With that money they abolished nearly $15 million in debt. The debtors included people who had been carrying the weight of a medical emergency for years, who had stopped answering the phone because they didn't recognize the number, who had arranged their lives around the fact that this money was owed and would never be paid. Then it wasn't.

Among those who received a Rolling Jubilee letter was a woman in her early thirties who had incurred $3,200 in emergency room debt when she had no insurance, had been transferred three times to collection agencies she didn't recognize, and had spent two years trying to figure out who she owed and why the number kept changing. The Debt Collective kept debtor identities private. The letter lifted a specific dread — owing money to people who have no interest in you except as a source of extraction — that had organized her relationship to her phone for two years.

Strike Debt grew into the Debt Collective, which organized the first student debt strike in American history and won $5.8 billion in debt cancellation for students defrauded by for-profit colleges. The logic was Graeber's: debt is a social relationship that has been weaponized. The weapon can be turned.
