*The birds remember.*

Not metaphorically. The doves in our yard greet us differently than they greet the neighbors. The finches, wrens, and house sparrows do not flush at our approach the way they flush at strangers. The bluejays announce us. The squirrels watch us with a different posture. The grackles, when they come through in season, treat us as known terrain. The animals around the house recognize us — individually — and respond to us individually, because we have addressed them, individually, for years. We talk to them. They notice.

Corvids, famously, hold human faces for years and pass the information to their young. Other beings remember in shorter arcs. And some forms of memory exceed any single body — the recognition passes between organisms, between generations, between species. Memory here goes transorganic: Deleuze and Guattari territory, Simondon's pre-individual field at work. The living world keeps track of who has paid attention, who has spoken kindly, who has thrown the rock, who has walked past five hundred times without looking up.

You are already in that ledger. The only question is what you will write next to your name.
