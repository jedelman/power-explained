  W.E.B. Du Bois wrote a book called Black Reconstruction in America
  in 1935. He took this idea further. He asked a hard question. White
  workers and Black workers needed many of the same things. So why did
  white workers keep siding with their race instead of their class? Du
  Bois said the system gave white workers something real. Not extra
  money. A kind of social pay. They got to count as fully human. They
  were protected by law. They could be seen in politics. They were
  spared the worst, cruelest treatment. They counted as people whose
  pain mattered. Du Bois called this the wages of whiteness.

  This pay was real. But taking it cost something. To get it, a white
  worker had to act like he was part of the class above him. He had to
  side with the bosses and the owners. But the bosses wanted very
  different things than he did. He had to treat Black workers not as
  people on his side but as the thing he was not. Taking this pay made
  the people who took it worse. Every day, they had to keep up a
  loyalty that hurt them in money terms. In return they got a status
  that kept them safe in social terms. The system offered this trade
  on purpose. It needed the working class split apart. The split is
  what it got.

  One action does both things at once. It pulls one group together by
  pushing the other group out of being human.

  This is not saying white people are sick. It is saying the system is
  built a certain way. The system is set up so that siding with your
  race pays off faster than siding with your class. That setup has
  never been taken apart.

W.E.B. Du Bois, in *Black Reconstruction in America* (1935), extended this analysis into something that cuts close: why white workers, who shared many material interests with Black workers, so consistently chose racial solidarity over class solidarity. The system, he argued, offered white workers something real — not a paycheck but a social wage. Access to the category of the fully human, the legally protected, the politically visible. Freedom from the most brutal forms of discipline. The status of the person whose suffering counts. Du Bois called these the *wages of whiteness*.

They were real wages. And accepting them cost something. To claim the wages of whiteness required performing an identification with the class above — with the employers, the owners, people whose material interests sharply diverged from white workers' own — rather than with the class alongside. It required treating Black workers not as people with shared interests but as the thing you were not. The acceptance degraded the people who accepted it. It required maintaining, daily, a solidarity that worked against them economically in exchange for a status that protected them socially. The system offered that trade because it needed the working class fragmented. It got what it paid for.

One motion: gather one category into being by casting the other out of the human.

This is not an argument about white pathology. It is an argument about structural incentives. The system is organized to make racial solidarity more immediately valuable than class solidarity. That organization has never been dismantled.
