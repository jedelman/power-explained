The floor is not a metaphor and it is not nothing. It is a place, with its own geography, and if you are on it right now I do not want to lie to you about it by making it sound like either failure or rest. It was neither, and it was stranger than both.

For the first time in my life I had nothing to do and no one telling me I had to do it. I could just *be* — and it turned out I had no idea how. It was profoundly disorienting. I cried a few times. I went through several entire existential crises without leaving my own head. Mostly I was inert. Some days Erin had a shift at a coffee shop and I would ride along just to be somewhere; they had a couch and a guitar, and I would pluck at the guitar and read. That is where I finally read Reich, all of him — *The Sexual Revolution*, *Character Analysis*, *The Mass Psychology of Fascism* — borrowed from the Redmond public library a few blocks away. So the man whose diagnosis runs through this whole chapter, I met for real while I was lying on the floor he was describing, by way of a public library that handed a broken stranger the most dangerous books in its catalog for free. The commons producing, as it does, something well in excess of what it was funded to produce. I studied ferociously, when I studied at all — esoteric Buddhism, Western and Chinese herbalism, Reich's somatotherapy. The mind that had collapsed at work would not collapse at this; it had only ever refused the thing it was being spent on.

Other days I stayed home. We lived in the woods on five acres, with a very small flock of chickens, every one of them named. They taught me bird language as much as any human ever has. I cooked. I wrote music. I did not have to carry water, but I chopped a great deal of wood. Many days we walked the valley trails — old railroad grades, the grade so gentle because a train once needed it to be — and somewhere out on one of them I caught myself asking, with genuine confusion, *is this what it's like to be retired?*
