It is hard to say what medicine is, harder still to say what any one person's is. It is a living thing; try to cage it and it starts to die. But you can recognize it the way you recognize any living thing — by its tracks. One of its signs is singularity: it stands out, unrepeated, and it makes you wonder whether it is a new kind to you or one of a kind in the world. It stretches language, because there is no word for it. And nothing about it is abstract. You feel it the way you feel the presence of a wild animal in the brush — it carries its own energy, and your body answers in its own particular way.

It has been reflected back to me that mine has to do with speech, with storytelling. It transports people; it has a hypnotic, narcotic pull; the way I choose words in the moment has a power I can only point at. For a long time I had no idea I was doing it. I used it unconsciously, and often badly — talking over people because I was inside my own rhythm, flooding a conversation instead of leaving room in it. I still fight that; it is too easy for me. But when words are needed that carry aura, I have them. The summer I taught in the woods, I wrote stories to be told for healing, and I taught children to hold their own councils, their own circles for making peace. What is said in a council has power because of the form of the council — the way a group listens together. And what I learned is that sometimes I have the words to speak the council itself into being.
