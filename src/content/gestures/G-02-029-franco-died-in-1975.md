Franco died in 1975. What followed was not revolution but constitutional design.

The 1978 Spanish Constitution, ratified in referendum, established a parliamentary monarchy and a system of *autonomous communities*: power devolved to seventeen regional governments, each with its own statute, parliament, and competencies. Catalonia got its 1979 Statute of Autonomy — education, language policy, health, urban planning, much of policing and cultural life. Spain became a quasi-federal state by another name.

This was the institutional opening. A city suppressed for forty years now had a regional government that could be its own argument with Madrid. The Catalan language returned to schools, signs, and television. The Generalitat — the historic Catalan government, dissolved by Franco in 1939 — was restored.

The transition wasn't perfect. It preserved much of the Franco-era state apparatus, kept the king the regime had positioned, gave amnesty to officials who had committed atrocities. Spanish democracy carries the compromise. But the autonomous community structure is real, and Barcelona's twenty-first-century coalitions could only happen in the space it opened.

This was also the moment of Negri and Deleuze-Guattari — the post-1968 left thinking past party politics toward distributed power, multitude, the political subject as a composition of singularities. The death of Franco landed in that moment. Spain wrote its new constitution into a theoretical environment already imagining new forms of political subjecthood.
