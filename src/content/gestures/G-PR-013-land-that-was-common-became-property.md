To create this system of privilege, land that was common had to become property. The people who farmed it for generations were pushed out, became landless, had to sell their labor to survive. Care that was communal — healing, childcare, the tending of the elderly — became unpaid domestic labor, then a convenience for those who can afford it, an unaffordable luxury for the rest of us, a multitrillion-dollar industry. Knowledge that was shared became intellectual property. Credit, which began as a social relationship between neighbors who trusted each other, became a product that extracts from the people who need it most. The most fundamental decisions about the places you live became something administered on your behalf, or simply done to you.
