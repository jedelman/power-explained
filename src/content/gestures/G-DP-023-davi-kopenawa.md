**Davi Kopenawa** — *the falling sky*

Yanomami shaman and Portuguese-speaking spokesperson for the Yanomami people in Brazil, born around 1956 near the Rio Toototobi. He works with the xapiri — the shaman spirits at the center of Yanomami cosmology — and has spent decades campaigning to expel illegal gold miners (*garimpeiros*) and rubber tappers from Yanomami land, work that has drawn death threats. He co-founded the Hutukara Yanomami Association in 2004; following his international campaigning, Brazil officially recognized Yanomami land in 1992; he and Hutukara received the Right Livelihood Award in 2019. His account is set down in his own words in *The Falling Sky: Words of a Yanomami Shaman*, told to and composed with the French anthropologist Bruce Albert (*La Chute du ciel*, 2010; English, Harvard University Press, 2013) — at once autobiography, ethnography, and cosmology. Where the book elsewhere draws on a scholar of shamanism, Kopenawa is the practitioner's own voice: a shaman defending a forest commons against extraction, answering the appropriation problem by speaking for the land himself rather than being spoken for.
