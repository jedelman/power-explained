Gramsci arrived at the University of Turin in 1911 on a scholarship — into the city of Fiat, of steel and industrial noise and a working class that was nothing like the Sardinian peasants he had grown up among. He discovered Marx and the industrial proletariat in the same semester, in the same city, as though the theory and its subject had been waiting for each other. By 1919 he was at the center of the council movement, theorizing it in real time in *L'Ordine Nuovo*, the journal he edited, arguing that the councils were the embryonic institutions of a new society.

When the occupations ended, when Mussolini rose, when the left that had seemed so powerful collapsed into its opposite — the same workers who had occupied factories now joining Blackshirt rallies — Gramsci was left with a question that refused release. The material conditions for change had been there. The organizational capacity had been there. The moment had been there. Why had the terrain not held?
