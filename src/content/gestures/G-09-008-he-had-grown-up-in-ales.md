He had grown up in Ales, Sardinia, the fourth of seven children of a minor civil servant who was imprisoned for five years on embezzlement charges when Gramsci was seven [CORRECTION: "three years" → "five years" | Francesco Gramsci was convicted in 1898 and released around 1904; sources consistently give approximately five years as the sentence length] — leaving the family in poverty, leaving Gramsci to work in a land registry office as a child to help support them. He was small, hunched from a childhood spinal condition, brilliant. He won a scholarship to the University of Turin in 1911 — and arrived in the city of Fiat, of steel and industrial noise and a working class that was nothing like the Sardinian peasants he had grown up among, discovering Marx and the industrial proletariat in the same semester, in the same city, as though the theory and its subject had been waiting for each other. By 1919 he was at the center of the council movement, theorizing it in real time in *L'Ordine Nuovo*, the journal he edited, arguing that the councils were the embryonic institutions of a new society.

When the occupations ended, when Mussolini rose, when the left that had seemed so powerful collapsed into its opposite — the same workers who had occupied factories now joining Blackshirt rallies — Gramsci was left with a question that refused release. The material conditions for change had been there. The organizational capacity had been there. The moment had been there. Why had the terrain not held?
