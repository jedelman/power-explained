What happened when the deer walked through camp was magic. The book uses that word in its strict sense. Magic is what happens when singularities compose in common — when irreducible particular things meet and, in meeting, become a momentary unity that did not exist before. The deer carried *the secret ways* — the forest's irreducible particularity that no grid can map. Each child carried *the gift* — their singular angle on the world, the body they had inherited from generations whose names they don't know. They did not learn the stalking gait. They remembered it. The body is what you have in common with your ancestors. When the deer appeared, the singular vantage in each child composed with the deer's presence and with each other's bodies into one moment of recognition. That recognition is magic — the subjective experience of singularities coming into common, like the climax of a symphony when the chord that has been gathering finally arrives and you feel it before you can name it.
