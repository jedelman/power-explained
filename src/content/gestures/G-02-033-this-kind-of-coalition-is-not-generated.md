This kind of coalition is not generated from nothing. Barcelona's working class has been organizing itself for a long time, and the city remembers.

In 1936, when Franco's forces moved against the elected government, Barcelona was the place where the people most decisively did not let them in. Anarchist unions and antifascist militias ran the city. Factories were collectivized. The trams kept running, the trains kept running, the bread kept getting baked — under workers' councils, not bosses. It was a real social revolution, and Franco needed three years and the collapse of international solidarity to defeat it. The city fell in January 1939.

What followed was forty years of dictatorship: executions, exile, the Catalan language banned in schools and the press, neighborhood organizations driven underground. But the memory did not die. It survived in kitchen tables, in songs, in the persistence of the language under suppression, in the slow rebuilding of cultural infrastructure after Franco's death in 1975.

When the assemblies of Barcelona en Comú began meeting in 2014, they were not inventing a new form. They were activating an old one. The neighborhood assemblies of the twenty-first century rhyme with the workers' assemblies of 1936 because they come out of the same city, the same memory, the same refusal.
