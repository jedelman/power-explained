**Mircea Eliade** — *the sacred*

Romanian historian of religions (1907–1986). He studied Sanskrit and yoga in Calcutta under Surendranath Dasgupta, taught in Paris after the war, and from 1964 held the History of Religions chair at the University of Chicago, where he served as editor-in-chief of Macmillan's *Encyclopedia of Religion*. His lifelong argument: the modern disenchanted world is the anomaly — for almost all of human history people lived in a reality charged with the sacred, structured by what he called hierophany, by a sacred time that returns rather than runs out, and by the initiatory descent he traced across the world's shamanic cultures. His record is inseparable from it. In the late 1930s he gave his prestige to Romania's fascist Iron Guard, writing for its press; arrested in 1938, he refused to sign a dissociation; he served the wartime regimes as a cultural attaché and never publicly reckoned with any of it afterward. The sacred is a terrain — and he is the witness that it can be cast either way.
