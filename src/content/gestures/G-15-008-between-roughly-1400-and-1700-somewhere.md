Between roughly 1400 and 1700, between 40,000 and 60,000 people were executed as witches across Europe and the American colonies — roughly half of the 100,000 or so brought to trial. About eighty percent of those killed were women. Most were over forty. Many were widows holding land or income of their own. Most were healers, herbalists, midwives — women whose knowledge of bodies and plants and birth made communities less dependent on the emerging market.

The body count understates the impact. The women killed were already the ones most likely to be holding what the new economy needed to take: communal knowledge, autonomous land tenure, the practical knowhow that let villages reproduce themselves without the wage. Two hundred years of trials systematically removed that demographic from the European landscape. The terror reached far beyond the executed: every woman watching learned what knowing too much, owning too much, or living too independently could cost.

The standard historical account treats this as mass hysteria overcome by Enlightenment.

Federici's argument: it was policy. Divination — naming what the surface concept had hidden.
