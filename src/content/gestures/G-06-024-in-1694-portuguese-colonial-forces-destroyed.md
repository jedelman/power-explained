In 1694, Portuguese colonial forces destroyed Palmares — a quilombo in northeast Brazil, a community of escaped enslaved people that had maintained independence for nearly a century. At its height it held around twenty thousand people, governed by a king who ruled in council with elected representatives from each settlement, organized around communal agriculture and collective defense, trading with neighboring villages on its own terms. It lasted through constant military pressure, through repeated assault, because it had built the governance structures that made collective action possible. Ostrom would recognize it: bounded community, self-developed rules, monitoring, conflict resolution, graduated response to violation. A commons built under conditions explicitly designed to prevent it. That is magic in the strict sense — singularities composing into something the system said could not exist.

Palmares is not a footnote to the Black radical tradition. It is a demonstration of what that tradition is: people classified as outside the human, building rigorous collective governance because their position forced clarity about what collective self-governance required.
