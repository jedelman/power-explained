  Fanon was raised on a promise. The promise said anyone could
  become civilized. You just had to take on its ways. It said the
  gap between a colonial subject and a citizen could be closed. You
  closed it with school and good manners. But the promise had
  rules it would never admit. His book is *Black Skin, White
  Masks*. It came out in 1952. It holds his ideas in their first
  form. In it, a young Black thinker looks at his own life from
  inside. He asks what it does to you. You are raised in a culture.
  That culture says you are less. You start to believe it about
  yourself. You spend your life trying to prove it wrong. You try
  to prove it to people whose view you do not need. And yet you
  cannot stop wanting it.

The assimilation project that raised Fanon — the promise that civilization was achievable by anyone willing to adopt its forms, that the distance between the colonial subject and the metropolitan citizen could be crossed by education and cultivation — was conditional in ways it would never acknowledge. *Black Skin, White Masks*, published in 1952, is the framework in its first form: a young Black intellectual analyzing, from inside, what it does to a person to be raised in a culture that names you inferior, to internalize that naming, to spend your life in the impossible project of proving it wrong to people whose recognition you do not need and cannot stop wanting.
