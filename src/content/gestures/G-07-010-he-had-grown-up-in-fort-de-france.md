He had grown up in Fort-de-France, Martinique — a middle-class Creole family, the French assimilation project, the promise that culture was universal, that civilization was achievable by anyone willing to adopt its forms, that the distance between the colonial subject and the metropolitan citizen could be crossed by education and cultivation. He was fourteen when Martinique came under Vichy control and the Vichy officers' casual racism made legible what the assimilation project had been organized to hide: the promise was conditional in ways it would never acknowledge. He volunteered to fight for Free France at eighteen, shipped to Europe, fought in Alsace, was decorated. He returned briefly to Martinique, then went to Lyon for medical school.

In Lyon he read Aimé Césaire — the Martinican poet who had argued that the violence colonialism exported to the colonies had always been preparing to return to Europe, that fascism was colonialism come home. He read Sartre, Hegel, Merleau-Ponty. He was building a framework. *Black Skin, White Masks*, published in 1952, is that framework in its first form: a young Black intellectual analyzing, from inside, what it does to a person to be raised in a culture that names you inferior, to internalize that naming, to spend your life in the impossible project of proving it wrong to people whose recognition you do not need and cannot stop wanting.
