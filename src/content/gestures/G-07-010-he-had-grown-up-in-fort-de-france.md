The assimilation project that raised Fanon — the promise that civilization was achievable by anyone willing to adopt its forms, that the distance between the colonial subject and the metropolitan citizen could be crossed by education and cultivation — was conditional in ways it would never acknowledge. *Black Skin, White Masks*, published in 1952, is the framework in its first form: a young Black intellectual analyzing, from inside, what it does to a person to be raised in a culture that names you inferior, to internalize that naming, to spend your life in the impossible project of proving it wrong to people whose recognition you do not need and cannot stop wanting.
