Armor does something strange in a group, something Reich saw clearly in *The Mass Psychology of Fascism*: it *resonates*. Put armored bodies together and the holding does not cancel out — it amplifies, each clenched body giving the others permission to clench harder, which is the rally, the mob, the parade, the whole machinery of the strongman running on nothing but resonant armor. In most rooms, in most of the West, that is what a crowd does to you: it tightens what is already tight. But resonance has another setting, and there is at least one place on the West Coast — which I will protect by not naming — where I learned the other setting exists. In that community the resonance ran the other way. The shared practices were built, knowingly, to shake the armor loose instead of locking it down: singing together, moving together, sitting still together, laughing. And underneath the practices was a particular quality of regard. Every person, even the brand-new ones, even me arriving half-dead off the floor, was held in a kind of curious attention. There was a question nobody had to ask out loud because everyone was always already asking it: *what is your medicine?*

The way it worked was that the people who had been there a while had some sense of what theirs might be, and they were out at the edge of it, exploring. So someone would sing. Someone would lead the group in a silly little yoga-dance. Someone would tell a story that came not just through their voice but through their whole body. And it wakes something up in you — something different in each person, but the awake part gets recognized, and recognizes the same awake part in someone else. Nobody had it figured out. Everybody was paying attention. And when everyone around you is paying attention, you get pulled up out of your own head, and if you have even a little curiosity left you start to wonder what they are all paying attention to. That curiosity, that wonder, can do absolutely everything — if no one around you shuts it down, if no one puts you in your place or pulls you back in line. The only place that community ever put you was a place of respect: for the earth, for the ancestors, for life itself. And they knew — they were *remembering* — that you cannot just put a person there and expect them to stay. People do not stay where they are merely placed. You have to sing them there, welcome them there, invite them there, make the place so beautiful and so alive with your own eloquence that they never want to leave — that they would sooner die than go back to the holding. I know it worked, because when my time there ended, it felt like dying to leave.
