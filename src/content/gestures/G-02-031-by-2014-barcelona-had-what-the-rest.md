By 2014, Barcelona had what the rest of Spain did not yet have: a coalition ready to govern.

The 15M movement — the squares movement of 2011, when Spaniards by the millions occupied plazas to refuse the post-2008 austerity consensus — had not produced a party. It produced something stranger: a culture of assembly. Culture in the fermenter’s sense — a living thing, particular to its vessel, useless without a substrate to work on.

Occupy Wall Street erupted in the same year on the same impulse, and produced the same thing. In the United States, the culture of assembly dissolved without leaving an institutional shadow — Zuccotti Park cleared by police. In Spain, most of the assemblies also dissolved. But in a few places they crystallized into something durable. The difference was not the will of the people in the parks. It was the ground the parks sat on. The same starter, poured onto different ground. You can smell the difference in soil before you can say it — freshness or rot, ripeness or desiccation. One ground had been fed for decades: turned, inoculated by every generation of practice that survived in it. The other had been paved.

Barcelona en Comú — Barcelona in Common — was born from that crystallization. It was not a party. It was a rhizome — a citizen platform built bottom-up from neighborhood assemblies, sending shoots up wherever the soil was good: PAH activists, Podemos cadres, ecologist groups, neighborhood associations, the green-left, independents who had never run for anything. The platform asked its assemblies what the program should be. The assemblies decided.

This is what years of organizing infrastructure make possible: when the political opening came, the coalition was already built. The CNT memory, the autonomous community framework, the participatory practice — these had not been waiting. They had been preparing. The lineage acting in the present, summoned by the opening.
