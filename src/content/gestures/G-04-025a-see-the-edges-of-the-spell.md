See the edges of the spell, too. The commons does not replace the market. No one has yet solved what the price system solves — how to coordinate the wants of millions of strangers who will never meet — and the commons does not pretend to have. It takes the other ground: the things the market prices wrong, or cannot price at all, the things that die the moment they are sold. And these scales are not one instrument. A fridge on a sidewalk is a gift. Seventy thousand workers is a federation. Thirty-five million is a state — Kerala is not a commons in the strict sense but a government that chose the floor, and the difference is worth keeping straight. One refusal runs through all of them: that what we need to live should not be enclosed. The commons grows where enclosure overreached — beside the market, inside it, in the ground it left unpriced. Not the whole world. The part worth holding.
