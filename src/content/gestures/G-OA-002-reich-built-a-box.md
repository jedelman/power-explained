That is what the name cost. Reich did not die comfortable and forgotten; he died punished. Late in his life, in America, he built a box — the orgone accumulator, a cabinet of layered metal and organic material he believed concentrated a healing life energy he called *orgone*, that a person could sit inside to be restored. The box does not work. There is no orgone; this is the part where the brilliant man went wrong. But watch what the government did about a cabinet that does nothing. In 1956 a federal judge ordered Reich's writings burned, and federal agents burned them — more than six tons of books and journals and papers, the only court-ordered incineration of an author's entire body of work in the history of the United States. Reich died the next year in a federal penitentiary in Lewisburg, Pennsylvania, serving two years for contempt of court, found dead in his cell about one day before he was eligible to apply for parole. Governments do not burn six tons of paper over a box that does nothing. They burn it over what the man spent his life saying. And to see what he had been saying — the thing worth six tons of fire — you have to follow the name out of the consulting room and into the street.
