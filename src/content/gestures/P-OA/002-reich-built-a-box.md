Wilhelm Reich built a box. He called it an orgone accumulator — a cabinet of layered metal and organic material that he believed concentrated a life energy he named *orgone*, and that a person could sit inside to be restored. The box does not work. There is no orgone. And the United States government, faced with this harmless and ineffective cabinet, did something it has done to no other book in its history: in 1956 a federal judge ordered Reich's writings burned, and federal agents burned them — more than six tons of books and journals and papers, the only court-ordered incineration of an author's entire body of work in American history. Reich died the next year in a federal penitentiary in Lewisburg, Pennsylvania, serving two years for contempt of court, one day before he was eligible to apply for parole. I am not asking you to believe in the box. I am asking you to notice the size of the response. Governments do not burn six tons of paper over a cabinet that does nothing. They burn it over what the man spent his life saying, and to understand why two governments on two continents wanted this particular physician gone, you have to know what he was actually doing before the box.
