So here is the practice, and it is not a regimen, because the floor taught me to distrust anyone who hands a collapsed person a regimen. It is smaller than that, and it has two halves, and I am going to tell you the first half the way I used to teach it — because I did teach it, for years, before I had any of the politics in this book, when the only tradition I was working out of was the one that taught me to sit down and pay attention to a body.

The first half is the mindfulness-of-the-body move that disrupts holding: how you actually taught people to locate a held place and let the locating be the whole of it, without the locating becoming one more thing to do well. Name that you taught it, and the lineage if you want it named. Claude's placeholder version below is convergent guesswork on the technique — replace it with the real instruction. (Placeholder, to be cut: "Once today, notice where you are holding — the jaw, the shoulders, the breath you take shallow so as not to feel the bottom of it. Do not try to fix it; trying to fix it is more holding. Just locate one place where the armor is, and let the locating be the whole of it.")}}

That is the first half, and you can do it alone. The second half is harder and you cannot do it alone, which is the point: notice the next time you are in the presence of other people and your body lets a held thing go without your permission — the shoulders that drop when a particular person walks in, the breath that finally reaches the bottom around a particular table. Mark that. That table, that person, that fire — that is where the medicine is kept. The enclosure has spent a great deal of money convincing you the medicine is the box. It is not the box. It was never the box. It is the people around it.
