Before the box, Reich was a physician, and the disease he set himself against was fascism. Not the politics of it — the *body* of it. He had watched ordinary people in Germany turn toward a movement that would devour them, vote against their own lives, line up for a man who openly despised them, and he refused to call this stupidity or evil, because those are not diagnoses and he was a doctor. He asked the doctor's question instead: what is the condition that makes a population *want* this? His answer, published in 1933 as *The Mass Psychology of Fascism*, was that a body raised in suppression — taught from childhood to hold, to obey, to distrust its own aliveness — arrives at adulthood already shaped to receive a strongman. The armor was the illness. Fascism was what the armored body did when offered permission. And the thing about a disease is that it can, in principle, be treated. Reich treated it. He thought you could *un*-make a fascist by un-making the armor, one body at a time, and he spent years trying.

This is why he had no country. The Nazis banned the book and he fled Germany ahead of them. But here is the part the comfortable reader needs to slow down for: the Communists threw him out too. The German Communist Party expelled him in 1933 — the same year, over the same book — because his diagnosis did not stop at the border of the Reich. An armored body is an armored body. A population trained to hold and obey will line up behind a strongman whether the strongman wears a swastika or a red star, and Reich was honest enough, or reckless enough, to say so out loud. By his own account the book was attacked and denounced as *counterrevolutionary* by the Communist Party leadership in Denmark and in Norway, the countries he fled to next. He had built a diagnosis that worked on Stalinists, and a diagnosis that works on your own side is the one nobody on your own side will forgive. The alternative he proposed was not a better strongman. It was what he called a *workers' democracy* — the people who do the actual work making the actual decisions, which is the same un-owned, un-bossed thing this entire book has been circling, arriving here in the unlikeliest place, the consulting room of a disgraced sex doctor who was right.
