Here is what he was right about. Before the box, before the prison, Reich noticed that the people who came to him carried their histories in their muscles. A childhood of being made small lived in a permanently raised set of shoulders. A lifetime of swallowed grief lived in a throat that had quietly forgotten how to fully open. He called this *character armor* — the body's defenses against feeling, set so long ago and held so continuously that the person no longer experienced them as defenses. They experienced them as themselves. And he made one further claim, the dangerous one, the one that got attention: that a population of armored bodies is the raw material of fascism. A body that cannot feel its own aliveness will go looking for aliveness in the parade, in the strongman, in the permission to finally let the held thing out as rage at someone weaker. The armor does not prevent the explosion. It guarantees it, and it aims it downward. This is the same thing the yogis meant by the oscillations of the mind that have never been calmed, the same thing the book has been calling enclosure, arriving now in the one place we have not yet looked for it: your own neck, your own jaw, the breath you are holding right now without having decided to.
