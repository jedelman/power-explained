The floor is not a metaphor and it is not nothing. It is a place, with its own geography, and if you are on it right now I do not want to lie to you about it by making it sound like either failure or rest. It was neither. It was a specific room at a specific hour and the day had a shape even when I had none.

For the first time in my life I had nothing to do and no one telling me I had to do it. I could just *be*, and it was profoundly disorienting — I had no idea how. I cried a few times. I went through several entire existential crises without leaving my own head. Mostly I was inert. Some days Erin had a shift at a coffee shop and I would ride along just to be somewhere; they had a couch and a guitar, and I would pluck at the guitar and read. That is where I finally read Reich, all of him — *The Sexual Revolution*, *Character Analysis*, *The Mass Psychology of Fascism* — borrowed from the Redmond public library a few blocks away. So the man whose diagnosis runs through this whole chapter, I met for real while I was lying on the floor he was describing, by way of a public library that handed a broken stranger six pounds of dangerous books for free. The commons producing, as it does, something well in excess of what it was funded to produce.

What I can tell you with certainty, from the inside, is that it was not laziness and it was not depression in the way the word gets used to mean a mood. It was a body that had finally refused. The armor had held for a decade by spending me, and when there was nothing left to spend, the holding stopped, all at once, and what was underneath the holding was a person who could not stand up. I had mistaken strength for rigidity. I still do, sometimes. It is a hard habit to break. The strength was never the holding — it was the thing the holding had been quietly eating the whole time.
