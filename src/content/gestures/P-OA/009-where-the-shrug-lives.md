That same childhood gave me something else, underneath the wound the training answered. The dojo dealt with the part of me that needed to not be prey. But there was an older layer the fighting never reached — not what was done to me by other kids, but what the place itself had already taught my body to expect. The book has a name for it: the shrug, the posture of diminished expectation, the way a body learns the ceiling of the possible before the child it belongs to has even thought to ask the question. I described it from the outside in an earlier chapter, as a thing you can see on a street in Richmond. I did not describe it from the inside, which is the only place I actually know it from. Kokoro is what you build on top of the shrug. The shrug is what is underneath, and it is harder to reach, because the spirit can be trained directly and the shrug cannot — the shrug was installed before there was a self to consent to it.
