Palmares was one form. The continent it remembered held many.

The Igbo, in what is now southeastern Nigeria, governed themselves for centuries without a centralized state — village councils, age-grade societies, women's market authorities, dispute resolution distributed by design. The Akan polities of West Africa held land matrilineally, the wealth attaching to the lineage rather than the individual. The Kingdom of Kongo administered an extended polity through a layered council system that predated Portuguese contact in 1483 and outlived the early disruption by generations. The Mbuti of the Ituri forest lived by gift exchange and consensus. No chiefs. No enclosure.

These were not preparations for Europe's arrival. They were what the continent was when it arrived — working systems with rules for use, transfer, and dispute. Ostrom would recognize them: bounded community, self-developed rules, monitoring, conflict resolution. The shapes vary. The architecture of a working commons is the same.
