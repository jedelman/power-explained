So, before anything else, do one thing. Notice your jaw. Most likely it is doing a small constant work it never told you about — a low clench you did not order and will not, on your own, call off. Now your shoulders: notice where they actually are, not where you would put them if asked, but where they have been sitting all day, probably nearer your ears than you would like. Now the breath. Notice that you can take it deeper than you were taking it a second ago — which means you were holding some of it back, which means there is a bottom of the breath you have not been visiting. None of that was a decision. You did not choose the clench or the held breath this morning; they were already running when you woke, the way they were running yesterday, the way they have likely been running for years.

Your body knows more than you do. It has been keeping records you never asked it to keep and never got to read. It has filed away every room that was not safe, every love that came with conditions, every time you were made small and learned not to make a sound about it — and it filed these not as memories but as instructions, as a held jaw, a raised shoulder, a breath that does not go all the way down. You have been carrying the instructions and calling them *you*.

Earlier in this book we did a thing with names — we said that the first move against a power is to name it, because a thing with a name is a thing you can finally get your hands on. This is the same move, turned inward. The held thing in your jaw has a name. Once it has a name you can stop mistaking it for your character and start treating it as what it is: a strategy your body adopted, under conditions, to survive something. And a strategy can be examined. A strategy can, eventually, be set down. But only once you stop calling it yourself. So let me give you the name, and the man who paid for giving it to us.
