Jane Jacobs documented the same logic operating at scale in the North End of Boston, an Italian immigrant district that banks had redlined entirely. With no access to conventional lending, the community ran a completely informal credit system that financed building improvements for an entire generation — neighbor to neighbor, tracked in memory and relationship rather than in any ledger a bank would recognize. That district became the most expensive real estate per square foot in Boston — and then, predictably, the banks arrived. The credit commons had produced what conventional finance declared unfinanceable. Enclosure claimed the value it had refused to help create.
