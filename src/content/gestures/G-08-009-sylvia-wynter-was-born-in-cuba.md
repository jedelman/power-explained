Sylvia Wynter spent years trying to write the Caribbean into literary existence — not as backdrop for European stories but as the center of its own history, its own ways of being human. That practice — telling a story about what it means to be human from a position the existing category of the human had excluded — is where the theoretical argument came from. You cannot write Caribbean history seriously without noticing that the people in it have been systematically classified as less than fully human. You cannot notice that without asking who made the classification, when, and for what purpose. When you follow that question far enough, you arrive at the claim: the category of the *human itself* was constructed to produce the outside.

Medieval Christian Europe understood what human beings were through a theological framework — to be fully human was to be made in the image of God. The Renaissance shifted this. The figure that emerged at the center of European humanism was *Man* — a rational, self-determining agent, capable of mastering nature through reason. But *Man* was not a description of the human species. He was a portrait of a specific kind of person — educated, propertied, European, male — whose particular attributes were elevated into the *definition* of what it means to be fully human. Everyone else was measured against this standard and found deficient.

Wynter calls this *overrepresentation*: one civilization's self-portrait became the definition of the Human itself. Everyone else was measured against a mirror they didn't make.

There is a version of this argument running through the present moment. The large language model produces the statistical average of human expression — the most probable next word, synthesized from everything that has been written down. It is extraordinarily useful for many things. What it cannot produce is singularity: the specific, irreducible thing that a particular person or community knows that no averaging process can recover. The most important thing about any person is precisely what averaging cannot hold. Wynter's overrepresentation is not a medieval error that modernity corrected — it is a structural tendency that keeps finding new forms, because the systems we build keep reaching for a universal human to optimize against, and the universal human is always someone's self-portrait pretending to be a mirror.
