Marx called the original version *primitive accumulation* — the foundational violence by which land held in common was separated from the people whose lives were organized around it, converted into private property, and made available for the extraction of value. The frontier this requires never disappears — it just moves. Atlantic City was that frontier in 1957. The Narmada valley was that frontier in 1999. The Weelaunee forest is that frontier now. It was not ancient history when Marx wrote about it. The English enclosures that drove peasants off common lands and into factory towns were living memory. The colonial land seizures dispossessing Indigenous peoples across the Americas, Africa, and Asia were ongoing. Marx named it primitive not because it was simple but because it was first: the precondition for everything that followed. You cannot extract rent from people who own the land they live on. You cannot compel wage labor from people who can feed themselves from their commons. Enclosure is not an accident of history. It is a political choice, made repeatedly, maintained by force.
