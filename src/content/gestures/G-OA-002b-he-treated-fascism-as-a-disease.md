Here is what he had been saying. The armor he found in his patients' jaws and shoulders was not only a private wound — it was, he argued, the raw material of fascism. Follow the logic and it is hard to escape: a body that cannot feel its own aliveness will go looking for aliveness somewhere, and the cheapest place to find a counterfeit of it is the parade, the rally, the strongman, the permission to finally discharge the held thing as rage at someone weaker. The armor does not prevent the explosion. It stores it, and it aims it downward. This is the same thing the yogis meant by the oscillations of the mind that are never allowed to settle, the same thing this entire book has been calling enclosure — arriving now in the last place we looked for it, which is your own neck. Reich set himself against this as a *physician*. He had watched ordinary Germans turn toward a movement that would devour them, vote against their own lives, line up for a man who openly despised them, and he refused to call it stupidity or evil, because those are not diagnoses and he was a doctor. He asked the doctor's question: what is the condition that makes a population *want* this? His answer, published in 1933 as *The Mass Psychology of Fascism*, was that a body raised in suppression — trained from childhood to hold, to obey, to distrust its own aliveness — arrives at adulthood already shaped to receive a strongman. The armor was the illness. Fascism was what the armored body did when offered permission. And a disease, at least in principle, can be treated. Reich thought you could *un*-make a fascist by un-making the armor, one body at a time, and he spent years trying.

This is why he had no country. The Nazis banned the book and he fled Germany ahead of them. But here is the part the comfortable left reader needs to slow down for: the Communists threw him out too. The German Communist Party expelled him in 1933 — the same year, over the same book — because the diagnosis did not stop at the border of the Reich. An armored body is an armored body. A population trained to hold and obey will line up behind a strongman whether the strongman wears a swastika or a red star, and Reich was honest enough, or reckless enough, to say so out loud. By his own account the book was attacked and denounced as *counterrevolutionary* by the Communist Party leadership in the countries he fled to next, Denmark and Norway. He had built a diagnosis that worked on Stalinists, and a diagnosis that works on your own side is the one nobody on your own side will forgive. The alternative he proposed was not a better strongman. It was what he called a *workers' democracy* — the people who do the actual work making the actual decisions, which is the same un-owned, un-bossed thing this whole book has been circling, arriving in the unlikeliest mouth, the disgraced sex doctor who turned out to be right.
