Medieval Christian Europe understood what human beings were through a theological framework — to be fully human was to be made in the image of God. The Renaissance shifted this. The figure that emerged at the center of European humanism was *Man* — a rational, self-determining agent, capable of mastering nature through reason. But *Man* was not a description of the human species. He was a portrait of a specific kind of person — educated, propertied, European, male — whose particular attributes were elevated into the *definition* of what it means to be fully human. Everyone else was measured against this standard and found deficient.

Wynter calls this *overrepresentation*: one civilization's self-portrait became the definition of the Human itself. Everyone else was measured against a mirror they didn't make.

There is a version of this argument running through the present moment. The large language model produces the statistical average of human expression — the most probable next word, synthesized from everything that has been written down. It is extraordinarily useful for many things. What it cannot produce is singularity: the specific, irreducible thing that a particular person or community knows that no averaging process can recover. The most important thing about any person is precisely what averaging destroys. Wynter's overrepresentation is not a medieval error that modernity corrected — it is a structural tendency that keeps finding new forms, because the systems we build keep reaching for a universal human to optimize against, and the universal human is always someone's self-portrait pretending to be a mirror.
