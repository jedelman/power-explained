Her argument is structural: the prison is not a response to crime. It is an institution for managing the people capitalism has made surplus — the outside made permanent, administered, funded. Davis was making this argument decades before Mbembe named it necropolitics. The Black radical tradition had already arrived where the European theoretical tradition was still heading. Mbembe was reading Davis, not only Foucault. In 1971 there were 200,000 people incarcerated in the United States [FLAG: ~200,000 is approximately correct for state and federal prison population (BJS data shows ~196,000 for 1970-72), but the total correctional population including local jails was closer to 360,000. Clarify whether "incarcerated" means state/federal prisons only or all confinement.]; by 2008 there were 2.3 million — the highest rate in the world, produced by traceable decisions: mandatory minimum laws applied with documented racial disparity, the defunding of public mental health care that moved people from hospitals to jails, the destruction of public housing that produced homelessness and then criminalized the homeless. Each decision followed the same logic — instead of addressing the material conditions producing surplus people, enclose them. The prison is cheaper than housing, cheaper than mental health care, cheaper than economic opportunity, if you don't count the people inside it as costs.
