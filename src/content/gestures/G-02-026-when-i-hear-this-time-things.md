"When I hear, 'This time things will be different,' I think it's malarkey," said one longtime resident, whose grandmother had been displaced from a different Norfolk neighborhood decades earlier. "They're saying it's for us, but really they're just sprinkling us in there to make us feel welcome."

She knows the structural relationship. She has learned it through her grandmother's experience and through her own. She cannot stop the demolition. But she can refuse to provide the legitimacy it needs by pretending the promises are credible.

That refusal — the naming, the seeing clearly, the refusal to call the loss progress — is not nothing.

It is where the work begins.
