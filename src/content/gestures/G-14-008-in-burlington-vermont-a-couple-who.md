In Burlington, Vermont, a couple who had been renting for seven years bought a Champlain Housing Trust home in the early 2000s — the trust Bernie Sanders had helped seed in 1984, introduced in Chapter 4. They couldn't have bought at market rates in the neighborhood they loved; the trust made it possible. A decade later, when they needed to move, they sold back at a formula price: their investment returned, a share of appreciation, but not the full speculative gain the open market would have offered. The next family paid below-market. They chose this deliberately.

Brenda Torpy, who led the Champlain Housing Trust for two decades and spent years buying land in Burlington's gentrifying neighborhoods and taking it permanently off the market, described the trust's purpose plainly: not to make the market work better, but to refuse the market's premise that land is a commodity. The trust's resale covenant removes land from speculation permanently — structurally, through a legal structure that persists independent of whoever is in office, built into every deed.
