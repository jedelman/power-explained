The standard political economy of enclosure focuses on land. The English enclosure acts, the Highland Clearances, the colonial seizure of common resources — all of these are real and important. What Silvia Federici's _Caliban and the Witch_ (2004) showed is that the same transition produced a parallel and equally fundamental enclosure that has received far less attention: the enclosure of women's bodies and reproductive labor.

This is not a metaphorical extension of the commons concept. It's the literal argument that the transformation from feudal to capitalist Europe required, alongside land enclosure, the subjugation of women as producers of the labor force — the enclosure of bodies and the reproductive work of maintaining and producing workers, carried out without compensation, under threat of violence enforced by law and by the witch trials that Federici analyzes as instruments of that enforcement.

## The argument in full

Before capitalism, women's labor — in the household, in the commons, in care work — was not considered separate from or lesser than other labor. It was part of the general productive activity of the community. The subsistence economy of pre-enclosure Europe included women's reproductive work as economic work, not as a private obligation outside the economy.

Capitalist accumulation required a specific reorganization: it needed a workforce that could be reproduced without that reproduction appearing on the balance sheet as a cost. The solution — achieved through the transition period of the sixteenth through eighteenth centuries, enforced through law, religion, and violence — was to define reproductive labor as women's private obligation rather than economic production. The work of producing and maintaining workers became invisible as work, while remaining absolutely essential to the system that depended on it.

[The outside capital needs](the-outside-capital-needs.html) captures this precisely: every system of extraction depends on a productive substrate it claims not to be extracting from. Women's reproductive labor is the most fundamental of those substrates — the original outside that makes the inside possible.

> The domestic sphere was never outside the economy. It was the economy's **unacknowledged foundation.**

## Where this shows up today

The claim
            Women's lower wages reflect their choices and qualifications.
          

The structure
            The wage gap is largest in occupations and sectors with the most care content — childcare workers, home health aides, nurses, teachers. This is not coincidence. The devaluation of care work is structural: care has been defined as women's natural duty, which means it can be paid less than work that has been defined as productive. The individual qualifications of the workers are not what determines the wage floor of care work; the ideological status of care as non-economic labor does.
          

The claim
            Childcare is a personal choice that families must fund privately.
          

The structure
            Childcare is the reproduction of the labor force — the work of producing and maintaining the workers that the entire economy depends on. Treating it as a private family cost rather than a social cost that employers and the state should bear is exactly the ideological move Federici describes: defining reproductive labor as outside the economy so that its costs don't appear as production costs. Universal childcare is not a welfare benefit. It's the social recognition of a cost that has been externalized onto women and families.
          

The claim
            Feminism is a separate movement from economic justice organizing.
          

The structure
            The enclosure of reproductive labor is economic. It produced differential wages, differential political power, differential vulnerability to poverty and domestic violence, and differential access to the public commons. Feminism that doesn't address the structural devaluation of care work — that only pursues representation in existing enclosing institutions — doesn't address the enclosure. And economic justice organizing that doesn't account for reproductive labor is analyzing an economy with half of its production missing.
          

## The commons response

If reproductive labor is an enclosed commons, then the demand is its declosure: social recognition and social support for care work as the productive foundation of everything else. This is the argument behind campaigns for universal childcare, paid family leave, and the valuation of domestic and care work in national accounts. It's also the argument behind [mutual aid networks](mutual-aid-isnt-charity.html) that treat care as a collective responsibility rather than a private obligation — not just in emergencies but as the ongoing organizational form of communities that refuse the enclosure.

The feminist commons tradition — from the Wages for Housework campaign of the 1970s to contemporary care work organizing — has always understood this structurally. The goal isn't to get women into the workforce on men's terms, which is just integrating women into an enclosing system. The goal is to recognize and structurally support the reproductive commons that the enclosing system depends on and refuses to acknowledge.

Federici herself connects this directly to the ecological commons: the same logic that enclosed women's bodies and land in the transition to capitalism is operating in contemporary accumulation. [Carbon markets and nature-based solutions](env-carbon-markets-are-enclosures.html) are performing the same ideological move on the natural world — defining its productive capacity as outside the economy until capital figures out how to enclose it as an asset class.
