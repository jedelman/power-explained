In the fall of 2011, something happened that the dominant political theory of the time had no adequate language for.

People gathered in Zuccotti Park in lower Manhattan — and then in parks and squares in eight hundred cities across the world — and did something simple and strange: they tried to govern themselves in public, without leaders, without a party, without a platform of demands, in real time and in the open air.

They were, by any serious accounting, a mess. The decision-making was slow. The general assemblies were exhausting. The people's mic — the human microphone, where the crowd repeated each sentence of a speaker so the words could carry without amplification — was beautiful and maddening in equal measure. There were no elected representatives, no majority votes, no formal authority. Every decision had to be made by consensus, and consensus was hard.

And yet something happened. A commons appeared. Kitchens, libraries, medical stations, legal support, childcare — all organized by people who had not met each other a week earlier, all running without institutional backing, all produced by the collective intelligence of a group that was making itself up as it went. The Situationists had tried to rupture the spectacle through theory. Stonewall had ruptured it through bodies in the street. Occupy ruptured it by building, in public, a functioning alternative — however imperfect, however briefly.

A herd of centaurs occupied Wall Street. Each one singular. The herd a multitude. The intelligence distributed through the field the way intelligence runs through mycorrhizal threads — not located anywhere, available everywhere, produced by the relationship rather than by any of its parts.

What they discovered, and what they couldn't hold, is what this chapter is about.

The herd needed governance. Not leadership — the herd was right to refuse the leader, right to insist that the multitude could not be represented without being betrayed. But governance: the pattern language that allows a composition of singularities to make decisions over time without dissolving into either tyranny or paralysis. Occupy had the field. It did not yet have the form.

It was not destroyed from within. It was demolished from outside, by coordinated municipal action across cities, by the institutional capacity of the system it was refusing. But the question it raised is still live: what would it have taken?

---

I need to tell you about burning out.

Not the Occupy burnout — my own. Because this chapter is supposed to answer the question *how*, and the honest answer has to include how it goes wrong, from the inside, when you are the person trying to hold the room.

At the restaurant, we started every service with a meditation circle. The circle worked. Over time, the practice moved from me into the institution — the staff carried it, told regulars about it with pride, held it even when I wasn't there. That is the governance principle in its most compressed form: the practice that survives is the one that no longer needs you.

But I was also, during those years, running Armed Forces Brewing — a cooperative governed by a board and a membership, with formal procedures, with votes. We had built something real: a community of people who cared about what we were making and why. And then a conflict arrived — the kind that tests whether a governance structure can hold relationships across a genuine disagreement.

The board abstained on a majority vote of the membership. Within their rights. No rule violated. Just fear, made decisive by the structure of the institution. I felt my heart leap at the members and sink at the board simultaneously. I resigned two months later. The restaurant was running on fumes. So was I.

This is what the machine looks like from inside, when you are the person trying to hold the room and the room is going badly and you can see what needs to happen and the structure won't let you make it happen. Not corruption. Not malice. The wrong form applied to the right desire.

Burnout is not the failure of desire. It is desire meeting a structure too small for it.

---

Gilles Deleuze and Félix Guattari argued that desire is not lack — not the endless reaching toward something absent, not the Freudian wound that can never fully heal. Desire is *production*. It connects, flows, builds. It reaches toward other desiring machines — other people, other communities, other fields of potential — and produces reality through the connection. Desire doesn't want what it doesn't have. It makes what it can.

The commons, governed well, is the institutional form of desiring production at community scale. It is a structure that allows the desire of each singular member to connect, flow, and produce something beyond what any individual could generate alone. The speaking circle produces a quality of listening that none of its members could produce in isolation. The annual roof repair produces a quality of relationship. The governance meeting — when it works — produces a decision that carries the weight of everyone who made it, which means it will be honored in ways that imposed decisions never are.

When the board abstained, the desiring production stopped. The flow met a wall. The desire of the membership — to govern, to decide, to carry the institution forward — found no route through the structure. That is what burnout is. Not the depletion of desire. The blockage of it.

What Occupy had, in its best moments, was unblocked desiring production: the flow of collective intelligence through a community that had temporarily dissolved the structures that usually block it. What it lacked was a form that could hold that flow over time, through conflict, across the moments when the flow slows and the structure must carry the weight.

You cannot build that form through desire alone. You need the accumulated wisdom of communities that have been doing it for centuries.

---

Elinor Ostrom spent thirty years sitting in rooms that worked — and rooms that didn't — asking what the difference was.

Not theorizing from a distance. Sitting in on water board meetings in Los Angeles, interviewing lobster fishermen in Maine, spending time in the communities whose governance she was documenting. When the model said communities couldn't solve the collective action problem without privatization or state control, and the community in front of her had been solving it for four hundred years, she trusted the community.

What she found were patterns. Not rules — patterns. The same recurring forms that, when present, tended to produce governance that worked, and whose absence tended to produce governance that failed. Christopher Alexander found the same thing in built environments: 253 patterns encoding centuries of accumulated wisdom about what makes spaces feel alive. Ostrom found the equivalent for the governance of shared resources.

Her eight patterns — she called them design principles, but they are pattern language — describe what the room looks like when it works:

The boundaries are clear. Everyone knows who is in the commons and what they can take from it. Not as a bureaucratic exercise — as a lived understanding that everyone in the room shares. When the Törbel villagers met in 1483 to write their charter, they were doing this: drawing a boundary that made the commons real rather than abstract.

The rules fit the place. What works for the Valencia water tribunal does not work for the Maine lobster gangs. The governance that holds is the governance that emerged from the specific conditions of this resource, this community, this history. Imported forms fail because they do not fit. This is why Occupy could not simply adopt Roberts' Rules of Order — the form didn't fit the field.

The people whose behavior the rules govern can change the rules. This is the principle most often violated by well-meaning institutions: they build governance structures that the founders can revise but the members cannot. The practice calcifies. The institution loses contact with its own conditions. The living form becomes a dead one.

Someone is watching. Not surveilling — attending. The resource and the people who use it are being observed by people accountable to the community. This is the tracker's gait applied to the commons: following what is actually there, not what the model predicts.

First violations get small penalties. Repeated violations get larger ones. The graduated sanction is not primarily about deterrence. It is about preserving the relationship that makes future governance possible. The $50 penalty for a missed maintenance shift rather than $500 is not leniency. It is the recognition that you will need this person next month, and they will need you.

Conflicts get resolved fast and cheap, before they compound. The Valencia tribunal meets every Thursday. The dispute goes in; the resolution comes out before lunch. The conflict that has been running for six months and hasn't been named yet — the one that was really the first agenda item at seven-fifteen — is the sign that this principle has failed.

The external authorities leave the community to govern itself. This is the principle most often violated from outside. The Spanish anarchist collectives in 1937 were not failing from within. They were deliberately demolished. The indigenous community that has governed its territory for generations loses standing when the state grants a mining concession. External power doesn't compete with commons governance — it destroys the conditions that make commons governance possible.

And for commons that span communities: governance nested at appropriate scales. The watershed governed at the watershed level. The neighborhood governed at the neighborhood level. The herd of herds.

These are not the design principles of a utopia. They are the minimum conditions for a room that works. The pattern language of the commons — encoded not from theory but from centuries of communities that governed themselves and survived.

---

But Ostrom's patterns describe the form. They do not describe what must fill it.

Bill Plotkin would say: initiated people. People who have made the descent, discovered what they carry, and brought it back. The governance meeting is where the gifts either find their function or collide without resolution. The collective-choice arrangement works when the people using it understand themselves as carrying something irreplaceable to the table — not a position, not an interest, a gift. The conflict resolution works when the people in conflict can still recognize each other's singularity through the disagreement.

The uninitiated community cannot govern itself sustainably. Not because its members are bad. Because it has not yet called forth what it needs. The meeting goes three hours and resolves nothing because nobody in the room has been told — by anything larger than their own ego — what they are there to offer.

This is what Hardt and Negri mean by the multitude as the new Prince. Machiavelli's Prince was a sovereign — one person whose will organized the political body. Gramsci's modern Prince was the revolutionary party — the collective actor that performs the sovereign function. Hardt and Negri's postmodern Prince is the multitude itself: the composition of singularities that governs without a sovereign, that produces the commons through the connection of irreducible differences rather than their subordination to a unity.

The herd of centaurs. Each one a singularity. Each one having made some descent and brought something back. Each one in relation to the others through the field — the mycorrhizal network, the speaking circle, the pattern language of the commons. The governance structure not the cage that holds the herd but the form that allows the herd's collective intelligence to produce decisions.

This is what Occupy was reaching for and could not yet name. Not the absence of governance — the presence of a governance form adequate to a multitude. The people's mic was not chaos. It was an attempt to build a form that could carry the multitude's intelligence without a sovereign to filter it. It was imperfect and it was real and it pointed somewhere.

The herd is already forming. In the Zapatista caracoles, in the Cooperation Jackson assemblies, in the Crown Heights mutual aid networks, in the data cooperatives being built before the AI enclosure completes, in the communities of practice that meet every week to govern things they care about. Each of them imperfect. Each of them pointed somewhere.

*Preguntando caminamos.* Asking, we walk.

---

Martín Prechtel has a name for what it's all for.

The Flowering Earth.

Not the sustainable earth — sustainability is a managerial concept, the earth as a resource to be kept from depletion. Not the just earth — justice is a corrective concept, the earth as a system to be balanced. The Flowering Earth: the earth as a living being in the process of producing beauty, complexity, singularity. Flowering.

The human community's deepest function, in this understanding, is to feed the flowering. Not to manage it. To feed it — with grief and with praise, with the labor of the roof repair and the speaking circle, with the governance meeting that went until midnight and resolved something real, with the annual return to the threshold where the community reconstitutes itself through the singular journeys of its members.

The commons is not a mechanism for allocating resources efficiently. It is the institutional form of love — the force that makes things more real, that nourishes what is already becoming, that holds the field open so the flowering can keep happening. Ostrom's pattern language is love made structural: the accumulated wisdom of communities that understood, without having the philosophical vocabulary for it, that the commons they were governing was alive, and that what they were governing it for was the continuation of that life.

This is the answer to the question this chapter was asked to answer.

*How?*

With each other.

The herd of centaurs — each one carrying what the underworld gave them, each one bringing it back to a community that was waiting, each one in relation to the others through the field that nourishes without flattening, each one contributing to the governance that holds the field open, each one feeding the flowering that is the point of all of it.

The governance meeting at seven-fifteen. The eleven people. The unread agenda. The conflict that's been running for six months. This is the room where it happens. Not in the theory. Not in the vision. In the room, with the people, doing the unglamorous load-bearing work of deciding together and repairing the relationship when the decision breaks it and deciding together again.

You wonderful weird birds.

Every owl that shows up. Every person who stayed in the room past midnight. Every singularity that brought something back from the underworld and found, finally, a community at the threshold.

This is where the commons is built. This is where the earth flowers.

Show up. Stay. Learn each other's gifts. Grieve what passes. Praise what is.

Build accordingly.
