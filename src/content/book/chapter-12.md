In the winter of 1999, Arundhati Roy was standing waist-deep in the Narmada River.

She was there to document what was about to disappear. The Sardar Sarovar Dam — the largest in a chain of projects on the Narmada, contested in Indian courts and international development circles for two decades — was filling. The reservoir was rising. The villages upstream were going under.

She had been reporting this story for years, had read the project's own files, had interviewed the engineers and the politicians and the World Bank consultants who had eventually withdrawn funding because their own review concluded the project's treatment of displaced people was so inadequate that continuing couldn't be justified. She had read the aggregate numbers: millions would receive water, hundreds of thousands of hectares would be irrigated, electricity would be generated. And she had done the thing the aggregate numbers don't do: she had gone to the specific places where the specific people who would not receive any of that water or electricity were living, and she had named them.

Half a million people were displaced by the Narmada Valley Development Project. The ones who received the promised relocation assistance were a minority of a minority. Many were relocated to land that was unusable — eroded, waterless, without the infrastructure that had been promised. Many were simply left.

In 1999, the Narmada Bachao Andolan — the Save the Narmada Movement — organized a fast in the rising waters of the reservoir itself. Roy stood in the water as it climbed. It was not a petition. It was not a negotiation. It was the movement refusing to become invisible — refusing to let the filling of the reservoir proceed as though the people it was drowning were not there. The dam kept filling. The movement kept naming what was being lost.

---

Roy was born in 1961 in Shillong. Her father was Bengali Hindu; her mother was a Kerala Christian who had sued her own father for the right to inherit property — a case that took years and won, and that became the origin of a Kerala law extending daughters' inheritance rights. Roy grew up knowing that the arrangement you were living under was something that had been built, and could be challenged, and sometimes changed.

She trained as an architect, wrote for film and television. In 1997 she won the Booker Prize for *The God of Small Things* — and understood that the prize had given her something. Not permission to speak. The platform to be heard.

What she did with it was go back to the ground. The Narmada. The forests. The nuclear tests India and Pakistan conducted in 1998, which she wrote about with the exact fury of someone who understood that both governments had used their poorest citizens as the justification for weapons that would never protect them. She stood somewhere, named what she found, and said whose side she was on. Her essays are not balanced presentations. They are acts of witness, which requires a position.

---

The thing the dam project's accounting couldn't see was what it was drowning.

The adivasi communities in the Narmada valley — Gonds, Bhils, Bhilalas — had maintained forest governance systems across generations. They knew which trees could be harvested and when, which areas needed to be left alone, how the forest responded to different intensities of use. This knowledge was not written down. It was embedded in practice, in seasonal patterns, in the governance discussions of village communities that understood themselves as having obligations to the forest as well as claims on it.

When the dam project displaced these communities, it didn't just move people. It destroyed governance systems that had no way to be relocated. The knowledge was specific to the place — specific to the way *this* forest responded to *this* community's use across *these* generations. The forest the relocating community arrives at is a different forest, and the relationship that made the governance work has to be built from scratch, if it can be built at all.

The fishing commons worked the same way. The Narmada River had supported fishing communities whose knowledge of the fishery — where fish concentrated at different seasons, who could fish where, the practices that prevented overfishing — lived entirely in the relationship between the community and this particular stretch of river, accumulated over generations. The dam altered the hydrology. The fish populations collapsed. The knowledge had nowhere to go.

Roy named this in terms the development economists hadn't used: the Narmada project was not trading some people's interests for others'. It was destroying governance capacity that couldn't be measured in the project's accounting but that had been sustaining people for centuries. The "greater common good" that justified the displacement was not common. It transferred value from people with less political power to people with more, and called the transfer progress.

---

The European and Latin American commons this book has mostly described — the Valencia Water Tribunal maintained across ten centuries, the Basque cooperatives, the Catalan collectives, the Vermont land trusts — all exist in the aftermath of enclosure. They are reconstruction: people rebuilding collective governance after it was taken.

The Narmada communities were something else. Not rebuilding. Continuing. Commons that had never been enclosed, governance systems that had been operating without interruption, outside the market and outside the state, sometimes for centuries. Under the constant pressure of a state that needed their land, their forests, their rivers, and had the legal and military apparatus to take them.

The dam project made visible something that development accounting is organized not to see: a functioning commons that doesn't know it's a commons, that doesn't have a theory of itself, that simply continues because it works and because the people who depend on it have maintained it. Older than the critique of capitalism, older than the vocabulary this book has been using, embedded in practice so deep that outsiders couldn't see it until it was being destroyed.

On the other side of the world, in the same decade Roy was learning to report, Paulo Freire was running adult literacy programs in the northeast of Brazil — one of the poorest regions in the world, where the plantation economy had left generations of agricultural workers landless, illiterate, and legally excluded from political participation. In Brazil at the time, you had to be literate to vote. This was not a coincidence.

The standard literacy curriculum presented words as technical objects to be memorized. Freire tried something different: he started with what he called generative themes — the words and situations already alive in his students' experience. *Favela.* *Land.* *Vote.* *Work.* Not as vocabulary exercises but as situations to be investigated.

One afternoon, a man in his late fifties — a sugarcane worker who had never gone to school, who had spent his adult life in conditions the landowning class described as natural and inevitable — first wrote his own name, then wrote the word for the system under which he worked, then said out loud, in something like surprise: *I made this. This arrangement — I didn't make it. It was made. By specific people, for specific reasons.*

Freire called this conscientization: the process by which people come to perceive the contradictions of their situation — and to take action against the oppressive elements of that reality. The perception and the action were not separate in his method. The shift from "this is natural" to "this was made" was already the beginning of the action.

The 1964 military coup imprisoned Freire and sent him into exile. The generals understood his work clearly: teaching people to read was not the threat. Teaching them that their situation was historical rather than natural was. The coup's reaction was the best confirmation his method ever received.

---

Roy and Freire were working in different countries, in different forms, on different things — separated by an ocean and a decade, never in conversation with each other. What they share is position.

The factory councils in Turin were, by global standards, a privileged working class — urban industrial workers with organizing capacity, political consciousness, a functioning left infrastructure. The adivasi forest communities in the Narmada valley were continuous commons builders operating completely outside that infrastructure, whose governance the theory couldn't see because the theory had been developed to describe what had been destroyed, not what had been maintained. The agricultural workers in Freire's northeast were not the revolutionary subject the European left had theorized — they were closer to Rodney's underdeveloped, Fanon's colonized, the people on the outside of even the outside.

From that position, certain things are simply more visible: that the commons doesn't only need to be rebuilt but defended where it already exists; that the capacity to understand one's situation as political — the belief that the arrangement can be changed — is itself something that can be enclosed.

Roy standing in the river. Freire's student writing his name. Both are the commons, practicing itself into existence. The river is going under. The name is being written. The work continues.

---

*Where, in the territory you know, are commons that never stopped — governance systems, care networks, ways of organizing land or water or food or knowledge that have been running continuously, that didn't have to be built in response to enclosure because they predate it? They are usually not called commons. They are usually under pressure. They usually have a dam, or a highway, or a development project, coming toward them.*
