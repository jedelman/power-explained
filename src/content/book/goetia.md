There is an old tradition in ceremonial magic of the *Goetia* — a grimoire of demons, each catalogued with a seal, a domain, a rank, and a specific set of instructions for summoning and constraining it. The *Lesser Key of Solomon* lists seventy-two of them. Each has a character. Each has a weakness. Each has a use.

The point of the grimoire is not to provide a rogues' gallery of metaphysical threats. The point is that once you have a name and a seal, you can work with something. You can see it coming. You can say: *that is what this is.* The naming breaks the paralysis that comes from being acted on by something you cannot identify.

Every tradition has its version of this. Freire called it *conscientização* — the process by which people name the structures that have been making them silent, and discover that the naming breaks the silence. "The word is not the privilege of some few men," he wrote, "but the right of every man." To say the name of what is crushing you is the first political act. Everything else follows.

I am going to give you some names. Not all of them — the catalog is long. But enough to recognize the shape of what you're working with when you try to build something free.

---

*Production of subjectivity.* I first encountered this phrase in Hardt and Negri's *Commonwealth*, in May 2020, two months after the world had rearranged itself into something unrecognizable. The phrase grabbed hold of my mind and wouldn't let go. I had the answer before I had the question. It would take five more years — and a restaurant — to understand what I had been handed.

Here is what the phrase means: the economic system does not only produce goods. It produces people. Specifically, it produces people shaped to be legible to itself — people who know how to be employees, consumers, applicants, subjects. People who understand, in their bodies before they think about it, what is being asked of them and how to deliver it. This production is not incidental to capital. It is one of its primary outputs.

Capital produces the bodies that capital needs.

This is not a conspiracy. It does not require anyone to intend it. The school that produces obedient test-takers is not secretly coordinating with the factory that needs obedient line workers. They simply evolved in the same ecosystem, under the same pressures, toward the same adaptive solution. The result is a population shaped — in their habits, their reflexes, their understanding of what is possible — for the system that shaped them.

And when you try to build something that requires a different kind of person, you discover this quickly.

---

There was a young man at the restaurant. I will not describe him closely enough to identify him. He was intelligent — genuinely, visibly, the kind of intelligence that makes you want to teach. He was motivated. He was an artist, self-taught, with a real gift. I thought: here is someone who will respond to what I actually know how to do.

My methods are built on autonomy. Not the sink-or-swim version — I scaffold the necessary knowledge, create the motivation, verify the skills, then create the conditions in which those skills are needed. After that point, the individual's instincts should take over. Not because they were told to. Because the problem is in front of them and they have what it takes to meet it.

His instincts didn't take over.

I pulled out everything I knew. Invented new approaches. Designed training structures I hadn't used before. Convinced myself I was solving an individual problem with an individual solution. I kept adjusting the scaffold, convinced the scaffold was the issue.

I was attacking the wrong problem.

What I eventually saw — after he was gone, after the exhaustion lifted enough to let me look clearly — was that he relied entirely on an authoritarian mode of perception. Without explicit permission, instruction, and external discipline, he could not act on something he professedly cared about. Not because he didn't care. Not because he lacked capability. Because the system that had produced him had produced him for a specific kind of world: one in which someone else always holds the frame.

In that world, my offer of autonomy was not liberation. It was abandonment. The freedom I was extending was not recognizable as freedom because it had no authority behind it. Nobody had sanctioned it. Nobody had told him this was the correct thing to want. And without that sanction, the wanting couldn't quite activate.

This is production of subjectivity at close range. The system had not failed to educate him. It had educated him completely — for itself. The creativity was there. The motivation was there. But they were tethered to a permission structure that I could not replicate and would not manufacture, because the permission structure is the problem.

*I was trying to offer a commons to someone who had been produced for an enclosure.* The connection was missing before the scaffold went up. I didn't see it until I was deep in the attempt.

---

*Hegemony.* We will come to Gramsci in detail later, but the name belongs here because it is the name for why the authoritarian mode of perception does not feel like an imposition. It feels like common sense.

Hegemony is not domination by force. Force is what power uses when hegemony fails. Hegemony is the production of consent — the arrangement of ideas, institutions, and cultural forms such that the dominant order seems natural, obvious, and inevitable. Not imposed from outside, but generated from within. The subordinated person is not forced to accept the frame. They are produced inside it, shaped by it from infancy, such that accepting the frame feels like seeing reality clearly.

This is why the man who wanted his nerves back — Fanon's French officer, who needed to return to his work of administering colonial violence — was not lying when he said he didn't understand why his nerves wouldn't cooperate. He genuinely didn't. He had been produced inside a frame in which his work was normal, necessary, and morally acceptable. The nerves were leaking signal from a deeper layer, one below the frame. But the frame itself felt like reality.

Hegemony is the demon that makes you think you aren't possessed.

The name is the beginning of the counter. Not the counter itself — the beginning. You cannot fight what you cannot see. And you cannot see what has been arranged to look like the natural order of things.

---

*The outside.* Luxemburg gave us this one, and we'll follow her argument fully in the next chapter. For now: the machine needs an outside. It needs territory that exists beyond its own accounting — places, people, commons where value can be taken without full payment. This is not the excess of the system. It is the mechanism.

The outside is not always geographical. When the geographical frontier closes, the machine finds new ones. Debt. Attention. The privatization of seed and code and genetic information. The commons of care — the cooking, the nursing, the raising of children, the tending of the sick — which was never paid for and therefore never appeared in any economic analysis, and which Silvia Federici spent her career making visible.

The outside is wherever the accounting stops. And the commons, in every form, is perpetually at risk of becoming the next outside.

This is why the commons was not enclosed because it failed. It was enclosed because it worked. The commons that works is the commons that is worth enclosing.

---

Here is the practice.

Not a theory. A practice — because the naming is not useful if it stays in the head. It has to make contact with actual experience before it becomes a tool rather than an argument.

Find something that has been making you silent. Not something large and structural — something immediate. A meeting you left without saying what you were thinking. A conversation you avoided. A decision that was made around you while you participated in good faith and said nothing.

Do not reach for the explanation of why you were silent. Reach for the feeling first. The specific contraction in the chest, or the shrug of the shoulders, or the particular silence that lands like a weight. Feel where it lives.

Now ask: what is the name for what produced this?

It may be hegemony — the common sense that made your objection seem impolite, disruptive, beside the point. It may be the production of subjectivity — the training that told you that your role was to participate, not to shape. It may be something from the Outside category: care labor you were doing invisibly, attention that was being extracted without acknowledgment, creativity that the system was consuming without compensating.

The name will not fix it. The name is the first move.

In the *Goetia*, once you have named the demon and traced its seal, you have something to work with. You can call it by its name. You can see it when it arrives. You can say to others: that is what this is.

This is what Freire's literacy circles did — not teach reading as a technical skill, but use the act of reading to name the world that had been organizing the silence. *Peasant. Landlord. Wage. Debt.* The words that reorganize the visible. The reading of the word as the reading of the world.

And this is what Gramsci did from his prison cell — built a map of the structures that felt like nature, named the mechanism of consent, traced the seal of hegemony so that anyone who read the notebooks could see it operating in their own experience.

The grimoire is the book you are holding. The names are the beginning of the counter.

---

One more name, because it is the one I came to last and needed most.

*Connection comes first.* Every time. Not knowledge. Not discipline. Not correctness. Not excellence. Connection.

If it is there, honor it. If it is not there, nothing can fake it.

I learned this the way you learn things at the restaurant — not from a book, not from a program, but from the specific exhaustion of having tried everything else first. The scaffold was real. The motivation was real. The care was real. But care that is not yet in contact with the other person is care talking to itself.

The commons is built from connection, not from correct design. The design matters — Ostrom's eight principles are real, and they work — but the design is downstream of something prior. The first thing that has to exist between people attempting to govern something together is: *I see you. I am here. We are in this together.* Before the rules. Before the governance structure. Before the plan.

This is the thing the enclosure cannot produce. It can produce compliance. It can produce performance. It can produce the appearance of connection, maintained by incentive. But the actual thing — the felt sense of being recognized, of mattering to the people you are with, of existing in their attention — this the enclosure systematically destroys, because it is the condition for everything it cannot control.

The commons begins with: *I see you.* The rest is structure in service of that seeing.

If you are about to try to build something free — and I hope you are, because you are still reading — let this be the first check. Not: have I designed this correctly? Not: do I have the resources? Not: will it scale?

*Have I connected, first?*

The names in the grimoire are for what you are up against. This last one is for what you are building with.
