There are doves watching me type this.

Two of them, on the railing outside the window, doing what doves do — that particular blend of stillness and alertness, the head-tilt that means something registered, the return to stillness that means it wasn't a threat. They don't know what I'm writing. They don't know about the enclosure, the commons, the seventeen chapters of argument that came before this page. They are just here, in the morning, in the way that living things are here in the morning.

This is the thing the book kept circling without quite landing on: the commons is not only what was taken. It is what is still here.

---

Last winter, during a storm, I watched a tiny kinglet taking shelter in the tall grass of a managed meadow two blocks from our house. A kinglet is one of the smallest birds in North America — barely four inches, burning calories against the cold at a rate that requires almost constant feeding. This one was tucked into the grass, out of the wind, exactly where a meadow should be. The parks department management plan says to cut that meadow. They haven't cut it in two years. I walk past it every morning expecting the mowers. Every morning it's still there is a small triumph.

The night herons will return to the willow oaks in a few weeks. Every spring, up from Central America, to raise their young in the same trees. We find the nests. We tell the herons we love them and wish them good luck. They don't hear us. We do it anyway.

---

Six weeks after closing the restaurant and two burnouts in, I don't have faith in humanity. Not the sweeping kind — the kind that says *people will rise to meet the moment* and *the arc bends toward justice*. I've spent enough time in the rooms where things don't bend to say that plainly.

What I have instead: faith in the wisdom traditions. Faith in the indigenous elders who are quietly saving their corners of the world, who never needed the theory of the commons because they were living it. Faith in the awareness practices that let you see the trickle when the catastrophe has trained you to expect the flood.

Enclosure arrives like a catastrophe — sudden, total, a betrayal. Recovery doesn't come back the same way. It comes as a trickle. The farmers market is back and not bad. There's a music gig next week. Bluesky and the open-source ecosystem, doing things the old platforms can't. The Airbnb guests who sit at the kitchen table in the morning and don't want to leave. The meadow not cut. The muskrats last year, first time I'd seen them here. The black-and-white warbler that just arrived in the park down the street.

You need the awareness practices to see the trickle. Most people don't have them. Part of what this book is trying to be is an awareness practice — a way of training the attention so that the trickle becomes visible, so that the seed sprouting in someone else's consciousness registers as something worth watering.

---

Everyone in this book did what they did for the birds and the food and the music and the laughter and the friendships. For the particular quality of a summer evening when the work is done and the people you love are around you and the thing you built is humming. For the chance to look back at the people who did this before you — Rosa Luxemburg naming birds through prison bars, Paulo Freire's sugarcane worker writing his name, the employee telling the regulars about the meditation circle — and feel that you showed them respect. Not by winning. By continuing.

The night herons don't know about the road. The doves don't know about the argument, or the enclosure, or the seventeen chapters. The deer that walked through the camp in Washington didn't know that twenty five-year-olds were about to become, briefly, something older than themselves. Life is what we have in common — the herons and the doves and the deer and the people and the elders and the sugarcane worker writing his name and the employee at the bar, telling the story proudly, not needing me to be at the center of it.

All of it is the commons. All of it is what the enclosure is trying to take. What the enclosure cannot take is the life the commons is made of, because the life was never property.

---

The awareness practices are political acts. The walk around the neighborhood is political. Waking up for the cardinal at 5am. Finding the heron nests and telling them you love them — this is political. Not because it changes the zoning board or restores the common land or forgives the debt — but because it trains the attention toward what is real and living and shared, and attention trained that way is the root of everything else.

The commons is not a project to be completed. It is a practice to be continued. The practice that survives is the one that has moved from the leader into the institution, into the culture, into the daily life of the people who carry it forward without knowing they are carrying it forward — the employee telling the story at the bar, the child who stalked the deer, the friend who finds the heron nests every spring without having been asked to.

I'm helping out where I can. For now, that's enough.

The doves are still on the railing. The herons will be back in a few weeks. The meadow hasn't been cut.

*Go find the nests. Tell them you love them. They won't hear you. Do it anyway.*
