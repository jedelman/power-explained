title: "Interlude: Counter-Hegemony Without the Word"

In Washington State, in places I won't name precisely, there are educators who have decided that the most important thing they can do is put children in direct contact with old-growth forest.

I was one of them, for a summer. I want to tell you about the last day.

Not teach them about old-growth forest. Not explain the carbon cycle or the spotted owl or the board-feet of timber at stake. Take them there. Let the forest do what it does to a child who stands inside it for long enough — the scale of it, the age of it, the specific quality of silence that isn't silence but is the sound of something much older and more patient than anything the child has encountered before.

The group I was working with that week were five-year-olds, younger than I usually taught. The curriculum I'd been trained in — nature awareness, observation, tracking, bird language, stealth — needed to be rebuilt almost from scratch for children who had been alive for sixty months. My expectations were different. Five days in a state park, not at a designated camp, fully in the field. I'd built something workable. I didn't know if it was working.

On the last day, a deer walked through camp.

I didn't orchestrate this. Nobody did. The deer appeared at the edge of the clearing and the children saw it — and something happened that I had been trying to create conditions for all week without being able to force. They dropped. All of them, simultaneously, into a stalking gait — weight low, feet placed softly, the instinctive crouch of a predator or a prey animal moving carefully. And they went silent.

Do you know how hard it is to get a group of five-year-olds to be silent? For twenty minutes, we stalked that deer through the edge of the forest. The children moved like they'd been doing it all their lives, because in some evolutionary sense they had. Nobody told them to. Nobody needed to. The deer showed up and the children became, briefly, something older than themselves.

That's the whole thing. That's what the work is actually for.

The educators who do this work understand something that most policy advocates don't: that you can't protect what people can't imagine losing, and you can't imagine losing what you've never met. The intellectual argument for old-growth preservation is overwhelming. It hasn't been sufficient. The emotional and bodily argument — standing in the presence of a tree that was already old when your country was founded, breathing the air that the canopy produces — is different in kind, not degree.

I won't give you their names. They work on margins, and margins are vulnerable. But anyone who reads this book and decides they want to find the people doing this work will find them. That's how it works. The networks exist. You follow the thread.

Let me try to describe what the guide is actually doing, because it looks like almost nothing from the outside.

When I'm in the field with a group, I'm paying attention to what everyone around me is paying attention to — and also to what they're not attending to. I'm listening to what people are saying and what they're not saying, watching how their bodies agree or disagree with their words. I'm listening to the birds, who are commenting on our collective energy and also on things I can't perceive yet. I'm feeling the moisture in the air, noticing how ambient sound and light shift, tracking how all of this affects the people around me. All of this at the same time. This is not exceptional — it's what human beings are built to do. We've just been trained out of it.

What I'm watching for is the moment when someone notices something they haven't noticed before and gets curious. It's like a seed sprouting. My job is to water it — but carefully. Too much attention, too much validation or explanation, and the seed drowns: the person returns to self-consciousness instead of continuing to open into environmental awareness. Too little, and the moment passes. The timing is everything, and it can't be forced.

I'm not telling anyone anything when I'm guiding well. I'm maneuvering the conditions — the direction we're facing, the pace we're walking, what I say and what I leave unsaid — so that a connection can happen that I couldn't produce directly. When it works, I'm not sure I did anything. That's the sign it worked.

What struck me, coming back to this practice after years of political organizing, is that it is entirely Gramscian in its logic — and I would be surprised if a single person involved has read Gramsci. They didn't arrive at this approach through theory. They arrived at it through generations of observation: of what changes people, of what doesn't, of what produces the kind of deep identification with a place that makes someone willing to defend it over years and decades rather than attending a rally and going home.

Gramsci, writing in prison in the 1930s, argued that lasting change requires changing what people feel is natural — that hegemony works through the body and through culture as much as through law and economics, and that counter-hegemony has to work the same way. You have to produce new common sense, new ways of being in the world that feel normal rather than radical, before the political change becomes possible or sustainable.

The educators in Washington State are producing new common sense. They are changing what a generation of children understands as normal — that you can stand in the presence of something centuries old, that this is what a forest is, that this is what you are part of. They are doing it without manifestos, without theory, without the word hegemony ever appearing in their lesson plans.

The work is patient. It measures its effects in decades. It will not produce a policy victory next year. It may produce, twenty years from now, a generation of voters, organizers, parents, and officials who understand in their bodies what is at stake when an old-growth forest is auctioned for timber. That understanding, once embodied, is very hard to undo.

This is what building the commons looks like at its slowest and most durable. Not a rally, not a lawsuit, not an election. A child standing in a forest, learning for the first time what old means.

In Barcelona, in the El Born neighborhood, I heard music coming from a side street before I could see where it was coming from. Someone had set up a rig — instruments, a small sound system, a gathering of people who had arranged to be there. This wasn't spontaneous. It was community. The distinction matters. Spontaneity is alone. Community is a practice that has been built by many people over time until it feels, from the outside, like it just happened.

I didn't join. I didn't need to. What I needed was to be near it and understand why it exists. What I felt, standing there, was relief. The ease of knowing that life goes on even if yours doesn't. That the work of building the commons is larger than any one person's contribution, that the deer will walk through camp again next summer, that somewhere right now someone is sitting in the presence of an old tree and feeling, for the first time, what old means. The music doesn't need you to be there. The herons don't know about the road. Life continues. That knowledge — not as consolation but as fact — is what makes it possible to go back to the meeting, the crossing, the vote that passed and was ignored, and try again.
