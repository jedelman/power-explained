On August 25, 1991, a twenty-one-year-old Finnish computer science student named Linus Torvalds posted a message to a Usenet newsgroup:

> "I'm doing a (free) operating system (just a hobby, won't be anything big and professional like gnu) for 386(486) AT clones."

He thought he was making a toy. He did not expect what followed: thousands of programmers from around the world, none hired or paid, contributing improvements, bug fixes, and new features to something that belonged to all of them and none of them. Eleven months later, Linux 1.0 was released under the GNU General Public License — a license requiring anyone who modified and distributed the software to release their modifications under the same terms. Free to use, free to modify, free to distribute, but not free to enclose.

Today, Linux runs more than 90 percent of the world's cloud infrastructure, most of the internet's servers, every Android phone, and all five hundred of the world's five hundred fastest supercomputers. The software he gave away in 1991 became the infrastructure of the digital economy. No one who contributed gave up anything by making it open. They built something no private company would have built, because no private company could have assembled the collective intelligence the commons produced.

This is what knowledge enclosure looks like in reverse.

---

In 1936, Alan Turing published a paper to solve a problem in mathematical logic and in doing so invented the conceptual foundation of every computer ever built.

The paper's central device — the Turing machine, an abstract mechanism that reads and writes symbols on a tape according to a finite set of rules — was powerful enough to compute anything that can be computed at all. Then Turing described a Universal Turing Machine: a single device that could simulate any other Turing machine if given that machine's rules as input. One machine that could do everything, because the program was just data, read from the tape like anything else.

This is the concept your computer instantiates: not a device built to do one thing, but a device that can do anything you can describe. Because the program is data, it can be copied, transmitted, shared, improved, and given away at zero marginal cost. Computation is a commons by nature — one copy is as good as any other, sharing it doesn't deplete it, the capacity of the machine is not reduced by the programs already written for it. The artificial scarcity that proprietary software creates runs against the nature of what it encloses.

Turing never patented any of this. He published in a mathematical journal. The concept became available to everyone who read it, built on by everyone who worked in the field, instantiated in the machines that now run the world.

In January 1952, Turing reported a burglary at his house. During the police investigation, he acknowledged a sexual relationship with a nineteen-year-old man. Both were charged under the same law used to prosecute Oscar Wilde. Turing pleaded guilty. He was given a choice: prison or chemical castration — a course of synthetic estrogen injections. He chose the injections. His body changed. He described the treatment to friends with sardonic detachment.

In June 1954, he was found dead at forty-one, a half-eaten apple on the nightstand. The inquest ruled suicide.

The state that prosecuted him had, nine years earlier, depended on his work at Bletchley Park to break German military codes — work that historians believe shortened the war by at least two years. The state knew this. It prosecuted him anyway. Not despite knowing — indifferently to it. The knowledge was useful. The person was a problem. The two were separable, in the state's accounting.

Federici's executed healers. Roy's displaced adivasi communities. Turing's prosecution. The pattern: the system takes what it needs from people it won't recognize.

Berners-Lee also chose the commons — with every financial incentive pointing the other way.

On March 12, 1989, he submitted a proposal to his supervisors at CERN. The proposal was for a system to manage information across a distributed network: documents linked by hyperlinks, accessible through a common protocol, identified by unique addresses. His supervisor wrote on the cover sheet: *Vague but exciting.*

By 1991 Berners-Lee had built the first web server, the first web browser, and the first web page. In April 1993, CERN released the web protocols into the public domain. No license fee. No patent. No requirement of any kind. This was a decision. Berners-Lee and CERN deliberately chose to forgo the intellectual property that could have made them enormously wealthy. His reasoning: a proprietary web — one where you paid to use the protocols, where a single entity controlled what could be built on them — would not be the web. Its value was universal access. Enclose it and you destroy it.

The open web became the infrastructure for the most valuable economy ever built. He was right — even if the proof of it didn't benefit him. He has never commercialized what he invented, while the people who built platforms on top of his gift became the wealthiest individuals in human history.

The enclosure didn't happen to the protocols. It happened on top of them. HTTP, HTML, and URLs stayed open — anyone can still build a web server without asking permission from anyone. What got enclosed was the application layer: the platforms that became the default interfaces through which most people accessed the web. Google enclosed search. Facebook enclosed social connection. Amazon enclosed commerce. Not by owning the web — by becoming the chokepoints through which most people used it, capturing the social layer the open protocol left unspecified.

The commons was used to build the enclosure of the commons.

Berners-Lee has spent twenty years saying this is a betrayal of what the web was designed to be. His project Solid attempts to rebuild the social layer on open protocols — putting data ownership back with the people who produced it. Whether it succeeds matters. That its inventor felt it necessary is a measure of how thoroughly the enclosure succeeded.

In 1997, a Texas company called RiceTec was granted a patent on Basmati rice — a grain that farming communities in India and Pakistan had developed over centuries, the result of generations of selection, breeding, and agricultural knowledge accumulated in practice. The patent included varieties that were, in any meaningful sense, the product of those communities' accumulated work. Indian farmers and the Indian government challenged the patent; some claims were invalidated in 2002. But the attempt illustrated the logic exactly: take the knowledge the community built over generations, encode it in the language of intellectual property law, sell it back to the people who developed it under terms that make their own traditional practices conditional on a license.

The English language wasn't invented. It grew. No single person produced it; no institution owns it; it emerged from communities and evolved through use. The accumulated body of human writing — every book, argument, essay, and conversation ever recorded — is the collective product of billions of people across centuries, for no single purpose.

The AI systems now being trained on that corpus are a new kind of enclosure. The commons goes in — the accumulated patterns of human thought and expression — and a proprietary artifact, controlled by whoever built the model, comes out. The people whose expression constituted the training data have no governance rights over what was made from it.

The farmer whose ancestral seed variety was patented got the structural argument in one transaction. The writer whose work trained a language model gets it in a legal dispute. The structure is identical.

---

The early 1990s are the governing reference point for AI.

Torvalds posted his message in 1991. Stallman had written the GPL in 1989. Berners-Lee gave away the web in 1993. The governance decisions made in those years — about licensing, about what could and couldn't be enclosed — determined what was possible when Android arrived in 2007, when cloud computing consolidated in 2015, today when Linux runs the majority of the world's servers. The governance structure was set before the infrastructure was valuable enough to fight over.

The same window is open now. One institution making this argument concrete: Hugging Face, a platform that publishes open model weights — AI systems whose underlying parameters are publicly released, available for anyone to run, modify, or build on. It is Linux for language models: imperfect, partial, not sufficient on its own, but a real demonstration that the commons model works in this domain. The data cooperatives being built now — communities pooling their data under collective governance, with rights over the systems built from it — are the CLT model applied to the training corpus: the governance structure built before the enclosure completes.

Once the infrastructure consolidates, the path dependencies lock in. The same question the Törbel community answered in 1483, the same question the adivasi communities in the Narmada valley were forced to answer when the dam came: who governs the resources communities depend on, and in whose interest?

---

*What knowledge in your domain is being enclosed right now — through intellectual property, through platform capture, through proprietary systems trained on commons-produced data? Open-source tools, Hugging Face, and data cooperatives are operating alternatives. The decisions being made in the next five years will shape what is possible for decades.*
