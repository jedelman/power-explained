Nobody invented the word *love*.

Not the sound of it, not the weight it carries, not the way it moves differently in the mouth of someone who has just lost someone and someone who has just found someone. It grew. Communities shaped it across centuries, handed it forward without intending to, changed it without asking permission, received it already changed. The grammar you are reading this in — the way a sentence builds toward its end, the rhythm that makes a thought feel complete — none of it was designed. It emerged from the collective practice of millions of people communicating across time, owned by no institution, available to anyone born into its reach.

You didn't make the language you think in. It made you, partly. And you are making it, slightly, right now, in ways you won't be able to trace.

The philosophers Michael Hardt and Antonio Negri argue that love, in its deepest form, is not sentiment but force — the power that recognizes your singularity and nourishes it, that holds the space open for you to become more fully what you are. Not merger. Not the demand that you complete an arrangement. Love that says: *become*, and in becoming, enrich what we share. This is what the commons does at its best. This is what language does. This is, if you have ever felt it, what the living world does.

Go into an old-growth forest. Scientists now know that the trees are connected underground through networks of fungal threads — sharing nutrients, sending signals, the older trees feeding the younger ones through roots they have never touched. The forest is not a collection of individual trees competing for light. It is something closer to a single living process that has learned to individuate into trees. Each tree draws from the network without depleting it. Each tree contributes to the network by growing. And the network does not ask the tree to be a different kind of tree. It nourishes what the tree is already becoming.

This is what the philosopher Gilbert Simondon called the *pre-individual field* — the charged, generative excess from which individuals emerge without exhausting it. You are not a finished object. You are an ongoing event, always in relation to a field that exceeds you, drawing from it, contributing to it, never separable from it. The individual isn't prior to the collective. The collective is the condition of the individual.

Love, in Hardt and Negri's sense, is the name for a relationship to the field that nourishes rather than encloses. The Earth offers it unconditionally. You belong to the living world by virtue of being alive. No performance required. This is why time in nature hits so differently than time in the city — not because nature is prettier, but because it is the one environment that does not ask you to complete its arrangement. You are sufficient as you are. Become.

But there is another kind of love. One that places conditions.

---

Think of a room you didn't fit in.

Not a room you hated — a room that was fine, that functioned, that other people moved through without friction. A room where the unspoken rules were legible to everyone but you. A social script that everyone else seemed to have been given in advance, and you were expected to perform without ever having received it.

That feeling — the slight wrongness, the sense that belonging was available but only on terms you couldn't quite meet — was not a personal failing. The French philosopher Jean Baudrillard, writing in 1968, called it the *system of objects*: a closed environment in which everything references everything else, and in which a person is expected to become another legible element in the arrangement. The furniture, the clothes, the conversational moves — together they produce an atmosphere that means *successful* or *normal* or *one of us*, and the person inside it is supposed to mean the same thing.

Your grandmother's weird lamp ruptures the system. The cracked mug you refuse to throw away ruptures the system. Not because they are ugly — they might be beautiful — but because they carry a *specificity* the system cannot absorb. They point somewhere the system doesn't control.

You, when you don't fit, are doing the same thing.

This is conditional love at the level of objects and spaces. It offers belonging — but only in exchange for legibility. Become readable to us, complete our arrangement, and you may stay. The emptiness you feel in those rooms is structural. There is always another adjustment that might finally make the feeling stop, another way to smooth yourself into acceptability. The resolution is always deferred. The promise is the product.

Guy Debord, writing the same year as Baudrillard, called this management of belonging *the spectacle* — not images, but social relations mediated by images. You are not experiencing your social world directly. You are consuming its representation, a version optimized for legibility and reproduction, and the representation gradually becomes more present than what it depicts. The love the spectacle offers is always at one remove. You are connected to the image of connection. You belong to the image of belonging.

In May 1968, students in Paris took this argument into the streets. They had been reading Debord. They spray-painted his ideas on walls: *Under the cobblestones, the beach. Be realistic, demand the impossible. It is forbidden to forbid.* They were trying to rupture the spectacle from the inside — to insist, in public, that the living process was more real than its representation, that another world was not only possible but already present underneath the managed surface. They occupied the Sorbonne. The factories joined them. For three weeks, France stopped.

And then it didn't.

De Gaulle survived. The workers went back. The uprising was absorbed — and then, more completely, it was *spectacularized*. The slogans became posters. The barricades became documentaries. The revolt became content. Baudrillard watched this happen and drew his conclusion: the spectacle does not need to suppress the rupture. It only needs to represent it.

One year later, in June 1969, the people at the Stonewall Inn in New York tried something different.

They didn't have a theory. They had bodies — bodies the state had spent decades trying to organize into legible, manageable categories, bodies the law had decided were criminal, bodies whose desires were supposed to be hidden or cured or contained. When the police came, as they came regularly, the people inside did not perform the expected submission. They refused. In their bodies. In the street. Marsha P. Johnson — Black, transgender, a person who had been refused legibility by almost every system she encountered — threw a shot glass, or a bottle, or possibly a brick (the history is contested, as histories of refusal tend to be), and something that had been building for years broke open.

The Situationists had tried to rupture the spectacle through theory and performance. Stonewall ruptured it through the body — through the insistence that no external system had the final word on what these bodies were or what they were allowed to become.

The philosopher Gilles Deleuze, writing in the aftermath of 1968, had a name for what both uprisings were reaching toward. He wrote about the *body without organs* — not the anatomical body organized by its assigned functions, but the body understood as a field of potential, not yet resolved into the form the system has prepared for it. Before the categories. Before the assigned role. The body as pure becoming, capable of connections and transformations that the organized, legible body forecloses.

Enclosure, in Deleuze's terms, is the imposition of an organism on a body without organs. It is the demand that the field of potential resolve into a fixed arrangement. It is the system saying: *this is what you are. Stop becoming something else.*

The refusal is the insistence on remaining in process.

In the early 1930s, a young Englishman named Alan Turing was becoming someone the world had not yet seen.

He was a runner — seriously, almost-Olympic-level — who trained alone for hours on roads outside Cambridge, working through mathematical problems while his feet found the rhythm. He was funny in the way that brilliant people who have spent too much time in their own heads are funny: precisely, unexpectedly, slightly wrong-shaped for the social occasion. When his closest friend Christopher Morcom died suddenly in 1930, Turing wrote letters to Christopher's mother for years afterward, because the grief had nowhere else to go and he did not know how to leave it.

He was also, quietly and without apology, gay — in a country where this was not only socially unacceptable but criminal. He had learned, by necessity, to live in the gaps between the world as it was organized and the world as he actually experienced it. To see the categories that other people inhabited as choices rather than necessities. To find the spaces between things.

In 1936, he published a paper that most people outside mathematics have never read, solving a technical problem in mathematical logic that almost no one outside the field knew existed.

To solve it, he imagined a machine. Not a physical machine — a thought experiment, a formal device on paper. It worked by reading symbols from a long tape, following a set of rules, and writing new symbols back. Simple enough that a patient person could simulate it by hand. But powerful enough — he proved — to perform any calculation that could be performed at all.

Then he described something stranger: a *universal* version of this machine. One that could become any other version of itself, if you described that other version to it first. Not a machine built to do one thing, but a machine that could do anything describable — because the description was just more symbols, read from the tape like everything else. The instructions and the machine were made of the same stuff.

He had dissolved a category everyone else had taken for granted: the distinction between the device and what the device does. Between the fixed thing and the process running through it. Between, you might say, the organism and the body without organs.

This insight is the foundation of every device you use that runs software. Not the hardware — the concept underneath it. The understanding that the machine does not have to be organized around a fixed function. That the rigidity is a choice. That the choice can be refused.

He published the paper in a mathematical journal. He took out no patents. He did not attempt to control what followed. He handed the concept to anyone who read it, because that is what you do when you understand that an idea is, by nature, a commons — the more people who hold it, the more powerful it becomes, and sharing it costs you nothing.

The device that runs on this concept — the thing we eventually called a computer — did not exist yet.

---

In January 1952, Turing reported a burglary at his house. During the police investigation, he mentioned a relationship with a nineteen-year-old man. Both were charged under the same law used to prosecute Oscar Wilde half a century earlier. Turing pleaded guilty. He was given a choice: prison, or a course of hormone injections designed to chemically suppress his sexuality. He chose the injections. His body changed. He described the treatment to friends with sardonic detachment.

In June 1954, he was found dead at forty-one, a half-eaten apple on the nightstand. The inquest ruled suicide.

The state that prosecuted him had, nine years earlier, depended on his work at Bletchley Park — where he had led the team that broke the German military's coded communications. Historians believe this work shortened the war by at least two years and saved millions of lives. The state knew this. It prosecuted him anyway. Not despite knowing — *indifferently* to it. The knowledge was useful. The person was a problem.

But the person was not separable from the knowledge.

The insight that dissolved the boundary between the device and its function — that came from a mind shaped by living outside the categories everyone else took for granted. The lateral leap required someone who had learned, by necessity, to see rigidity as choice. The universal machine required Alan Turing: not a normalized version, not a chemically managed approximation of acceptable personhood, but the actual ongoing event of a human being who had been finding the gaps in organized systems his entire life.

What the state attempted was precisely what Deleuze described: the imposition of an organism on a body without organs. The demand that the field of potential resolve into a fixed, legible, manageable arrangement. The insistence that *this is what you are — stop becoming something else.*

He refused. Not with a manifesto. With sardonic detachment. Wrong-shaped for the occasion, declining to perform the expected suffering — the dry wit of someone who has seen through the system entirely and will not grant it the dignity of his despair.

That is queer joy. Not happiness — *joy*. The present-tense insistence that the living process of a person is more real than any category the system can impose on it. The mycorrhizal network of queer community — the chosen families, the underground networks of recognition and care, the houses of the ballroom scene in Harlem — operated on exactly this principle. You belong here by virtue of what you are becoming. No completion required.

Federici's executed healers. Roy's displaced adivasi communities. Turing's prosecution. The pattern: the system takes what it needs from people it will not recognize. The knowledge is useful. The person — the ongoing, individuating, gap-finding, category-refusing source of that knowledge — is a problem to be managed.

---

In 1989, Richard Stallman wrote a license.

A license is not a glamorous object. It is a legal document specifying the terms under which software can be used and distributed. But Stallman's GNU General Public License did something that had not been done before: it used the law of ownership to *prevent* enclosure. Under normal copyright, the creator controls distribution. Stallman's insight was to use that control to require that anyone who modified and passed on the software must do so on the same terms — free to use, free to modify, free to share, but not free to enclose.

This is détournement: hijacking the system's own instruments against the system's purposes. The legal apparatus of property, repurposed as the legal apparatus of the commons. Not naïve. Not trusting. Using the logic of the master's house to build something the master cannot enter.

In that same year — 1989, the year the Berlin Wall fell, the year China's students occupied Tiananmen Square — Tim Berners-Lee, a British scientist working in Geneva, submitted a proposal to his supervisors at the research organization CERN. He wanted to build a system for sharing information across a network: documents that could link to other documents, accessible through a common set of rules, from anywhere. His supervisor wrote on the cover: *Vague but exciting.*

Two years later, in 1991, a Finnish student named Linus Torvalds posted a note to an internet discussion group:

> "I'm doing a (free) operating system (just a hobby, won't be anything big and professional like gnu) for 386(486) AT clones."

He thought he was making a toy. Programmers around the world — none paid, none coordinating through any central authority — started contributing. The operating system shipped under the GPL. By 1993, Berners-Lee had built the first working version of the World Wide Web, and the organization he worked for released the underlying rules into the public domain. No license. No fee. No owner.

All of this happened in four years. Stallman, Berners-Lee, Torvalds — none of them coordinating, working in different countries on different problems, all arriving independently at the same understanding: that the nature of what they had made required giving it away. That a proprietary web would not be the web. That universal access was not a feature — it was the thing itself. Enclose it and you destroy it.

They were right. What they built became the infrastructure of the digital world. None of them became billionaires. The people who built platforms on top of their gift did.

The story does not end here. The story never ends. That is why the title of this chapter is *Build and Defend the Commons* — because the commons is not a noun, not a thing you finish constructing and then possess. It is a practice. The Törbel community didn't write their governance charter in 1483 and consider the matter settled. They governed, repeatedly, in perpetuity, by choice. The gift is never secured once and for all. The field must be held.

The spectacle does not need to own the protocol. It only needs to own the interface.

---

The enclosure didn't happen to the underlying rules of the web. Anyone can still build a site, run a server, send an email, without asking permission from anyone. What got enclosed was the *layer on top* — the platforms that became the default surfaces through which most people experienced the open web. Google enclosed search. Facebook enclosed the experience of staying connected with people you know. Amazon enclosed the experience of buying things. Not by owning the web — by becoming so convenient, so frictionless, so much the path of least resistance, that the open alternatives became invisible.

This is Debord's spectacle completing itself. Berners-Lee gave away the infrastructure of connection. The platforms enclosed the *representation* of connection — made their version more vivid, more present, more legible than any alternative — and the representation became dominant. You are not using Facebook to connect with people. You are consuming a simulation of connection, optimized not for the quality of the connection but for the duration of your attention.

The commons was used to build the enclosure of the commons.

Berners-Lee has spent two decades saying this is a betrayal of what the web was designed to be. His grief is the measure of how thoroughly the enclosure succeeded. And yet the web still exists. The protocols are still open. The fight is not over, because the fight is never over. Every generation of the commons requires its own defense.

---

You have felt this.

The slight flatness after you close the app. The way a conversation that happened in person feels more real than the same words exchanged over a platform. The low hum of dissatisfaction underneath the scroll — Baudrillard's structural promise that is never redeemed, the belonging that is always one more adjustment away.

That is not a personal failing. That is the system of conditional love working exactly as designed. The representation is more legible than the living process. The living process is more real. The system has made it harder to reach.

This is how it starts. The commons doesn't disappear. It gets replaced by its image.

And then something stranger happens.
